Rule Thirty-Three: Keep the Cold Room Cold

The Jeongs’ walk-in is the size of a confession booth and about as warm, and at half eleven, with the front locked and the books nearly squared, I had no reason to be standing in it except that the cabbage delivery had come late and Halmeoni had asked, and nobody alive says no to Halmeoni.

I did not ask for help. It arrived anyway, peeling off the wall where he’d been pretending to read a delivery slip.

“I can move cabbage,” I said.

“You move cabbage like you’re auditing it.” He took the heavy end before I could argue, which is the whole strategy — do the thing before the no lands. “Sack of radish for your thoughts, Jae-wu. You’ve got your tax face on. The shelf hasn’t done anything to you.”

“The shelf is loaded wrong.”

“The shelf is fine.”

“The shelf is top-heavy, and somebody stacked the daikon above the napa, which is how you get—”

That was the sentence I was in the middle of being right about when the top crate let go.

It came off the high rail all at once, thirty kilos of radish remembering gravity, and I had time to file it — loss, breakage, my fault for being right too slowly — and no time to move. He had time. He always has time; it’s the one thing he’s never short on. He got an arm across me and turned us both, hard, so the crate clipped the shelf edge instead of my head and emptied itself across the floor in a clatter that, in that steel box, sounded like the building coming down.

And then it was very quiet.

We’d ended up in the gap between the racks, which is not a gap built for two. My back was to the cold steel. His arm was still braced past my shoulder where he’d thrown it, his other hand flat on the shelf beside my hip, and the half-inch of air that’s meant to live between two people who share a flat and nothing else had gone somewhere and not come back.

He was breathing like he’d run. I could see it — the cold turns every breath to smoke — his coming fast and short and ragged, fogging the space between us and vanishing. Up close, the rogue had walked clean off the job. The mouth that has an answer for the weather and the price of cabbage and the exact meaning of my face had nothing in it at all. His eyes were doing something I had no column for. And under the hand I hadn’t known I’d flattened on his chest — to push him off, that was the plan, it was always going to be the plan — his heart was going so hard and so wrong it frightened me, the way a figure frightens you when it’s triple what it should be and you cannot find the error.

One of us was going to have to keep her head. It was not going to be the man coming apart against the radish.

“You can let go of the shelf,” I said. Level. I don’t know where I found it.

“I know.” He didn’t.

“The crate’s down. Nothing else is falling.”

“I know.” A breath, smoke, gone. “Give me a second.”

And I gave it to him. That’s the part I can’t account for. I stood in the cold with my palm on a heartbeat I had no business reading and I gave the man his second, and not one instant of it did I spend leaving. The half-inch stayed open on nothing but the fact that somewhere in the last three years I’d decided I was a person who holds things — and I was holding it, by the back teeth—

“OH.” Halmeoni, in the doorway, a cabbage in each hand and the face of a woman who has walked in on the best thing all year. “Don’t mind me. Don’t mind me! The radish can wait. The radish can wait forever.” She was already reversing out, luminous with joy, the worst ally I have ever been assigned. “You two finish.”

We sprang apart so hard I cracked my elbow on the shelf. He said something to the ceiling that was technically Korean. Halmeoni was gone, humming, off to brief everyone she had ever met.

I crouched and started gathering radish. After a moment he crouched too, the far side of the spill, and we worked in a silence with its own weather.

“For the loss column,” he said eventually, holding up a daikon split clean down the middle. “Breakage, or—”

“Don’t.”

“—or an act of God.”

“It was the shelf. I told you it was the shelf.”

“You did.” He set the broken half in the crate, careful, like it was worth setting down gently. He wasn’t smiling. That was the thing I couldn’t file. He looked at me across a floor of wrecked radish in a freezing steel room, not smiling, still undone at the edges, and said, “You were right about the shelf,” as though being told you were right about a shelf were the most dangerous sentence in the language.

I put it on the list that night, awake, because the cold room had got into my chest somehow and would not come out.

Item twenty-two. Subject is faster than gravity. Subject is not faster than himself.
