# Chapter One

### Rule One: The Register Always Balances

Three years ago.

There were two things I trusted at twenty-one, and one of them was the till.

The till didn’t lie. You counted it in, you counted it out, and at close the number was the number — nothing extra, nothing missing, no part of it deciding overnight that it had never really been yours. I have loved very few things that reliable. People aren’t. Faces least of all, which is why I’d stopped keeping those years back — remembering a face costs more than a girl living that thin can run, and the only ones worth the overhead were still on the schedule.

So when I tell you I don’t remember his face, I want it on the record, given everything that happened after. I didn’t keep it. I kept exactly one thing off that man, and it wasn’t his face, and I didn’t even mean to keep that.

But I’m ahead of myself. It was raining.

The roof had been leaking a month, and that night the rain came to collect — drumming the awning, sheeting flat across a dead road, ticking through a brown stain in the ceiling tile into a mop bucket. One drop a second. I’d timed it. There’s not a lot to do on a graveyard shift but time the leak and balance things in your head, and I was good at both. Under it the cooler hummed, and the strip light over the register buzzed on the half-second, and the radio went round and round losing the same song.

A delivery driver set down cigarettes and a canned coffee.

“Filthy night for it.”

“It is.” The drawer rolled open.

“That your bucket doing the dripping, or the radio?”

“The bucket.”

“Somebody ought to fix that.”

“Somebody ought to.” I counted his change into his palm, coin on coin. “Four thousand.”

He pocketed it and didn’t leave, the way men don’t leave when the night’s long and the counter’s the only lit thing for a block. “Long shift?”

“Long enough.”

“What time you off?”

“When the morning girl comes.” I’d already found the door more interesting than his face. “Drive safe. It’s slick out.”

He took the hint, or the cold of it, and went, the bell knocking once behind him.

Forty-one shifts left. That was the other number I kept, the real one — what was owed, what was saved, the long thin distance between a wet floor in Samgeo and a desk in a room with a door that locked. I kept it where I keep everything that matters, which is nowhere a soul can reach it. You can’t take a thing off me if I never let you see I’m holding it. This is, I’m aware, not a personality. It’s a filing system.

The drip found a second line. I walked the bucket a hand’s width left to catch it — a slow drum gone to two notes now, off the beat — and there, taped to the till in a hand that had given up years ago, was a strip of card: OWNER — CALL FOR ANYTHING.

I had never once called for anything. I called for this.

Four rings. A man surfacing from the dead. “…Mm.”

“It’s the fill-in, Samgeo branch. Your ceiling’s coming through onto the floor by the window.”

“Put a bucket under it.”

“There’s a bucket under it. What’s also under it is a wet floor with two customers six feet off, and if one of them goes down, that’s an incident, and an incident wants a name, and the only name working tonight is mine.”

A long exhale down the line — a man deciding, in the dark, exactly how much trouble I was going to be.

“You always this much fun?”

“Only on wet nights.” The mop was already in my other hand. “I’ve logged that I told you. That’s the whole call. Goodnight.”

I hung up before he could hand it back to me a second time. For the record: he did nothing about the leak. I knew he’d do nothing. The point of the call was never the leak. The point was the sentence on the sheet that made the nothing his.

“Hey.” The nearer drunk waggled an empty at me. “Two more.”

“Coolers are closed.”

“Coolers are right there.”

“Closed at one. House rule, not mine.” I didn’t look over. “Water’s free. I’ll bring you water.”

“We don’t want water.”

“Then you’re all set.” Pleasant. Final. The door of it shut so smooth he couldn’t find the handle to argue with. He grumbled something to his friend, the friend laughed — a wet, low, going-nowhere laugh — and they folded back down over the cans they still had, which is how it was always going to end. I have closed a lot of doors on a lot of men. I’m very good at it. It’s the one part of the job I’d put on a résumé if I could find the box for it.

Then the bell went, and a man came in out of the rain.

The water had all of him — hair flat under a cap under a hood, the coat gone black and heavy, a thin stream off one cuff to the floor I’d just claimed credit for. He stood on the mat dripping a second, the rain still coming through the closing door behind him, then crossed to the case, took a can off the bottom shelf, and brought it over.

He set down coins. I counted the change into his palm without looking up, because that’s the job, and because the till is the till.

“Cold one.” Two words, rough, like they’d had to be dug up.

“Hot’s on the warmer.” A nod, no glance. “Same money till morning.”

He took the cold one anyway. Carried it to the window counter and sat — two stools off the drunks — and the stool took his weight with a wet sound, and the rain came off him onto the floor in a slick spreading toward the legs, which meant I’d be mopping that too, which meant I clocked him properly for the first time, the way I clock a till. Fast. Total. Filed.

Here’s what the file said, and I’m only telling you because of what it turned into later. The hair was silver. Not grey — silver, a pale ashy expensive silver, dark at the roots, salon-bright even drowned, the kind of colour that costs what I made in a week and a stylist’s afternoon on top. And it did not match the coat. The coat was good — that registered before anything else, good wool, cut close, the kind of coat that costs like the hair — but it had been through something: split clean along one shoulder seam, the lining showing, the way good wool doesn’t go on its own. Wool like that doesn’t tear with age. It tears when somebody’s hand is in it, or when it catches on the thing you’re getting over in a hurry. And over the collar of all of it sat a face that belonged on the other side of a magazine I’d never buy.

A normal person sees that and thinks: poor guy, rough night. I’m an accountant, or I was about to be one, and what an accountant sees is figures that don’t reconcile. Money on the hair, money on the coat, the coat torn off him in the last few hours, and the whole expensive ruin of it standing in my shop at one in the morning in the rain. That isn’t a hard week. A hard week wears in slow. This came off him fast and tonight. The numbers didn’t add up, and a thing that doesn’t add up isn’t sad to me, or pretty to me. It’s a discrepancy. I don’t trust discrepancies. I audit them.

I’d have liked to audit this one. I had a floor to mop instead.

“Hey. Hey — silver.” The nearer drunk had found him. “You a model?”

Nothing from the window.

“He’s a model. Look at him. Hey. You deaf?”

“Leave it,” the other one said, bored already. “He’s nobody.”

The man gave them nothing. Not a word, not a turn of the head, not the half-second it’d take to make it into anything — and after a moment they slid off him the same way they’d slid off me, beaten by a closed door they couldn’t find the edge of, and went back down over their cans. So that was one thing we had in common. I added it to the file. The file, at that point, had one item in it. The file would get worse.

I lifted the hot coffee off the warmer, carried it the length of the counter, set it by his hand, and took the cold can away.

He looked up.

His eyes were grey. Storm-grey, no floor under them, and for about half a second they just — stopped me, before I was anywhere near the thing underneath.

I knew that look. I’d worn it, this side of the counter, more nights than I’ll count for you: going under, quiet, too far out to bother waving. Not pity — pity comes from dry land and I’ve never once stood on any. Just one swimmer clocking another. It costs nothing to set a cup down on your way past. That’s the whole of what it was. I need you to believe that’s the whole of what it was, because I believed it, right up until it wasn’t.

“On the house,” I said.

He didn’t answer. Took the cup in both hands and held it and didn’t drink.

“Mind your feet.” I went for the mop.

The nearer drunk swivelled. “Aw — don’t quit on our account, sweetheart. Stay down there where we can see you.”

“She likes us.”

“You like us?”

The mop went back and forth. Water lifted off the tile in long clean pulls and I didn’t look up, didn’t slow, didn’t hand them the half-second that turns a catcall into a conversation. That part ran off me like the rain off the glass. Nothing to catch on, nowhere to land. I’d been deflecting men since before I could legally serve them; it’s muscle memory, like the till.

What didn’t run off was the other one. The man at the window wasn’t laughing. He was watching — not the way the drunks watched, but quiet, total, the way you’d watch something you’d decided was worth getting right — and my hands went careful on the handle without my permission. Every pass of the mop turned into a thing being done in front of somebody. Heat climbed the back of my neck and sat there and would not be mopped away, and I wrung the cloth out harder than the floor could possibly need.

It was the hour. The rain. Being watched while you wring a mop is its own specific horror and has nothing to do with the watcher; I’d have felt it from a security camera. That’s what I told myself. I told myself a number of things that night and the till is the only one that balanced.

I dropped the mop, stood the cone over the wet, and carried the bucket to the back.

There were coins on the counter when I came out. Set down careful, in a little stack, beside the cup.

“I can pay.” Low. “I can pay, I’ve got it—”

“I know.” I slid the coins back across the laminate with two fingers.

“You don’t have to—”

“It’s still on me.”

“I—”

“Floor’s wet till it dries.” And I went back behind the counter, which was the end of it, the way a closed drawer is the end of a sum.

He sat with the cup in both hands. The radio came round into a second verse and lost the same thing all over again. The cooler hummed. The drunks forgot me, the way they were always going to.

“Thank you,” he said. To the window. Not to me. It came out rough, like it had climbed something on the way up.

I didn’t answer. There was nothing the answer would’ve added to the room.

He left before the drunks did — out into the rain with the cup still full and steaming, the heat of it wasted on the night, which struck me as a waste of a perfectly good coffee and exactly the kind of thing a man with a four-figure haircut and a torn-off coat would do, the kind of man who breaks something and then can’t sit still in the warm. The bell chimed once. The rain closed over the doorway and took him. I went back to timing the leak.

By seven the rain was down to a drizzle and the morning girl came in smelling of the bus, shaking a cheap umbrella over the mat.

“Please tell me the coffee machine’s not dead again.”

“Machine’s fine. Floor’s wet by the window — cone on it, mind it. Owner knows. I called it in at twenty to three and logged it.”

She looked at the cone, the bucket, the brown bloom widening on the tile. “He’ll do nothing about that, you know.”

“Probably nothing. But it’s his nothing now, and it’s on the sheet that it’s his.” I was already pulling the vest off over my head.

“You’re a piece of work.”

“I’m a fill-in. Friday’s my last day. Saturday it’s your leak.” I hung the vest on its hook. It came out almost kind, which for me is a lot of overtime.

She laughed and tied her own on. “Quiet night, then?”

I thought about it for exactly as long as it took to square the vest on the hook. A wrecked stranger, a wasted coffee, a discrepancy I never got to audit, a heat on the back of my neck I’d already filed under the hour.

“Quiet,” I said. “Couple of drunks. The usual rain.”

Then I counted the till.

It came out to the won. Nothing extra, nothing missing — except it wasn’t, was it, because somewhere in the drawer was the price of a hot coffee I’d handed over for a cold one and waved off the difference, and the drawer didn’t know that, and the drawer balanced anyway, because I’d covered it out of my own tips without once stopping to decide to. I banded the cash. I signed the sheet. I wrote one line under the date in small square figures — leak over window counter, owner notified 02:40, cone placed — and put my initials beside it, because if a name’s going on a sheet I’d rather be the one who put it there.

I’d half-forgotten his face already. I’d trained that out of myself a long time ago and it’s a good system and I stand by it. But not the look. The going-under, the too-tired-to-wave. That one I hadn’t kept on purpose, and that one I couldn’t seem to file.

It was already mine.

Outside, the rain had nearly stopped. I locked the drawer, signed off, and stepped out under an awning that had finally quit dripping — and I did not think, not once, not for a second, to count the one thing I’d given away that night for free.

Which is, when I look back on it, the single most expensive accounting error of my life. I just had to wait three years for the bill.
