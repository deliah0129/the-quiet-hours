# Chapter Twenty-One *(rebuild — band scene, chaos-forward)*

### Rule Twenty-One: Never Tell Four Idiots a Secret After the Fact

The practice room over the chicken place hadn’t been cleaned since we outgrew it, and all four of them were already in it when Hyun shoved me through the door, which meant the yelling started before it shut.

“HE LIVES—”

“Sun, off him—”

“—I will NOT off him, he went dark for two months, I aged, look at my face, I have a LINE—”

“You have concealer for that.”

“Reo I will end you—”

“Both of you,” Tae said, not loud, and both of them stopped, because that’s the trick Tae has, and he put a water in my hand and scanned me head to foot like he was checking for exit wounds, and found none, and didn’t say anything, which from Tae is a whole speech.

Yejun didn’t get up. Yejun had three phones fanned in front of him and a laptop and the face of a man who’s been doing arithmetic since four a.m. that he hates. “Sit,” he said. “We have a problem and you’re going to make it worse by existing, so sit while I explain how.”

I didn’t sit. My phone was going in my hand — Hyun’s was going in his — and the group chat I was physically standing inside of was *also* going, all five of us in a room and the chat still detonating on the table like we weren’t, because that’s what twelve million people will do to a Tuesday.

“Okay,” Yejun said, “the clip—”

“Is it him?” Sun said. “It’s him. It’s obviously him, I’d know that voice in my—”

“We KNOW it’s him, Sun, he’s standing there—”

“I’m CONFIRMING, emotionally—”

“—nobody confirms anything,” Yejun said, “that’s the entire point, hold—” and his phone rang, and he declined it, and it rang again, and he turned it over, and Hyun’s rang at the same time, two rings stacking, and Hyun stepped into the corner with one finger in his ear going *no comment, no, who gave you this number,* while Reo read off the other phone in the flat voice of a man reading a weather warning he intends to ignore.

“Reposts are at four hundred thousand. Original’s gone — poster nuked it—”

“Wait, gone?” I said.

“Gone. Deleted. Whoever filmed it deleted the post and the account sometime this morning, no warning—”

“So nobody can ask them where—”

“Nobody can ask them anything,” Reo said, “which is the one piece of good news in this dumpster, so naturally everyone’s lost their minds about it. No source means no shortcut. So now they’re doing it the insane way.” He flipped the phone around. A still, arrows on it, a rooftop, water tanks, a convenience-store sign half in frame, blown to pixels. “They’re matching *water tanks,* Do-yun. They’re triangulating your address off the *plumbing.* There’s a woman live right now walking Mangwon holding her phone up against buildings like a metal detector. It’s slower than a source would be. It is not *slow.*”

“How long,” I said.

Four of them looked at each other, which is its own answer.

“Day,” said Yejun. “Maybe two. The window doesn’t move, and the window’s the only thing they need.” He finally looked up at me. “You can’t go back. A man going *in* that door is the one thing that turns a voice into a name. You’re done there. I’m sorry. The two months are over as of this morning, you just don’t get to feel it yet, we don’t have time—”

“Okay but the *bigger* problem,” Sun said, and the room turned, because Sun doesn’t get grave, and Sun had gone grave. “The roommate.”

And here’s where it went sideways, and I want it on the record that I tried to get a word in and the chaos would not let me.

“Right—” Yejun, nodding, already moving, “—the roommate, that’s actually fine, that’s the one clean thing, because Jae-wu knows, so we just route through Jae-wu, have him keep his head down, don’t open the door, don’t talk to—”

“Wait,” I said.

“—because a guy who’s been living with you for two months obviously clocked it weeks ago,” Reo said, “you can’t hide a *cup* in a flat that size, let alone a comeback—”

“Wait—”

“—and Reo’s family knows you two are tight,” Tae said, to me, reasonable, “so the assumption’s been baked in for ages, halmeoni’s mentioned your roommate, *Doha’s friend, the quiet one, does the books*—”

“The books?” I said.

“—point is Jae-wu’s read in,” Yejun steamrolled, “Jae-wu’s an asset, we brief Jae-wu and Jae-wu manages the flat from the inside while we—”

“*Jae-wu doesn’t know.*”

It came out louder than the room, and the room stopped.

Yejun blinked. “…Jae-wu lives with you.”

“Yes.”

“For two months.”

“Yes.”

“And hasn’t worked out that you’re—” he gestured at the phones, the chat, the country “—*this.*”

“No.”

“*How,*” said Reo, with real offense, like I’d insulted his intelligence personally.

“Because she—” and I heard it the half-second too late, the wrong pronoun out in the open in front of all four of them, and there is no taking a word back in this room, they are bloodhounds, they have known me since I was a boy with a cracking voice, and I watched it land on each of them in sequence like a row of lights going on.

Silence. The phones kept buzzing. Nobody picked them up.

“…She,” Sun said.

“She doesn’t own a television,” I said, which is true, and a complete non-answer, and the only thing in my mouth.

“Jae-wu,” said Tae, slowly, “is a woman.”

“Yun Jae-wu reads as a man’s name,” I said. “Hyun thought she was a man. I thought she was a man until she walked through the door. The lease says Jae-wu. Your family thinks she’s a guy, your family thinks the guy knows, the whole — *everyone’s* been assuming a man who obviously knows, and she’s a woman who has genuinely no idea, because she doesn’t watch TV and she’s never heard the name and she thinks—” I stopped.

“She thinks what,” said Reo.

“…She thinks I’m a spy.”

There was a sound from Sun like a kettle coming to the boil.

“Do-yun,” Tae said, very gently, the way you talk to someone on a ledge, “you’ve been living with a woman, who doesn’t know who you are, for two months, and you never *told* her?”

And there it was — the actual problem, the one under all the other problems, the one I’d been not-looking-at so hard I’d let an entire country find out before she did.

“I was going to,” I said.

Four faces.

“I *was.* I kept—” I dragged a hand down my face. “Every morning I’d think, today, over coffee, I’ll just say it, and then she’d look at me like I was a number that wouldn’t add and say something that took the legs out from under me and I’d — there was never a *good* — how do you even — ‘morning, the milk’s off, also I’m the most famous man you’ve never heard of and my mother’s marrying me to a stranger in two days’? There’s no — I’ve never *done* this, I don’t know how people — I’ve never had a — “ I couldn’t finish any of the sentences, which I think told them more than the sentences would have.

“Oh my god,” Sun said, delighted and horrified, hands over his mouth. “Oh my *god.* He didn’t tell her because he likes her.”

“That is not—”

“He *likes* her. He likes her so much he forgot to mention he’s a *person of national interest.* Hyung. HYUNG.”

“He froze,” Reo said, and for once there was no knife in it. He’d gone still by the window, reading my face the way he reads a crowd for threats. “He’s not hiding it like a strategy. He’s hiding it like a guy who’s never had something he was scared to lose. He froze, and the freeze ran two months, and now it’s run out of road.” He looked at me, and his voice did the thing it almost never does, dropped the armor for a beat. “You absolute disaster. You went and got attached to a girl who doesn’t know your name, and the country found your *voice* before you found your nerve.”

“…Yeah,” I said, because there was nothing else true to say.

Hyun came back off his call with both phones still lit, took one look at the four faces, the one mortified face, and the whole shape of what he’d missed. “What,” he said. “What now. What’s worse.”

“The roommate doesn’t know,” Tae told him.

Hyun looked at me. Hyun, who’d vetted the lease, who’d said *the tenant’s a Yun Jae-wu* and let the fight go out of him because a man behind the wall was safe. Hyun put both phones down on the table, face up, still buzzing, and pulled out the one chair, and sat in it, and put his head in his hands.

“You have to tell her,” he said into his palms. “Today. Before the hunt finds the door and she finds out from a crowd outside her own building instead of from you. Do-yun. Tell me you understand that telling her is now the *least* dangerous thing on this list.”

And I did understand it. That was the worst part. I’d run out of every reason except the real one, which isn’t a reason, which is just that the second I say the true thing out loud to her, the two months are a lie she’s been living inside, and I will have to watch that land on a face I’ve spent eight weeks learning, and there is no version of it where she looks at me after the way she looked at me before.

The phones buzzed. The chat scrolled. Somewhere two stops away a woman with a pen behind her ear was doing somebody’s books, not knowing she was the most-discussed unanswered question in the country, humming, probably, without realizing it, a song she’d heard through a wall.

“Right,” I said. “Where is she. I’ll go now.”

And Reo, checking his phone, frowned. “Define *now,*” he said. “Because halmeoni just posted in the family chat that the accountant left early. Looked like she’d seen a ghost.” He looked up, slowly. “Her words. Not mine.”
