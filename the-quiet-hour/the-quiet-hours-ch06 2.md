Rule Six: Never Let Them Drive You to the Door

Do-yun (“Doha”)


Hyun drove, because nobody else on earth was allowed to know where I lived.

That’s the rule under all the other rules. A hired car keeps a log, a log is a trail, and a trail with a box-room address on the end of it is the end of the only weeks that have ever been mine. So it was Hyun at the wheel at seven — Hyun who found the room, Hyun who signed a stranger’s name to the lease, Hyun who drove the boxes over at midnight and then stood at the bottom of four flights and watched me carry my own life up them load by load, because the fewer hands that touch the address, the safer it stays. Hyun, who pretends none of it frightens him, and is a worse liar than he thinks.

“You ate?” he said, to the windscreen.

“There was coffee.”

“That’s not what I asked.”

“There was coffee,” I said again, “and I’m being measured for three hours, not running a marathon.”

“You’d be amazed what they read off a waistband.” He signaled, changed lanes, the wipers keeping their slow time, the city sliding past grey and streaked. “Bag by your foot. Eat it. Sun made me swear on the group’s lives.”

There was a bag by my foot. A rice triangle, a banana going soft, a sports drink the violent blue of a thing that should not legally be a flavour. “Sun packed this.”

“Sun stood over me in a convenience store at six this morning narrating my choices. Quote: ‘hyung runs on spite and we are switching him to carbs.’” A glance in the mirror. “He’s not wrong. Eat.”

I ate. It was the second thing that day somebody had handed me with their name attached to the handing — a thank-you this morning, the wrong size and dragged out of a woman who’d rather have died than give it, and now this — and I filed the two of them together, because it is not a long list and I have the shelf space.

“So.” Hyun let a block go by. “Your mother’s office moved the fitting up. Twice. And she’s coming herself, which she wasn’t, and now is. Which means it is not, repeat not, about the suit.”

“I know what it’s about.”

“Then you’ll keep your face on.” The river slid past, flat and pewter. “The announcement’s locked. The fourteenth. Both houses on a step in front of a hundred lenses, you in the grey, the Seo girl in whatever they pour her into, everyone smiling like it was their own idea.” He said it lightly, which is how Hyun carries the heavy things. “There’s a seating chart. There are talking points. There is, Do-yun, a laminated card.”

“…Someone laminated a card.”

“Colour-coded. Yours is blue, the Seo girl’s is rose, and there’s a third colour for the things neither of you is to say within range of a microphone.” He checked his mirror, deadpan. “It has a lanyard, Do-yun. A person sat down, chose a font, and ordered a lanyard.”

A laugh got out of me before I could stop it — short, real, surprised out sideways — and his eyes flicked to the mirror and caught it, and something in his shoulders came down half an inch.

“There it is,” he said.

“There what is.”

“You laughed.” He picks his words the way he picks everything, with both hands. “You’ve been — better. This week. Lighter on your feet. You sleep?”

“Some.”

“Some.” He let a stretch of wet road go by. “You were humming when you got in the car. Before I’d said a word. That four-count thing, under your breath. You haven’t done that since the party. I’d started to think they’d taken it out of you for good.”

They nearly did. I looked out the window. I did not say there’s a wall in Mapo with a woman on the other side of it, and the song came back the night I moved in. I said, “The rest’s working. Doctor’s pleased.”

“Mm.” He didn’t believe me — the same mm my mother would use in an hour — and he let it go the way she never would. At the SEORÉ gate he stopped without pulling in. “I can’t come up. Reo’s vocal at nine, Sun’s taping at eleven, and Noh wants a status on the record.” His thumb tapped the wheel, once. “He asked if the writing’s ‘unblocked.’ I told him the muse is a delicate animal. He said muses don’t keep release schedules.”

“What did you tell him.”

“Spring. I keep telling them spring. Spring’s starting to sound like a place we never quite reach.” Not an accusation; Hyun doesn’t make those. “Text me when she’s done. Car to the Hannam building — you get out there, and you walk the rest. Don’t let anyone drive you to the door.”

“I never do.”

“I know. I taught you.” And he was gone, back into a morning that stops for neither of us.

-----

The SEORÉ atelier is on the ninth floor, north light, the air held to the half-degree the cloth likes — colder than a body likes, because the cloth is the client here; people only visit. The lift opened straight into it and the machine took me at the threshold: a girl with a tablet, a girl with a steamer already hissing, a rack rolled out with my name on it in a hand that wasn’t mine — KANG D. — three garment bags unzipped to the waist.

“You’re early,” the tablet said, walking backward ahead of me. “Director Ahn will weep.”

“Director Ahn does not weep.” The voice came from across the room, dry as chalk. Ahn was at the cutting table, glasses down her nose, three pins in her lips, not looking up. “Director Ahn has wept twice in forty years, and both times the client earned it. Come into the light, let me see what the cat dragged up nine floors.”

I came. She took my jaw in two fingers — the only living person allowed to; she earned it the night she fitted my first stage suit, when I was seventeen and shaking so hard the hem wouldn’t read, and told me stand up straight, the cloth can’t hang off a boy who’s folding. She turned my head to the window and said nothing at all, which from Ahn is louder than shouting.

“Who,” she said, “did this to your hair.”

“I did.”

“I know you did. There’s no colourist alive who’d leave that line at the temple.” She tipped my head the other way, reading it like a seam gone wrong. “And it’s coming back at the crown — the patch you can’t see to cover, working blind over a sink with one mirror. You did it fast, with a chemist’s box, in bad light, more than once.” Everything a pair of eyes can find on a head of hair, and not one thing past it. “What I can’t read is why. Seungwoo’s hair is the exact colour of the campaign that pays for this room, the steam, the lights, and my girls’ lunch — and you’ve drowned it in a bottle meant for hiding grey before a funeral.”

“Saves time.”

“It does not save time. It costs me an afternoon and a colourist on her knees.” She was already writing on the pad. “You’ll be stripped and re-silvered before the fourteenth, or your mother will skin us both and use me for lining.”

“Leave it black.”

The pencil stopped. The long look, over the glasses. “Why,” she said again. The quiet one, with a floor under it.

I gave her the shutter. “It suits me.”

“It does. That’s the whole trouble.” She capped the pencil and set it down plain, so it would land. “Black hair, a scrubbed face, no stylist’s hand on you in weeks — you look like a man. Not a maison. And a man is the one thing we are not selling on the fourteenth. We never have been. We sell the silver.” She turned for her kit, and for one breath the dryness went out of her. “Up on the dais. Coat first. And your hands — when they start.” She tapped two fingers on the table, my rhythm, tap, tap-tap, tap. “Do it where I can see it, not into the cloth. I’ve known you since you were seventeen and shaking inside your first jacket. I know what the counting’s for. I won’t pretend I don’t, and I won’t make you stop. I’ll just thank you not to bleed it into my pins.”

That’s Ahn. Forty years of reading bodies, and the kindest thing she’s ever done for me she did with a tape measure and a refusal to look away.

-----

They build a man over you.

The jacket came onto my shoulders from behind, an assistant to each arm, settled the way you’d hang a coat on a stand and step back to judge it. Chalk down the spine, cold. The tape ran my arm, my chest, the breadth of my back — and the numbers went up over my head to the girl with the screen, not to me. Nobody said them to me. The measure of my own body got read out across me to a tablet, the way nurses talk over a bed. I held still, because still is the one thing fifteen years made me good at, and I let my fingers find the count against my thigh — four and four and four — and when Ahn passed she laid two fingers flat over them. “Where I can see it. Good. Not the cloth.”

The phone went on the velvet tray, face-up: TAE. Ahn flicked two pins’-worth of fingers at it — go on, I’ve got your bottom half — and I picked up.

“You’re alive.” Tae doesn’t waste question marks.

“I’m a dress form with a pulse.”

“Mm.” The pause did the asking for him. “…And otherwise.”

“Otherwise I’m fine.”

“Okay.” He let fine sit there, unbothered, the way he’s let it sit for fifteen years, knowing to the won what it’s worth. He never pushes. He leaves the door open and waits to see if anything walks through. “Reo’s got a vocal, Sun’s taping, Yejun’s wherever Yejun is. Just me on the line tonight.” He let the quiet go warm. “So. The record. I can hear you carrying it like the whole weight’s yours — like if it doesn’t come, that’s on you alone. It isn’t. Put it down. We’ll pick it up at practice, all five of us, and I’ll sit on you until you do.”

“…Okay.”

“Mean it next time.” And he was gone, and the line going dead was its own small loss, the way it is now — five of us flung across a dozen call times in a city that keeps us moving precisely so we never sit still long enough to feel how tired we are.

The phone buzzed against the velvet before it had cooled. Not a call. The group — the thread that holds the building up now that the building has no walls, messages dropped into the dark for whoever surfaces next to find.

Reo: did you eat the tubs. don’t lie to me. i number them now.
Reo: HYUNG.
Sun: [the practice-room mirror — three of them caught mid-stretch, and a clean gap at the end of the row where a fourth should stand]
Sun: no rush. answer whenever. we love you, that’s all.
Reo: also i’m at the restaurant til four. eomma’s “shorthanded” again, which means she fired the dishwasher again, so if anyone wants me i’m elbow-deep in someone else’s galbi.
Sun: does she pay you
Reo: she pays me in guilt and leftover banchan.
Yejun: The ninth. Your mother. It’s already in your calendar as a dentist so you won’t dodge it. Reo, your tubs are turning, I can smell them from the next room. Bin them.
Reo: THEY ARE NOT TURNING. THEY ARE FERMENTING. THERE IS A DIFFERENCE.

I looked at the gap in the photo, the exact shape of where I should have been standing. There is nothing you can type into that little box that’s both true and kind, so I typed nothing, and turned the phone face-down, and let the four of them go on without me — which is, in its own way, a thing you can stand inside and be held by.

-----

My mother arrives the way weather arrives.

The steamer cut out. The tablet girl discovered urgent business at the far wall. Ahn’s pinning didn’t alter by a hair, which is its own statement and is meant to be. Then the doors parted and she was in the room — never hurrying; the room is what hurries — in SEORÉ grey she wears like a title she married into and never once took off, her perfume half a step ahead of her, the same cold expensive scent it was when I was six and it meant she was leaving for the night.

“Director Ahn.” A nod. Thirty years between them, four words a year. “The shoulders.”

“Done by the fourteenth. The hair too — it’s a wash-out; we’ll strip and re-silver before the step. We won’t discuss that in front of him, or he digs in.” Ahn lifted her pins off the dais, and the last of the assistants drifted out in her wake. “I’ll leave you to it.” The door closed, and it was the two of us and the merger.

She looked at me on the dais the way she looks at everything: what it’s worth, set against what it costs to keep. “What have you done to your hair.”

“It washes out.”

“So Director Ahn tells me.” She crossed to the rack and took the grey sleeve between two fingers, weighing the cloth the way I’d test a string, without deciding to. “Black. You look like anyone in it. A man I’d pass at the market and not turn for.” She let the sleeve drop. “I spent your whole childhood building the other face. The one a room turns to find. You undid it in a weekend, over a sink.”

I said nothing. She taught me silence is the rudest thing a person owns; I learned it off her like scales.

She turned from the mirror to me, which is rare — the reflection holds still better. “You’re thinner. And you’re somewhere else. You’ve been behind your own eyes since the lift opened, and you flinched when Ahn said silver, which you haven’t done since you were fourteen.” Not surveillance. Only forty years of reading what’s in front of her, and a thing she couldn’t name yet moving under it. “There’s something. A girl, a habit, a doctor with a soft voice — I don’t much care which. Tell me now, while it’s small, while it’s still the kind of thing I can keep small.”

I gave her the truth, because the truth, said flat enough, hides everything underneath it. “A doctor. A quiet room. A wall.” Bored, level, hating every word as it left me. “That’s the list.”

She held me the length of a held note — hunting it, not finding it — because the one thing my mother cannot picture is a person who wants nothing from me, and so she went looking for the price, and there wasn’t one she could see, and the look came back empty without coming back satisfied.

“Mm.” She filed it; she didn’t believe it. She came to the foot of the dais. “You’ll be silver on the fourteenth. You’ll stand on the step. You’ll marry the Seo girl.” Not instructions — a forecast, each thing as fixed as the date. “You have never once in your life had to carry a decision, Do-yun. I carried them. Every one, since before you could speak. So don’t reach down and pick this one up now — heavy as it is, sharp as it is — only to prove to yourself you can lift it.”

“Eomma—”

“I put you in front of a camera before you could walk.” Quiet, almost fond, which is the worst of it. “Your first whole sentence, you said it to a director. Everything you are, I made — the way Ahn makes a coat, one true line at a time.” She straightened my lapel with a dresser’s hands; they have always been a dresser’s hands. “And a coat doesn’t get to decide it would rather be a curtain.”

Then she took my right hand off my thigh before I knew she’d moved — turned it over, ran her thumb once across the ridge on the knuckles, the small white scar where it split on a senior director’s teeth. “This. The man. The photographs. The girl who saw it.” She named them the way she’d read items off a bill she had already paid. “You didn’t have to carry any of it. You woke and it was gone, and you have never once had to learn what gone costs — because I paid it, the way I’ve paid for all of it, so you would never have to.”

I never got that girl’s face. She was turned away, hustled out, gone before I’d come round from swinging — and by the time I thought to ask, there was no one left to ask, because my mother had already paid the room quiet and signed the whole night closed before anyone checked whether my hand was broken. A faceless girl my family made disappear. I’ve carried the shape of her for weeks now — what it means that a man’s fist is an asset and a girl is a cost — and I have never once been handed her name.

Her thumb stilled on the scar. “I came up out of nothing with these two hands, Do-yun. I am not going back down into it, and you are the only thing standing between me and the fall. So if anything comes near the fourteenth — a girl, a habit, a wall, I don’t care which — it goes. The way the rest of it went. I won’t enjoy it, and I won’t ask you first; I’ll only have spared you again, and you’ll be angry with me, and you’ll be safe.” Almost gentle, which was the worst of it. “Better you end it yourself, while ending it can still be a kindness.”

A girl, a wall. And the wall she meant was four flights across the city, with a kettle on it and a bolt thrown against me in the dark. I kept my face shut and gave her nothing, because nothing is the one thing I have always been able to afford.

She lifted the dinner shirt off the rack and held it to the light, a woman back on solid ground. “Be silver by the fourteenth.” At the door she stopped, didn’t turn. “And fix the hair. I will not have a son of mine looking like someone’s husband from the suburbs.” And she was gone, the perfume staying in the cold room behind her like the smell of a door closing.

-----

Ahn came back with her pins, the steamer took up its hiss, and they got me out of the grey and into my own black. The watch came back on the velvet tray with my keys and my phone, in the order they’d been taken — the Cartier, steel and heavy, the maison whose men’s campaigns my own face has carried two years running, the watch a woman behind a wall had priced with one flat look the night she found me in a towel. I pocketed it instead of putting it on. The studs were already out of my ears, the ring already off my hand. I wasn’t wearing any of it back.

The phone, under it, had not stopped.

It lit while I was still doing up a cuff. Noh’s office: a status on the record by Friday, non-negotiable. Again — the Cartier desk: the spring shoot’s been moved up, confirm the silver, confirm a date. Again — the schedule: a car at six for the thing tonight I’d let myself forget was tonight. Again — my mother’s assistant, already, the seating for the fourteenth, a question shaped like an order. Again — Hyun: you good? car’s downstairs.

None of them ask. They tell. The band tells me gently and the label tells me with lawyers and my mother tells me with thirty years of bills, and the sum is always the same sum: every soul in my life needs me to be a specific thing by a specific date, and not one of them — not one — has ever needed me to be a person who’s allowed to say no.

And it came the way it comes now, without a door I can point to that it walked through. The steamer’s hiss went too loud and too far away at once. The walls eased back half a step, the way a platform does when a train you’re not catching pulls out, and the floor read lower than it was. My breath snagged up high and wouldn’t drop — pulling at the top of my chest with none of it landing, the dream where you run and the ground won’t move. My hands stopped being quite mine; the cuff I was fastening was suddenly a long way from the ends of my fingers.

I know the shape of it. I’ve had it under stage light, in the back of a car with my hand in pieces, in a bathroom at six in the morning with a chemist’s box in the sink. It crests and breaks if you let it; it drowns you if you fight. So I didn’t fight. I put one hand flat on the dais rail — something solid, something exactly where it is — and let the count come up under my palm, four and four and four, the one rhythm Ahn won’t take off me, and dragged the next breath down past the top of my chest to the bottom, where the air actually lives. And I made my face do the single thing fifteen years built it to do, which is nothing at all. The hiss came back to the right distance by degrees. The floor returned. My hands came back to me a finger at a time.

Then I answered the phone in my palm, because that’s the other thing fifteen years built. Noh — Friday. Cartier — confirm the silver, dates to Hyun. Schedule — I’ll be at the car. The yeses went out one after another, the way they always go out. My no is a sound they wait through; my yes is the only word that ever ends a call. So I handed it over, the way I’ve handed it my whole life.

-----

Down at the underground gate the car waited, engine running, the driver’s eyes finding me in the mirror. Cap. Mask.

And I couldn’t.

Not the car — not the back of a logged car with a stranger’s eyes in the mirror, watching me come apart by inches the whole way across the city; not signing the next hour of where I am over to anyone. So I didn’t get in. I lifted a hand — I’ll make my own way — turned my collar up, and went out the pedestrian side into the rain before the evening could be written down anywhere.

Two streets on, soaked already, I had the phone in my hand before I knew why — and stopped dead on the pavement, because there was no one on it to call.

I don’t have her number. I’ve never had her number. In the whole machine in my palm — every label, every desk, every car and handler in the country — there is not one string of digits that reaches the single person I was walking toward. I couldn’t tell her I was coming. I couldn’t ask if she was even there. I couldn’t do the one ordinary thing a person does when they want to reach another person, and it undid me on a wet pavement faster than my mother had managed in an hour.

And the old cold came up fast and total — the convenience store, the diner, every time the world set her in front of me and then took her back before I’d learned how to keep her. What if I get there and she’s at work. What if she’s out. What if she’s simply not in, and I’m standing in a stairwell with a dead radiator and no way on earth left to find her again.

I looked at the phone. Noh, the Cartier desk, the schedule, my mother’s office, Hyun — all of it still lit in there, all of it waiting to tell me what to be and by when. And I did the thing there has never once been a version of my life that allowed.

I held the button until the screen went black.

Not silenced. Off. The machine lost me entirely, for the first time since I was twelve years old, and the quiet that came up after it was so complete it rang.

Then I put the dead phone in my pocket and walked — the rain coming straight down, the way it came down the night a stranger slid a warm can across a counter and waved my coins back — and the count went into my feet, four and four and four, the wet folded through it, and two new bars of the rain-song arrived the way they only ever come when I’m pointed at her. I stopped under a shuttered awning and dug the reflex out of my coat: a receipt gone soft already, a pencil stub. Got the two bars down against my own thigh, the staff lines crooked, the pencil dragging on the wet paper, the notes blurring even as I set them but set — caught, mine. Not the words. Never the words out here. Just the music, smeared grey on a receipt for instant coffee and a packet of dye, folded twice and buttoned into the inside pocket before the rain could lift it off the page.

The only coordinate the grey city would give back, under all of it, was green going to gold toward the centre, ringed clean as if it were drawn. I walked at it with no way to call ahead and no way to be reached and, for the length of the whole walk, not one soul alive who knew where I was.

Four flights, no lift. I stopped at the top, heart going, the fear right up under my throat — and through her door, through the wall, a radiator knocked twice and went still, a kettle started its slow climb toward the boil, a chair dragged an inch across the floor.

She’s there.

The relief of it nearly put me down on the stairs.

I stood in my own dark room with a dead phone in my pocket and a soaked cap in my hand and listened to her be alive six feet away, and the panic finally let go of my hands.

Careful, I told myself, and meant it more than I’ve meant anything in years. But I was home — the one place in the city that has never once asked me to be silver, or a son, or a yes — and the phone was off, and no one in the world knew where I was, and for the first time in fifteen years that was not a danger to be managed.

It was the only thing I owned that was free. And I stood there in the dark and held it the way you’d hold something warm in two cupped hands.
