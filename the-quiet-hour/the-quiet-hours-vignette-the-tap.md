Rule Thirty-One: A Spy Who Fixes Your Tap Has Been Listening to You Sleep

The kitchen tap had dripped for fourteen months.

One drip every four seconds, give or take — fifteen a minute, nine hundred an hour, somewhere north of ten million since the day I signed the lease and the landlord said he’d send a guy. He did not send a guy. The drip became a roommate. It paid no rent and had opinions about when I should be awake, which was always, and especially at two in the morning the week before an exam that would decide whether the last three years of my life had been a plan or a hobby.

So when I came out for water and the kitchen was silent, my first thought was: he’s killed the landlord.

My second thought, when I found the actual roommate — the paying one — on his back half under my sink, a flashlight in his teeth, my one good wrench in his hand, and no shirt on, was that this was worse.

The no shirt registered first. The way a wrong figure registers before you’ve read the line it’s sitting in — a thing flagged and set aside for later. I set it aside. I had questions about the wrench.

“It’s not what it looks like,” he said, around the flashlight.

“It looks like you’re robbing my plumbing.”

“It’s a rescue.” The flashlight came out of his teeth. He didn’t sit up. “Your tap’s been crying for help for a year and you walked past it every day like a person who’d stopped hearing it. Someone had to come.”

“It’s a tap.”

“It’s a hostage.”

“I was busy.”

“For a year.”

“It’s a long year.”

“It’s the same year everyone else got.” He said it up into the dark behind the pipe, unhurried, a man with all night. “You alphabetized your spice shelf last Tuesday. You’re not busy, Jae-wu. You’re punishing yourself, and you recruited the tap to help.”

I opened my mouth to file a correction and didn’t have one.

He folded himself back under — head and shoulders swallowed by the cabinet, both hands working somewhere up in the dark, narrating the whole operation to the U-bend, because the man cannot do a quiet thing quietly.

And I did a thing I don’t do.

I looked at him. Properly. The way you only look when the looking is safe — when the subject is busy and blind and talking to a pipe and cannot, will not, catch you at it. My eyes went down the length of him and stopped taking orders: the flat of his stomach, the shift of the muscle there every time the wrench bit, the dark line below his navel, the place where the cotton gave up the argument. My pulse picked a tempo I hadn’t set. And somewhere in there my mind slipped its leash and went wandering — to a towel, the first night, a bathroom door breathing steam, a bead of water I’d followed all the way down before I caught my own eyes at it and hauled them back. I had thanked God out loud for that towel.

There was no towel now.

I went red. In the dark, in my own kitchen, over a half-dressed man on his back fixing my sink. Red, like a girl. My weight shifted foot to foot — I was fidgeting, me, who taught herself stillness like a faith — and I could not read a line of it. I can read a balance sheet in the dark. I could not read why a man in disrepair under my sink, a man I’d logged as a liability and probably a fugitive, could reach up into a pipe and empty my whole head out onto the floor.

So I did what I do with a number that won’t reconcile. I assigned it a cause. The radiator. The hour. Low blood sugar. The specific provocation of a man who takes his shirt off to turn a wrench, an act that has never in the recorded history of plumbing required a bare torso. No one’s stomach is an accident at two in the morning. This was a deployment, and I would be filing it under hostile —

He kept surfacing. That was the trouble. Even buried to the ribs in the cabinet he couldn’t stay down — he’d slide half out to land a line, to aim a word at my face instead of at the plumbing, there and gone, quick, then fold himself back under. He has never once in his life been able to hold still.

And on one of them — one of the quick ones, mid-sentence, his eyes coming up to find mine the way they kept doing — he stopped.

He caught it. The colour in my face. In the dark, by the light of one flashlight pointed the wrong way, he caught it anyway, and he knew exactly what it was.

And his face did a thing I hadn’t budgeted for. Not the easy, finished smile he wears like the rest of his good clothes — the one that costs him nothing, that he hands to waiters and to bus drivers and to me on an ordinary Tuesday. This one got away from him. It started before he’d decided to allow it, soft and caught-out and far too pleased, and the second he felt it showing he tried to press it back down — almost managed — then gave up on dignity entirely and ducked under the sink to keep the rest of it off his face where I couldn’t reach it.

He didn’t say a word. He didn’t have to.

I have watched this man be looked at. He’s at home in it the way I have never been at home in anything — strangers’ eyes slide off him and he doesn’t so much as bank the change. A look from me should have been nothing. One more pair of eyes in a life that is apparently full of them.

It wasn’t nothing. Whatever had just crossed his face, I had put it there — me, fidgeting in my own kitchen — and a thing a man does only for you, that he would never waste on a crowd, does not file under flattery. It files under something I didn’t have a column for yet.

“So the washer was shot,” he told the pipe, from under it again. “I cut a new one out of a flip-flop. You’re welcome. It’ll outlive the building and possibly us.”

“You listen through the wall,” I said. My voice came out level. A small mercy, distributed unevenly.

“To the tap. Jae-wu, the tap could be heard from orbit.”

“To me.”

He went still. The fingers that had been drumming a four-count against the wrench stopped.

“You count when you can’t sleep,” he said. “Out loud. A little. Numbers.” He worked himself out from under the sink the last time and sat up — wet to the forearms, a smear of pipe-grime on one — and dried his hands down the thighs of his sweatpants. Then he reached for the counter, where a glass of water was already standing. Poured. Beaded with damp, the chill long gone off it. Poured before he’d got down on the floor, before he’d touched a single pipe. Before I had come out at all.

He’d set it there for me, and then got to work on the noise that kept me up — on the side of the wall I never asked him to listen through.

“Figured if the drip quit,” he said, holding the glass out, “the count would have nothing to keep time to. Figured you’d come out for water, the way you do. Figured you’d sleep.”

I took the glass. He didn’t let go.

Half an inch of cold glass between his hand and mine, and neither of us moved it, and the flat was so quiet — no drip, nothing, just the fridge and the two of us not finishing whatever this was — that I heard the exact second I decided not to think about it.

“This changes nothing,” I said.

“It’s a quieter nothing.” He was wearing it again — or still — the thing he’d ducked under the sink to hide the first time.

“Put your shirt on.”

“It’s across the room.” He made no move toward it, and the half inch of glass between us didn’t grow. “You’ve had a year of opinions about my plumbing, Jae-wu. That’s the first one you’ve ever had about my shirt. At two in the morning. While holding very still.”

“I’m holding a glass.”

“You’re holding that glass like it’s the last solid thing in the room.” The grin came up the rest of the way — the unhidden one now, the one he’d stopped trying to put away. “You can look. I don’t mind. I just didn’t think it would land on you, of all people, and now it’s the only thing I’m going to think about all night.”

“I’m not flustered.”

“You’re a very specific colour.”

The worst of it was that he wasn’t gloating. He’d have been so much easier to file if he’d gloated. Instead the grin slipped, for half a second, into something he hadn’t planned to let me see either — the two of us caught now, one on each end of the same stupid glass — and then he tucked it away behind a joke I didn’t get to hear, because I talked over it.

“It’s evidence,” I said. It was the only word left in my head with edges.

“Of what?”

“I’ll tell you when I’ve compiled it.”

He let go. I went back to my room and didn’t drink the water and didn’t sleep, but I didn’t count either, which was worse, and I lay there in a flat that had finally, after fourteen months, shut up — and added three lines to the list.

Item nineteen. Subject silenced the tap.

Item twenty. Subject conducts plumbing without a shirt. Possible intimidation. Possible heat.

Item twenty-one. Subject saw me notice. Subject said nothing. This is the most dangerous thing he has done yet.

Conclusion: pending.

Threat level: rising.
