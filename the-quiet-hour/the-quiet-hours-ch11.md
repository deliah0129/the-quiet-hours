# Chapter Eleven

### Rule Eleven: Do Not, Under Any Circumstances, Let the Spy Make You Coffee

The last guest left the banquet hall at half past four, and the staff became visible again the moment the doors shut, the way furniture comes back into a room once it empties of people.

I cleared in the rhythm I’m good at — chargers stacked, glasses racked, the long tables broken down to bones — and I was carrying the last of the linen when the floor captain called the tip-out, reading the figure off his phone to the dozen of us in our black aprons. He got it wrong.

Not by much. By the price of a bus week, split twelve ways, in the house’s favour — the service charge run against the wrong subtotal, a rounding that wasn’t a rounding. I knew it before he’d finished saying it, the column squared in my head against the false bottom of his number, clean and certain and unarguable.

I could have said it. I had it, to the won. I got as far as drawing the breath—

and read the room. A temp in a borrowed apron, hired by the night through Director Oh, who skims me the way the captain was skimming all of us and would skim me worse for the sin of counting out loud. The girl beside me — younger, a student too, by the textbook spine poking out of her bag — signed for the short number without a flicker, because she didn’t know it was short. No one in the hall knew it was short except the man counting on it and the woman holding a column nobody asked her for.

So I signed the sheet for the wrong amount, like everyone signs it, and folded the thin envelope away, and said nothing, because nothing is precisely what I have standing to say.

That’s the number. Not the tip-out. The other one — the one the licence buys, the only one that’s ever mattered: a name a room is obliged to hear. A title that turns she’s right into she has standing to be right. Four years of being the most correct and least heard person in every room the night lets me into, and seven days now between me and the credential that ends it.

The captain told us we’d done well and to come back Saturday. On my way out I took a foil tray of untouched canapés off the discard cart into the cooler bag I bring for exactly this, because I stopped being too proud for good food bound for a bin a long time ago, and I stepped into the wet dark with the bag and the light envelope and the squared column nobody wanted, and walked to the first bus of the morning.

-----

I climbed the four flights at seven with the night still in my shoes, and the flat, when I let myself in, was dark and cold the way I’d left it — except for the one thing that wasn’t there before.

A second cup, set out on the shared shelf, beside mine.

I stood in the genkan with my shoes half off and looked at it. It was his — the plain one he bought to keep his promise about separate things — and it had been moved, deliberately, from his side of the shelf to mine, set down next to my chipped one like a place laid at a table for a meal nobody’s decided on yet. No note. He doesn’t leave notes any more than I do. Just a cup, put where I’d find it, by a man who was asleep before I got home and arranged the small evidence of having thought of me so that I’d never have to thank him to his face.

I thought of the stack in the back of my ledger — eleven of them now, the scratched scraps I keep fishing out of bins and from under the couch and off the floor by the door, flattened and saved in the one place I keep the things that matter. I have never been able to say why I keep them. I tell myself it’s evidence, a case I’m building. It isn’t. A woman who has spent her whole life unable to take a thing freely given without flinching has been quietly gathering up the things this man throws away and keeping them, and I have not once let myself ask what that is the shape of.

I set my own cup beside his on the shelf where he’d left it. I did not move his back to his side.

That, too, I decided not to examine.

He’s keeping the ledger now too. I made him rice; he left me a cup. There were entries on both sides of the column between us now, and a balanced book with another person in it is a thing I have never once carried and do not know the weight of, and the not-knowing was the part that frightened me, standing in my own cold doorway at seven in the morning, more than any unpaid bill I own.

-----

The phone had a message on it from the dark hours, and I knew the sender from the shape of the buzz before I read it.

Director Oh, 4:12 a.m.: The Hwangbo warehouse needs an answer by tonight. Two weeks, starts Monday. I held it for you out of the goodness of my heart but goodness has a shelf life. Yes or no by six.

I sat at the level table — his shim still holding, three weeks on — set the cooler bag down, and ran the money, because the money is the one language that has never once lied to me.

Rent due in nine days. The trimmed envelope from the catering had left me short of it by the price of a week I don’t have. The warehouse would close the gap and then some: two weeks of overnights, the day rate paid for night hours, the ninety-minute bus each way I can’t read on without my stomach turning. It would cover the rent. It would buy a month’s breathing room.

And it would eat the seven days before the exam whole. Fourteen nights of inventory under sodium light, four hours daily on a bus that won’t let me study, and a body so wrung out at the end of it that the figures — already refusing to land for reasons that have nothing to do with the warehouse — would have no chance at all.

I typed yes. Looked at it. Couldn’t make my thumb finish it.

I deleted it and typed no, and looked at that, and the rent looked back at me from nine days out, and I couldn’t finish that one either.

The exam is the door that locks. It is the entire reason I have poured coffee and bussed tables and stood at a convenience-store counter at midnight for four years — the credential that turns good with numbers into a name a firm has to keep, a desk no one can hand back, a small brass plate with me on it. I have given up everything that could be given up to reach it. The one thing I cannot give up to reach it is the reaching itself.

But I can’t study in a flat I’ve been evicted from, either.

I set the phone face-down, both answers undrafted, pressed the heels of my hands into my eyes, and the number that came up was the same one it had been coming up as all week: short. The same amount, from every direction. The one figure my mind would still produce on command, and the cruelest, because it’s the only thing it can balance and it balances to not enough.

-----

His door went, soft, and he came out into the cold kitchen in the dark clothes he sleeps in, hair flat on one side, and stopped when he saw me at the table with my face in my hands.

“You’re home.” Carefully, a man checking the temperature of a room before he fully enters it. Then, because I hadn’t lifted my head: “Bad shift?”

“They’re all the same shift.” Flatter than I meant. I dropped my hands and found the level voice, the one that gets me through. “I’m fine. Go back to sleep.”

He didn’t go back to sleep. He didn’t hover, either — he’s learned that, somewhere in three weeks, the way he learned the thank you and the first knuckle. He went to the kettle, filled it, set it, took down two cups without asking, and I watched him reach for the second one — his, the one he’d moved to my side of the shelf in the night — and pour into it like its living there now was a settled matter, no comment made.

It undid the same small load-bearing thing it undid at the market with the cabbage, and I was too tired that morning to get the wall up in time.

“I had a cup,” I said, which is not what I meant and we both knew it.

“Now you have a full one.” He set it in front of me. Steam, and under it the good coffee I still can’t afford and he still hasn’t stopped buying. He didn’t sit across from me, which would have made it a conversation I’d have to manage; he leaned on the counter at an angle, giving me the room, the way you stand near a cat you’d like to stay. “You don’t have to drink it. It’s just there.”

He was close when he set it down — closer than the kitchen strictly requires, or maybe the kitchen is only that small; I’ve stopped being able to tell where the room ends and the deciding starts. His hand was still on the cup when mine arrived, and for a moment we were both holding the same chipped rim, his fingers and mine a half-inch apart on the warm ceramic, and neither of us moved it.

The body I could explain. The body is an animal — I’ve read the chapter — the nearness of a warm person at the end of a cold night is a chemical and nothing past it. It’s the rest I couldn’t get a number around: that I didn’t take my hand back, and that he didn’t close the half-inch, and that the not-closing was somehow louder than closing it would have been. His fingers tapped once against the cup — that small four-count thing he does when he’s making himself hold still, I’ve catalogued it without meaning to — and then he lifted his hand away first and left me the cup, and the half-inch, and whatever was sitting in it unspent.

I drank it. I told myself it was so it wouldn’t go to waste.

-----

He was quiet long enough that I thought he’d let it go, and then he said, to the window, not to me: “The thing you’re not telling me has a number on it.”

My head came up.

“You do the same thing every time,” he said, still mild, still not looking at me, which is the only reason I didn’t bolt. “When it’s a feeling, you go still. When it’s a number, your hand moves — you start adding something on the table where there’s nothing to add.” He nodded at my right hand, which was, I discovered, doing exactly that against the wood. I stopped it. “So. A number. A bad one, by your face.”

I should have said it’s nothing. It’s the word that ends every conversation I’ve ever needed to end. It sat ready. And the warehouse sat next to it, and the rent, and the seven days, and the level table under my hand that he’d fixed without being asked, and somewhere under all of it the simple unbearable fact that someone was standing in my kitchen having watched me closely enough to know that my hand moves for numbers and my stillness is for feelings — when I have spent my whole life being the one who watches and is never watched back.

“There’s a job,” I said, and the saying of it was like setting down something I’d carried up four flights. “Two weeks. It pays the rent.” I turned the cup a quarter-turn. “And it costs me the exam. The one in seven days. The whole — the entire point of all of it.” I didn’t look at him. “So I’m sitting here doing the one sum that won’t come out, which is how a person is supposed to be in two places at once when both of them are the only place she can afford to be.”

The kettle ticked as it cooled. Out past the brick the city was starting its grey morning without either of us.

“Don’t take the job,” he said.

A laugh came out of me, short and without any humour in it. “That’s not a sentence. That’s a wish. The rent isn’t a feeling I can decide not to have.”

“I know it isn’t.” And here it comes, I thought — the offer, the cash fanned out, the number pressed into my hand the way the world has always solved me, by making me into a problem it can pay off. I braced for it. I got the wall halfway up.

He didn’t reach for his pocket. He’s learned that too.

“I’m not going to offer you money,” he said, low, reading the brace and answering it directly, which no one has ever done. “You’d throw me out the window, and you’d be right to. I’m saying — there are other numbers than the warehouse. There’s always another listing. You haven’t found it yet because you’ve been too tired to look, and you’ve been too tired to look because you’ve been carrying the whole thing alone with nobody to hand half of it to for ten seconds while you breathe.” He said it plainly, the way Ahn would lay out a seam — though I didn’t have the name Ahn for it then; I only knew it was the voice of a man used to things being measured properly. “Hand me the half I can hold, Jae-wu. Not the money. The looking. I’m good at finding rooms nobody knows about.” The grey eyes came to me, level, no shutter on them at all. “I found this one.”

The given name did what it does — straight past the surname, laid down soft like a thing he’s been keeping — and landed in the same undefended place it landed the first night, and I let it, because I hadn’t the strength left to mind it.

I meant to refuse it. The refusal was right there. I don’t hand people things; people who hold half your weight own the half, and then they lean on it. It is the truest law I have, the one that’s kept me standing, and I opened my mouth to say a version of it —

— and what came out instead, small, was: “I’ll think about it.”

Which is not yes. I was careful that it was not yes. But it was the first time in my adult life I’d answered I’ll carry it alone with anything other than yes I will, and we both heard the size of it, and he had the grace not to make me pay for the hearing. He just nodded, once, like a man accepting a thing handed across a counter, and turned back to the kettle, and left me the rest of my coffee and the wreckage of my own law on the table in front of me.

-----

He went to wherever he goes, careful down the stairs, and the flat was mine again, and the cup he’d poured was empty in my two hands, and I had not paid for one drop of it.

I should have slept; the night was still in me and the day would want me soon. Instead I opened the book, because the book is the one argument I’m winning if I’m winning any, and worked the problem I’d got wrong twice the day before — and it came out. Once. Clean. The figures landed where figures are supposed to land, the column squared, and I sat very still over it, afraid to breathe on it, because I couldn’t tell whether I’d finally learned the thing or whether the difference that morning was the small impossible fact of a man who’d looked at me closely enough to see that my hand moves for numbers, and then offered to hold half of one.

I’ll think about it. I turned Oh’s message face-up. Yes or no by six. I still didn’t have his answer. But I had stopped, somewhere in the last hour, drafting the yes — the warehouse yes, the give-up-the-exam yes, the yes I’ve handed every Director Oh my whole life because the alternative was a debt with a person’s name on it.

I typed, finally, the thing I could send: No. Not the warehouse. I’ll cover it another way. And before I could run the sum of how — before my mind could produce, on command, the figure that says I can’t — I sent it, and turned the phone over, and let it be done.

The exam was seven days out. The rent was nine. There was a job I’d just turned down and a cup I’d just emptied that I didn’t earn, and a man behind a wall who knew the one true thing about how I’m built and didn’t use it against me, which no one has ever once managed.

I lay down with the grey at the window. I did not count the rent, or the days, or the cost of what I’d just refused.

I counted, instead, without deciding to, the small ordinary sounds of him through the wall — water, a cupboard, the low almost-nothing of the hum — and was asleep before I reached the end of them, which is its own kind of counting, and the only kind, lately, that comes out whole.
