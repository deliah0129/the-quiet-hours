# Chapter Sixteen

### Rule Sixteen: Cooking Is Mostly Leaving Things Alone

She didn’t let me touch the rice first. She made me watch.

“You don’t start with heat. That’s your whole problem — you think cooking is a thing you do to food, fast, with the dial up.” She tipped the rice into the chipped bowl, ran the tap over it, worked it with her hand until the water went cloudy white. “It’s a thing you do with it. Slowly. Pay attention or I’m not doing this twice.” She poured the cloudy water off, refilled, worked it again. Three times, four, until the water ran nearly clear, and the late light through the one window caught the grains so they looked, for a second, like something worth this much care — which I would not have believed of rice an hour earlier.

“Now you.” She stepped back. “Rinse it till the water’s clear. Your hands, not a spoon.”

I put my hands in the cold water and the rice moved through my fingers, and it was nothing, the most ordinary thing two hands can do, and I had never once done it. Twenty-six years old. Fed my whole life by caterers and managers and the silver trays of hotels, by my mother’s staff and the company’s per diem, by anyone whose job it was — and I had never rinsed a bowl of rice until the water ran clear. The plainness of it went through me the way a blade goes through something soft, without resistance, all the way.

“Clear,” I said, when it was.

“Good. Drain it.” She watched me tip the bowl wrong and lose four grains down the drain, and didn’t sigh, just reset my grip with two fingers. “Like that. Now the water for cooking. You remember.”

I remembered. Water to the first knuckle. I set the drained rice flat in the pot, pressed my palm to level it the way she’d shown me weeks before, and stood my index finger upright on the wet bed of it — and she didn’t take my hand this time, didn’t need to, because I’d kept the lesson the way I keep everything that came from her, and the water came up to the pale ridge of the scar across my knuckles exactly. The small white country she touched once and never mentioned.

“To the knuckle,” I said.

“Every time. It doesn’t care whose knuckle. That’s why it works.” She turned the burner on, low, a flame I could barely see. “Now we leave it alone. Cooking is mostly leaving things alone at the right moment. You’re going to be terrible at that part.”

“I’m excellent at leaving things alone.”

“You rearranged my shoes by height.”

“They were unwell.”

It got out of her before she could stop it — a short laugh, the dry one, but real, and she turned to the vegetables to hide it, and I filed the sound away in the pocket where I keep the things worth more than money, and said nothing, because the surest way to make her stop laughing is to be caught enjoying it.

-----

The vegetables were the ones I’d bought. That was the part I couldn’t get over, working the knife badly under her correction — curl your fingers, you’ll keep them that way longer — the radish and the scallions and the good cheap greens that I, personally, with my own bluffing and a stranger’s discount, had put in the bag. I kept glancing at them. A pile of vegetables on a cutting board in a cold flat, and I’d procured them, and now I was cutting them, and they were going to become food that a person eats, and the whole chain of it from market to mouth had run through my hands for the first time in my life, and I couldn’t say any of that out loud because it would sound insane to a woman for whom it was just Tuesday.

“You’re crying.”

“It’s the scallions.”

“It’s not the scallions yet. You haven’t cut them.” She didn’t look up from the pot. “It’s allowed to be the scallions, though. We’ll say it’s the scallions.”

And that — the small mercy of it, the way she handed me the lie to hold so I didn’t have to be seen needing it — undid me worse than the rice had. She knows. Not what, exactly. But she knew there was a what, and she’d decided not to make me pay for it with an explanation. Nobody does that. Everybody I’ve ever known requires the explanation; the explanation is the toll. She just moved the gate.

The song came up in me then, the way it does — warm, unbidden, a bar with the rice-steam folded into it now instead of the rain, the one warm thing in a cold I couldn’t count — and my hand was already drying itself on the dishtowel and reaching for the pencil in my pocket and a napkin off the low table before I’d decided anything, the vow running ahead of the moment as it always does. I got the bar down against the wall, fast, ugly, and this time I didn’t tear the words off and bury them. This time I let the warm line sit on the napkin right next to the notes where anyone could read it, because for the length of one steaming evening in a flat that isn’t on any map, I forgot to be afraid of what the music knows.

“What is that.” She’d seen me do it a hundred times and never once asked.

I folded the napkin. Pocketed it. “Nothing. A tune.”

“You’re always writing nothing.” But she let it go — she’s good at letting my nothings go, the way I’m good at not asking why a woman who owes the world nothing keeps a stack of my discarded nothings flattened in the back of her ledger. I’ve seen it. I’ve never mentioned it. Some gates you leave where the other person set them.

-----

The rice was done. I lifted the lid the way she told me to — off the heat first, let it sit, leave it alone, I told you you’d be bad at this — and the steam came up white and the grains were separate and whole and not charcoal, not paste, rice, actual rice, made by my hands to her instruction, and I stared into the pot like a man looking at the first thing he ever built.

“It’s rice,” she said.

“It’s rice.”

“Don’t make it a whole thing.”

But I’d already made it a whole thing, and she could see I’d made it a whole thing, and instead of mocking me for it she did the kindest cruel thing she’d done yet — got two bowls down, the chipped one and the plain one I’d bought, set them side by side on the shelf without comment, served the rice I’d made into both, and we sat down at the low table on the floor, knees almost touching in the small warm room, and ate the thing I’d made, together.

It was plain. Rice and eggs and the greens from the market and a little of Reo’s mother’s gifted kimchi, and it was, without exaggeration, the best meal of my life, and I knew exactly why and couldn’t say that either. I’ve eaten at restaurants with three stars and my name on the reservation. I had never once eaten something I made, across a low table, from a woman who was letting me stay.

“Thank you,” I said. “For teaching me one thing.”

“You’ll forget it.” But she said it soft, and she wasn’t looking at the rice, she was looking at me, just for a second, longer than she meant to — the dry guard down, the thing she won’t name sitting right there in the open between the bowls — and the small space over the table went thin and charged, my knee a half-inch from hers, neither of us moving, the steam rising between our faces, and I could have closed it. I could have closed the half-inch right then and she might have let me.

I didn’t. Hers, I thought, the way I’d thought it on the flooded floor. The throttle is hers. If it happens it happens because she reaches, not because I took. I stayed exactly where I was and let the moment hang, and watched her decide — watched her almost, watched her want to, watched the calculation run behind her eyes and come up, for once, without a number she could hide behind — and then she looked back down at the rice, and the moment didn’t break so much as it set. Banked. Saved. Unfinished on purpose. A yes neither of us had spent.

“More rice?” she said, to the bowl.

“Please.”

She served me more of the rice I’d made, in the bowl I’d bought, in the flat she’d stopped guarding, and outside the one window the light went from gold to grey to gone, and for the length of one ordinary evening the man who has never been chosen for anything but his face got to be chosen for rinsing rice until the water ran clear.

-----

Later, when she was asleep behind her door and the flat ticked cold around me, I took the napkin out and looked at it — the warm bar, the warm words sitting beside it where I’d let them, the one warm thing in a cold I couldn’t count — and felt the whole shape of what was happening to me land at once.

Five days. The fitting. The silver booked for the morning of. The fourteenth coming in like a tide. A life already signed for, a step already photographed in advance, a woman in Seo Yuna who would stand beside me on a stage while this one slept six feet away behind a thin wall, never knowing she was the reason the song had finally turned warm.

I almost set it down that night. I felt it — the name, the weight, the whole machine — I felt how I might just not go back, might stay in that flat and rinse rice and be no one and be hers, might let the fourteenth come and find me simply gone. The wanting of it was so big it scared me worse than my mother ever has.

And then I heard it — not them, not yet, but the memory of them, Hyun’s voice in the car, anything near the fourteenth that isn’t supposed to be there goes — and I folded the napkin, and put it in the pocket with the others, and lay down on my side of the wall with the rice still warm in me and the song still turned toward her, and went back under. Quietly. The way I always do.

It was the last quiet night we got. I didn’t know that yet. That’s the mercy in it — that I got to lie there and believe, for a few more hours, that the worst thing coming was only the fourteenth.
