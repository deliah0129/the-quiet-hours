## The his/hers split — the thing most often gotten wrong

Both leads narrate first person, same house voice, **two temperaments. The polarity is flipped from a typical rom-com:**

- **Do-yun = the extrovert engine.** Fast, verbal, charming, fills silence, performs-then-reads-the-room, processes outward. His chapters carry the most banter and jokes (charm is his trained idol surface). His longing shows in how relentlessly he attends to her.
- **Jae = the dry introvert.** Terse out loud; her power is the confessional aside, not the volley. She **clocks, she doesn’t quip.** The comedy is the deadpan gap between what she sees and what she’ll say. Her interiority stays, but runs **fast, dry, short-burst — never lush.**

**Never give Jae a motormouth voice.** She is an INFJ; the verbal engine belongs to Do-yun. In a two-hander, he carries the talk and she answers short; her scarce words land harder for the contrast.

## Canon guard on Jae — protect this

Early- and mid-arc, Jae’s confessions-to-the-reader stay in **suspicion / irritation, never admiration.** She is frank with us about how *annoying* and *suspect* he is — not how she wants him. She has not admitted attraction to herself; she still frames him as an adversary. **Never let her narration call him beautiful or leak tender admiration.** When attraction surfaces physically, she explains it away (chemistry, a cold night, an animal body) or frames it as a threat to her self-sufficiency. The rueful “he’s everything I’d want, it just sucks that it’s him” confession is a **late-arc unlock**, earned only as the wall fails. Don’t spend it early.

## Show, don’t tell — the dominant narrative stance (LOCKED)

**This governs every scene, and it outranks the confiding-narrator texture in the Voice constitution wherever the two ever pull against each other.**

The reader works out what’s happening — and what it *means* — from what the characters do, say, and notice. **Never from the narrator stepping outside the scene to explain it.** Show the thing clearly enough that the reader arrives at the meaning on their own. That arrival belongs to the reader; do not take it from them by stating the conclusion first.

Cut, on sight:

- **Significance-flagging.** “This is the part that mattered.” “For the record.” “I need you to understand…” “Here’s the thing.” Any line whose job is to tell the reader how to weigh a moment.
- **Self-narration of arc.** A character naming their own wound, growth, competence, or change (“I was finally useful,” “this was the morning I stopped being afraid,” “I have never felt so alive”). Show the act; let the conclusion be the reader’s.
- **Explaining a feeling after it has already landed.** If the body and the action carry it, deleting the explanation makes it stronger, not weaker.
- **The “on-the-record” editorial tic in all its forms.** It had metastasized into something constant. It is retired.

**The test:** strike every line where the narrator addresses the reader to explain meaning, flag importance, or name their own significance. If the scene still works, it was doing its job. If the meaning evaporates, the scene wasn’t shown clearly enough — **fix the showing; don’t restore the telling.**

> **Before (tells):** *I read Pak in four seconds — it’s the one thing the machine ever sharpened in me, and for once I got to spend it on something real.*
> **After (shows):** *“That’s your fourth-best fish,” I said. “And it’s two thousand over.” Pak stopped. Looked at me. “You know fish?” “I know room-readers. You just read me wrong.”*

The reader gets *he’s good at people, and his real life wastes it* for free — and because they got there themselves, they believe it.

## Voice constitution

1. **Dialogue carries the scene** — long volleys, often tag-free, tracked by rhythm. Thought loses to dialogue when they compete.
1. **Low interiority, high momentum** — stay in the room. Interiority arrives as a fast aside and exits, usually on a joke.
1. **Name the feeling, then undercut it** — one sincere beat, then a deflection. The reader feels it *because* she won’t sit in it. *(The naming is the character’s own — a fast, in-character flicker before the deflection. It is never the narrator explaining to the reader what the moment means. See* Show, don’t tell *above.)*
1. **The narrator confides, but never explains** — direct address and the occasional confiding aside are part of the voice; narrator and reader are co-conspirators against the room. But that channel is for **comedy, intimacy, and deflection only — never to explain significance, weigh a moment, or hand the reader a conclusion.** *Show, don’t tell* outranks this rule. The old *for-the-record* editorializing is retired.
1. **Comedy is the medium, not the seasoning** — no scene wall-to-wall ache; heaviness lands because it’s surrounded by light.
1. **The world talks** — ensemble (KAIROS, neighbors, the diner, the agency) is a comic chorus that heckles, escalates, and outs feelings she won’t admit; plot smuggled under the back-and-forth; every secondary voice instantly recognizable.
1. **MBTI underneath, never named.**
1. **Logic is load-bearing** — every beat survives a skeptical reread; track canon in the state log.

## Toolkit (use, don’t checklist mechanically)

Fragments as comic timing (punchline on its own line) · hyperbole & pop-culture asides · comic cataloguing / call-and-response riffs · repetition & callback as escalation · the verbal spar *is* the flirtation · physical awareness noted-and-joked-past, never lush · dropped dialogue tags · casual, lightly profane diction (don’t write it prim) · cozy / found-family texture.

- **Heat: tension-forward, not explicit.** The charged detail (a half-inch of cup neither moves) does the work; the verge holds; fade-friendly.
- **Chapter titles are “rules”** — a joke on the surface, the chapter’s thesis underneath (e.g. “Rule Eleven: Do not, under any circumstances, let the spy make you coffee”).
- **One 🎧 standalone audio exchange per chapter** — a ~30-second dialogue beat that crackles read aloud on its own.

## Per-scene gate

Dialogue-carried? · interiority as fast aside, not paragraph? · a feeling named then undercut (in-character, not explained to the reader)? · **shows rather than tells — nothing flagging significance, naming the character’s own arc, or concluding for the reader?** · direct address used as comedy/deflection, not explanation? · a fragment landing a punchline? · ensemble heckling somewhere (if present)? · body joked-past, not lush? · Do-yun faster/funnier, Jae dryer/sparer? · Jae’s confession suspicion-not-admiration (until the arc earns the flip)? · one 🎧 exchange that carries aloud? · chapter title doubling as joke and thesis?
