# Chapter Nineteen

### Rule Nineteen: Study for the Wrong Test and You’ll Still Fail the Right One

The night before the exam, my whole life was one morning away, and I could not make the morning come faster or hold it off, which is the particular helplessness of an eve.

I’d stopped reading. There’s a point — I know it cold, I’ve worked toward this exam for four years — where more studying is just a way of refusing to sit still, and I’d reached it by eight and made myself close the book. The licence was on the far side of one sleep now. The room with a door that locks. The brass plate. The name a firm has to keep. Everything I’d poured coffee and bussed tables and counted other people’s tills toward, near enough now to touch, which is somehow worse than far, because far you can endure and near you can only wait through.

So I counted. The won in the envelope, the days I’d survived to get here, the steps from this flat to the test center, the locks on the door. Counting is how I hold still. It mostly worked. The numbers came when I called them — slower than they used to, the way a tap runs slow before the building works on the pipes, but they came — and I let them carry me toward the only thing left to do, which was not drop before morning.

Doha was quiet on his side of the wall, the careful quiet I’ve stopped hearing as a stranger’s and started hearing as his. And somewhere past nine, without a knock, a small thing happened that I’d have refused on any other night.

A cup of barley tea, set on my side of the shared shelf. Still steaming. No note — he doesn’t leave notes any more than I do — and no knock, no hovering, no standing there to be thanked, just a warm cup put where I’d find it and then the soft sound of him going back to his own room so I could take it without an audience. On any other night I’d have logged the debt and braced. That night I was too far down, too hollowed by the eve, and the bracing didn’t come in time, and the warmth went up into my hands before my guard could get there, and I just — drank it. Let it be a kindness. Let it land. The exhaustion did what three weeks of his patience hadn’t quite managed, which was get a gift past the door before I could close it.

It scared me more than the exam. I noted that, and drank the tea anyway.

-----

The phone went at ten, and I knew the wrongness before I turned it over, the way you know a draft means a door’s come open somewhere in the house.

Ji-hwan: Big day tomorrow, isn’t it. The accounting thing. You always did want the little desk with your name on it. I drove by the laundromat again — quaint. Does Doha know you’re up studying, or is he asleep? Roommates should look out for each other. We should talk before your big test. For old times.

I read it the way you read a number that’s been cooked: not the figure, the intent under it. I drove by again. He wanted me to know he’d been by, more than once, that he knew the building and the hour and the name through the wall and that I studied late. Does Doha know — fishing, needling, telling me he’d been watching long enough to have a schedule. There was no threat in the words. There’s never a threat in the words; that’s the craft of a man like Ji-hwan, who has spent his whole life learning to say frightening things in a tone you can’t report.

The ground tilted, the way it does when you realize a thing you’d filed as a closed account has been quietly accruing interest in the dark. He wasn’t going away. He’d dug, and he’d keep digging, and the digging had a patience to it that the loud ones never have. Dread, not fear — I want that on the record, because I don’t scare, I’ve trained it out of me, but dread is a different animal, the slow cold knowledge that something has your address and your hours and your weak point named in a contacts list.

I deleted it. No reply. The silence that is supposed to be a wall.

And then I did the thing I do — I went around the flat and checked the locks, the building door from the window, the flat door, the brass bolt I fitted myself, three between him and me — and I counted them, and the counting did not quite take, because three locks are three locks and a man who reads a no as a clerical error is a kind of weather they don’t keep out.

I did not knock on the wall. I thought about it. The man on the other side of it had stood his own shoulder between me and a stranger at a fish stall, twice, on instinct, asking nothing. If I knocked and said there’s a man who knows where I live and won’t stop, he would — I didn’t finish the thought, because finishing it meant admitting I’d come to think of him as someone I could hand a fear to, and that’s a debt I do not know the weight of, and the night before the most important morning of my life is no time to learn it. So I didn’t knock. I checked the bolt again. I sat back down.

-----

The whole world had felt faintly wrong all week, and that night it tipped over into something I couldn’t file.

Small things. The tune. His tune — the one with no name that finds the wordless place in me, the one I’ve heard through the wall a hundred nights and can hum now without meaning to — I’d heard it twice that week outside the wall. Once off a teenager’s phone on the bus, tinny, a few seconds of it, gone before I could be sure. Once from the open door of a shop, under the radio, just the shape of it, the same climb and the same place it always reaches for. I told myself I was hearing it everywhere because it had got into me, the way a song does. That’s a real thing; it happens. It did not explain the bus, or the shop, or the way the teenager had been watching something on her screen with the particular hunger people have for a thing that’s just started to spread.

And the whispers. I’d caught fragments all week I couldn’t make a column out of — the fourteenth, people said, on the bus, in the queue, the way you’d say a date everyone but you had circled. Is it really the fourteenth. They’re saying the fourteenth. I’d assumed they meant something in the news I hadn’t time for, broke and buried in standards as I was, and filed it under not my business, because almost nothing is my business, that’s how I’ve kept myself small and safe and unscrutable.

I did not know — I want this on the record too, for later, when I’d know everything at once and all of it cold — that the tune and the date were the same story. That the thing leaking subliminally out of teenagers’ phones and the date the city was circling and the careful quiet man on the other side of my wall were one single fact that the whole world was three days from learning and I was the last person in it who’d find out. I sat in my flat the night before my exam and felt the wrongness press in from every side and could not read a word of it, and that not-reading is the cruelest thing the universe ever did to me, because being able to read the room is the one thing I have always, always been able to do.

-----

He came out near eleven — soft, careful, the dark clothes he sleeps in — and found me at the table not studying, the book closed, my hands flat on it like a woman holding down a thing that might lift off.

“You’re not reading,” he said.

“I’m done reading. More reading just sloshes over the side now.”

“Good.” He didn’t make it a conversation; he leaned on the counter at his angle, the one that gives me the room. “Then you should be horizontal. You can’t carry a full cup up four flights of stairs, and you can’t carry a full head into a test. You’ve been full for two days. Let it settle.”

“That’s almost wise.”

“I have my moments. Roughly one a month. You’re catching the monthly one.” He looked at me then, and the play went quieter. “You’re going to pass, Jae-wu.”

“You don’t know that. You can’t make rice.”

“I can’t make rice. I can read a person.” The grey of his eyes came level and open, no shutter on it, the way it does once in a long while — and the look went into me like a key turning in a lock I keep forgetting is set, the same lock it found the first morning over the coffee he made, and my chest forgot its count. The body I can explain; I’ve read the chapter. It was the eyes I couldn’t get a number around. “You’ve already passed. The test just hasn’t caught up to you yet. Tomorrow’s just the paperwork.”

“That’s not how exams work.”

“It’s how you work. You’ve been right about every number in this flat for a month, including the ones I hid.” He said it plainly, no flourish, a man stating a sum he’d checked. “You walk in there tomorrow the same person who found four hundred thousand won in a shoebox a stranger handed you. The room doesn’t know that yet. You go remind it.”

And I had no deflection for it, because it wasn’t flattery — flattery I can bat down in my sleep — it was just a flat correct statement of fact, delivered by the one person who’s ever bothered to look closely enough to make it, and it landed in the undefended place and stayed.

“Go to bed,” I said, which was the closest I could get to thank you and we both knew it.

“Going.” At his door he stopped. “First knuckle,” he said, which made no sense and all the sense, the whole month folded into two words, you know how to do the thing, the measure is yours, it comes out right every time — and he went, and left me the room.

-----

I slept. Almost steady. The dread was still there, and the locks, and the tune I couldn’t place leaking in from a world I couldn’t read, but under all of it was a warm cup I’d let myself drink and a flat statement of fact from a man who’d checked his sums, and I counted the small ordinary sounds of him through the wall the way I do now, and they carried me down before the fear could finish its argument.

Morning came grey and certain. I was up before the alarm, the way you are on the day a thing finally arrives. I put on the cleanest of my thin clothes and ate something standing up and did not look at the phone, where I knew a deleted man was probably already typing. The book stayed closed on the table; I’d learned it or I hadn’t. The locks were where I’d left them. Through the wall he was quiet, asleep, and I didn’t knock, because what I had to do this morning I’d always known I’d do alone, and wanted to, the one thing in my life that was only mine to pass or fail.

I threw the bolt back. I went down the four flights, past the dying bulb, past the door where the couple were already at it, out into a grey morning that did not know yet what kind of day it was about to become — and neither did I — and I walked toward the only door I had ever tried to build for myself, three weeks of a warm flat at my back and the whole of the rest of it waiting, unread, just ahead.

It was the morning everything came due.

I went to sit my exam.
