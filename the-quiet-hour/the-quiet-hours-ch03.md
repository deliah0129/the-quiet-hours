# Chapter Three

### Rule Three: Never Sign Sight Unseen

The number broke on a Thursday, in the stairwell between the third floor and the fourth.

I’d been carrying it a week the way you carry a wound you already know is infected — not looking, and counting the not-looking as treatment. It isn’t. Director Oh picked that landing to call, because Director Oh has a gift for timing the way some men have a gift for languages.

“It’s a slow month,” he said. “I’m doing you a favour keeping you on the books at all.”

“You scheduled me for the Hwangbo job. Three days. I worked three days.”

“And the client adjusted. These things happen. You think I see that money?” Then the pause he’s proud of. “I could put you on mornings. The convenience store past the ring road. If you’d rather.”

The ring road is ninety minutes each way, and he knows it. That’s the whole of him: there is always a worse thing, and he is the man who decides which worse thing sits on the table tonight. So I said nothing. Silence is the one currency he can’t skim — you can’t take a cut of a number a woman won’t say out loud — and I gave him the entire amount. He heard yes in it, the way he hears yes in everything, and was gone.

The line went dead. The stairwell dripped. And I stood there and finally did the sums.

It went like this. Han-byeol’s half of the rent had left on the first — in a suitcase, on a flight to Vancouver, two years, a real program, a real visa. I hugged her at the gate and meant every second of it. Gladness and arithmetic can share one body if you make them take turns. But her half plus the empty half where her half used to be is a number forty-one shifts will not make, however many nights I’ve slid it around the dark until the sliding turned into a kind of sleep.

Which is why, four days before, on that same landing, I’d done the thing I never do. I posted the room. Female roommate preferred, quiet, no smoking. The answer came back inside a day, from a man named Hyun, writing in the clipped way of a person handling something for somebody else: the deposit offered before I asked, the full sum in my account before I’d finished the lease. “My client,” he wrote. Never “she.” Never “he.” Just “my client,” every time, and I let it mean what I needed it to mean, and signed with my thumb, sight unseen, because the clock was screaming and the money was real and the wrong question might have cost me the only deposit I’d seen in months.

I felt the cold then. The particular cold that comes after I’ve lowered my guard — the draft through a door I forgot to lock. Something is going to cost me for this, I thought, on the landing, in the dark, very pleased with how well I’d read the situation.

I just didn’t think it would be standing in my hallway. In a towel.

Four flights, no lift. The corridor smelled of cardboard and an expensive soap — vetiver and cedar, a clean cool money-smell laid over the smells the building actually owns, damp plaster and somebody’s fried garlic and the cigarettes living in the walls. The hall was choked with boxes. The good kind: matched, taped square, stacked by a person who has never once in his life had to wonder whether he’d run out of tape. One of them had been used to prop my own door off the latch. Through the wall a television muttered; down the hall the couple were running the argument they run every night, the words gone but the shape of it familiar as a song.

I shouldered the door open and stepped into my apartment and found it was not only mine anymore.

One room does the work of a kitchen and everything else: the two-ring stove, the dented kettle, the sink under a window that looks straight into the brick flank of the building next door, the fold-down table, two chairs that have never matched. Every object in it sits where I set it. That is the one luxury I have ever managed — a room with no surprises in it.

There were surprises in it now. Three of his boxes split open on my floor, one spilling folded men’s shirts. A pair of good shoes paired neat by the door. A charger nosed into my one good outlet. A watch I refused to price sitting on my table as if it had always lived there. And on the floor by the nearest box, dropped or fallen, a single napkin with something inked on it — five straight lines and a scatter of marks riding them that I took for a child’s drawing until I saw they were too deliberate, and three words crammed in the margin in a fast slanting hand, “gold, no word,” that meant nothing, and that I looked at half a second too long before I made myself stop. A man, said the room, before I had seen one. A man has moved into my flat. Steam breathed out of the half-open bathroom door, warm and green and smelling of him, the pipes ticking down from a shower just shut off.

I aimed the flat sentence down the little hall — the one that kills a mix-up before it can grow into a conversation.

“There’s been a mistake. The room’s taken.”

The man who came out of the bathroom was wearing a towel and nothing else.

Steam followed him and thinned in the draft from the door I’d left open. He was still wet, the towel low at one hip, water tracking down him in bright threads onto my floorboards. Tall. A body kept the way men keep a body when keeping it is the job — somebody’s program, somebody’s hours, the upkeep of an expensive thing. The hair was wrong: a flat matte black, a box and an hour old, a paler patch at one temple where a hand had missed, a few threads of grey still surfacing at the line of it, and the water sheeting off it not quite clear — running faintly grey, carrying the colour down his neck in thin dark ribbons. A discrepancy, in other words. A discrepancy with very good lighting.

The heat came first, because the body is quicker than the mind, and mine went off like a struck match — a low pull, unasked-for, so fast I forgot for one whole second to be afraid. One second. Then the rest of it piled in over the top: who is he, why is he wet, why is he here, where is my phone, how did I —

His eyes found mine. Grey. Storm-grey, no bottom to it.

And the floor of a convenience store three years gone came straight up through this one.

The hair was wrong — the man in the rain that night wore a head of strange expensive silver, and this was a cheap flat dark, badly done — and the face I never really kept to start with. I don’t keep faces. I trained that out a long time ago, because keeping them costs more than a girl living that thin can run. But I’d kept one thing off that man without meaning to, and it wasn’t his face. It was the look. The going-under. And dark hair or no, these were the same eyes. I know those eyes, I thought, with my shoulder finding the doorframe and staying there, because somewhere in the last three seconds my legs had stopped being something I could count on. I gave those eyes a free can of coffee in the rain. Him. My bag slid off my shoulder and slumped at my foot and I did not look down. Rain on the window. The argument rising and falling through the wall. I gave a man coffee once, on the house, and the universe had come to charge me the interest.

He went white. Struck — a man who opened his own door and found the floor gone. Water ticked off his jaw onto the boards. “You’re —” The radiator chose that second to knock, twice, loud; neither of us looked at it. He tried again. “Hyun said the tenant was supposed to be —” and stopped, but I could finish it for him. Not a person walked in. A woman walked in.

Then something surfaced under the shock the way a swimmer breaks water — the corner of his mouth tipped, warmth flooding up into the grey like he couldn’t help it — and he didn’t reach for the towel and didn’t apologise for it. He just stood there dripping in my hallway as though the hallway were the lucky one.

“Well.” Low, warm, far too at home. Two floors down the television laughed at something. “You’re not what the paperwork promised either.”

My eyes dropped. Once. A second too long. They caught on a bead of water — one of the grey ones, the dye still letting go of him — and followed it down because I could not make them do otherwise: the hollow of the throat, the collarbone, the centre of him, one slow inch at a time, down to where the towel mercifully ended the inquiry. Thank God for the towel. It was the only thing in the room with any sense left in it.

And here is the part I’d change if I could. My own body went somewhere I have never once let it go. The heart slamming a count I couldn’t keep. The breath snagging high and refusing to come even. Heat in my face and, under it, a second heat, lower, climbing without a grain of my permission — because I had never stood here. This near a man, any man, all of him bare and warm and an arm’s length off. Other women my age learned what this was years back, in the backs of cars and borrowed rooms, the ordinary fumbling everyone else got handed. I got shifts and a ledger and a bolt on my door. So my body, asked a question it has never in its life been allowed to answer, answered it all at once, with no manners at all, and left me to stand in my own kitchen and burn. My weight shifted foot to foot. I was — I realised, with a flush of pure horror — fidgeting. Me. Who taught herself stillness like a faith.

He caught me at it, and laughed. Not at me. A low, warm, surprised breath of a laugh, the sound of a man handed a thing he wasn’t expecting and pleased right down to the ground to have it. He didn’t cover up. Didn’t shift the towel. Just let me stay caught, and read, plainly, exactly what that look had been, and liked it — the knowing of it sitting open on his face — and the laugh was still in his voice when it dropped low.

“Take your time. I don’t mind being looked at.”

There it was. The ice. The second the words landed I heard myself, saw myself: a grown woman come undone over a wet stranger in her own doorway. The mortification went down me cold and total, the way a bucket of ice goes down a back, my face on fire and my ears on fire and the two heats meeting in the middle until I could have died of it. So I tore my eyes off him for good and reached, drowning, for the one thing that has never once failed me. A problem to solve.

“What is all this.” I fired it at the boxes, because the boxes weren’t him.

The gravity cracked, finally. His hand went to the back of his neck, the grin gone sheepish. “Mine. Clothes, mostly. I hadn’t unpacked — I was going to shower first.” A look down at himself, rueful. “Obviously.”

“You moved in.” The careful eye came back online the moment I had a problem to hold instead of a feeling. Good cloth, and a lot of it. “All of this. For two months.”

The sheepishness smoothed over too fast — a shutter coming down. “Short stays suit me.”

True, and not the reason. I filed it where I file everything I can’t use yet.

“Who are you.” Not the boxes now. Him. Hard, too loud, the fury arriving right on schedule — a demand with the question filed off it.

The grin didn’t survive what he said next. “…You’re Jae-wu.” Every bit of ease went out of him; he set the name down low and careful, the given name and not the surname, Jae-wu and not Ms. Yun, soft, the way only a person who’d kept it would say it — and the cold went straight down my spine, because that is not how a stranger reads a name off a lease.

No, I told myself, and crushed it before it could finish forming. Men do not carry the convenience-store girl around for three years. He read it off the paperwork. That is the whole of what this is.

“How do you know my name?”

“It’s on the lease.” Easy. Reasonable. But the eyes cut a hair sideways first. True, and not the reason.

“You’re Hyun?”

“Hyun works for me. I’m —” the shortest hitch, like he was choosing from more than one. “Doha.”

A plain name. A closed envelope of a name.

For a second neither of us moved. The rain ticked on the glass. Then he did what would, in anyone else, have been the decent thing, and went to get dressed. His clothes were in the open box on the floor — and the open box was behind me, between me and the door, and I was standing dead in the only line between him and it, and my feet had turned to cement. Move, I told them. They didn’t. I was too far off my own axis to manage the one simple thing, to step aside, and I knew it showed, and I couldn’t make it stop.

He could have asked me to move. He didn’t. He came to me instead.

Slow. Unhurried. Watching me the whole way, the grin gone quiet and sure — a man who had worked out exactly what he did to me and decided, with no shame in him anywhere, to enjoy it. He crossed the small room and kept crossing it, past the polite distance, past the line my whole body had been holding, until he was close. Close enough that the cedar-and-vetiver heat of him was the only air left. Close enough that my stupid heart gave up counting altogether and my mind went out like a blown fuse, white and empty, not one thought left in it.

He didn’t touch me. He reached past me — the bare warm length of his arm going by my cheek, a hair from my skin and never once landing on it — and his hand found the open box at my back, the men’s shirts folded in it, and rested there, taking nothing, because the box was never really the point. For one suspended second I was caught in the loose warm cage of him reaching, not breathing, every nerve I owned standing straight up.

Then he drew back, and the cold dropped over me all at once — what am I doing, what is my body doing, he can see it, he can see all of it — and I stepped back so fast my shoulder cracked against the frame.

So I built the wall. Fast, out of whatever was in reach. I didn’t step further in. I stayed where I was, bag where it had dropped, one hand on the frame and the keys biting the other, and I laid it down flat, one line at a time, level, because level was the only setting I trusted.

“That’s your room. This is mine. The bathroom we split — you knock. The kitchen, label your shelf, don’t touch my food, I won’t touch yours. I work nights. The noise stops at ten.” The apron-voice. The one that ties off at the spine and holds the rest of me up. “I signed a lease. I’ll honour it. That doesn’t make us anything.”

Somewhere mid-list the play drained out of him. He clocked it — that I wasn’t sparring, that the flat voice was the one cable holding me upright — and sobered all the way to the floor. The big easy gravity folded down small. He went still, and quiet, and careful, made himself the least alarming shape a man his size can make, and listened to the rest without interrupting, without bargaining for a single clause.

“Okay,” he said, when I was done.

Just that. Okay. As if the wall I’d built to keep him out were a thing I’d handed him, and he was taking it carefully, in both hands. It unsettled me worse than a fight would have. A fight I’d have known how to win.

I turned for my room — my door, my bolt, four walls and no grey eyes — and moved too fast for a floor I hadn’t learned, and my foot found a box, and this time there was no doorframe.

This time I went down.

He was there before I’d finished falling. One arm clamping my back, the other catching me up, and I landed hard against the whole front of him. Both palms flat to his bare chest. Wet skin, the furnace under it, his heart slamming double under my right hand. My face at his throat, the cedar and the vetiver and the warm clean salt of him closing over the world, the rain loud on the glass, the radiator ticking.

Every place we touched was on fire. I have never been this close to a man, I thought, never had one warm and bare and alive under my hands — and something I didn’t know I’d kept went molten and stupid and wanted, with no regard at all for the facts: that I was terrified, that I didn’t know him, that wanting has only ever cost me. It simply burned.

He held me one second. I felt him stop breathing.

Then something in him went taut, went careful, and he set me back on my own feet before I’d found the will to push — both hands quick and sure at my arms — and stepped off me, a full pace, turning away as he went. “Sorry — the floor’s wet. I dripped everywhere.” Already reaching for a box. “Give me ten seconds.”

He scooped up a shirt and a pair of joggers and was gone into the bathroom — ours now — and the door shut and the water ran, and when he came back out he was dressed, soft grey, decent, the towel over one shoulder, the whole bare impossible fact of him folded away. And I was still standing where he’d set me down, palms ringing, trying to remember how a person who lives here is supposed to stand.

My room takes four steps to cross. I know, because I crossed it more than once.

The bolt went over first — cheap brass, the one improvement I’ve ever made to a place that was never mine to improve — and then my back was against the door with the whole apartment on the far side of it: his careful footsteps, a cupboard easing shut, the tap, then nothing. A man learning to move quietly in a house he is frightened of frightening.

I could not get quiet. And that scared me worse than he did, because I always get quiet. Quiet is my whole system, the room I go to when the world gets too loud, and that night somebody had taken the door off it. My hands wouldn’t sit still. I caught them pleating the hem of my shirt, smoothing it, pleating it again, a thing I hadn’t done since I was small, and I pressed them flat to my thighs, and they started again the second I forgot them.

I lay down on top of the blankets in my clothes. The ceiling has a water stain shaped like a country I’ve named and renamed across four years of nights. The radiator knocked. Below, the late argument finally burned itself out. The rain thinned to a slow drip off the gutter, counting itself into the dark. And through the wall — six feet, maybe, of bad plaster — he made no sound at all, and the no-sound was louder than any sound, because I could feel him over there being careful.

My palms still rang with him. The furnace of his chest, the double-time heart, the bead of water that had fallen from his hair onto my cheek. My body lit before I cast a single vote, and it had not banked since.

I let my eyes go around the four small walls — the bolt, the stain, the two chairs that don’t match, every bit of it mine, the first thing in my life that ever has been — and my own name came up at me out of the dark, the way it does on the bad nights.

Yun Jae-wu. I didn’t choose it. Someone chose it for me, which is the only way an unnamed thing gets a name at all. I was left at the steps of a country monastery, barely two years old, with nothing on me — no papers, no note, not even a name a person could use — and from there I went to a group home, where the woman who ran it took me in and gave me both halves of what I have. The surname was her own, handed over because a child has to be someone’s on the paper and nobody else had stepped up to be the someone. The given name she chose on purpose, the two characters set down with care: one that means “to be, to exist, to be here”; and one that means the whole world, all of it under heaven, a house with room in it for everything. Put together they say one thing. You exist, and the world has a place for you.

She told me so, once, the year I was old enough to ask why she’d hung so large a name on so small a nobody. “Every child who comes through that door arrives asking the same question and hasn’t got the words for it yet — is there room for me anywhere. I can’t always answer it the way you deserve. So I put the answer where you’d carry it whether you ever believed it or not.”

I never believed it. That’s the private joke of my whole life. I go by Jae — only the first half, only the bare fact of taking up space and nothing past it. I kept the half that proves I exist and quietly let the other half go, the part that swears the world has room, because the world spent my childhood being very clear that it hadn’t. A thing to be placed and re-placed, aged out, moved on. Wanted by no one in the light, and only ever in the dark, only ever for what could be taken off me and not handed back. And then, grown, the proof came in one last time and settled the matter: a room full of money looked at me, decided I was a liability, and pressed a number into my hand to make me never-happened. So much for the world having room. The world will pay good money to have you gone. I don’t go near that night often. I only know the shape of what money like that does to a person like me, and I have been on the wrong end of it once already.

So I built my life out of the one half I trust. Take up the smallest space. Count it down to the coin. Keep no faces, owe no one. And buy, with my own money, in my own name, the only proof of the other half I will ever believe: a desk, a door, a small brass plate that locks in a way no landlord and no contract can revoke.

I turned down the dorm for it — a bunk in a room full of strangers, handed out by lottery — and got my own door instead, my own bolt, a roommate I chose. Then Han-byeol left, and the rent came due in a single breath, and I handed the key to the one stranger I’d never laid eyes on. I dodged the lottery and walked the long way round into the exact same room.

I counted. The won, the shifts, the drip off the gutter, because counting is supposed to set the world back into a thing I can hold in two hands. Two terms left and the licence is mine. The rent is covered now; he pays in full, on time, exactly as promised. I got everything I asked for. And none of it adds up. A man who smells like money he has never once had to think about, who pays cash to rent the box room off a stranger’s kitchen. A watch on my table worth more than my year. A head of hair he’d plainly dyed himself, in a hurry and badly, the water still running grey off it when he came out of my shower. Men like that have whole floors. They don’t fold themselves up small in a place like this, rinsing the colour out of their own hair over a stranger’s sink, unless the hiding is the point. What he’s hiding from I couldn’t have told you — I wouldn’t know his face from any other expensive face; I haven’t owned a television in years, haven’t the hours for one, read on the bus and keep my eyes off the billboards and on the prices. But I know hiding when I’m living three feet of bad plaster from it. And I, of all people, know what it looks like when money decides to make a person disappear.

And under all of it, the part I couldn’t count my way past: I have never wanted anything like this. There was never room for it — no time, no trust, nowhere safe to set it down — and one fall into a stranger’s arms had undone a whole life of careful in a single heartbeat. A mystery I could solve. This I could only outlast.

The counting didn’t work. First time in my life it hadn’t worked.

I lay in my clothes and watched the country on the ceiling and listened to him not-sleep through the wall, and somewhere past three I gave it up and admitted the one thing I couldn’t count my way clear of.

I was in serious trouble. And it wasn’t the kind that comes with a number.
