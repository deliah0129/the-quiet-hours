# Chapter Twenty-One

### Rule Twenty-One: Never Tell Four Idiots a Secret After the Fact

The practice room over the chicken place was full before Hyun got me through the door, and the noise hit first.

“HE LIVES—”

“Sun, off—”

“—two MONTHS, hyung, I aged, I have a line—”

“Both of you.” Tae didn’t raise his voice. Both of them stopped, because that’s the thing Tae can do, and he put a water in my hand and looked me over once, head to foot, checking for damage the way he’s checked every room I’ve walked into for fifteen years. Found none. Said nothing. From Tae that’s a paragraph.

Then everything went at once.

Yejun’s phone rang and he killed it and it rang again. Hyun’s went and he peeled into the corner with a finger in his ear — no comment, who gave you this number — and the group chat we were all physically standing inside kept detonating on the table anyway, four hundred thousand reposts climbing in real time, and Sun was reading them out, and Reo was reading a different phone out over the top of him, and for a second it was just weather, all of us inside the same storm yelling about it.

“Original’s gone,” Reo said. “Poster nuked the whole account this morning—”

“Gone how—”

“Gone like deleted. So nobody can ask the one person who knows where you were, which is the only good news, which is why everyone’s gone feral—” he flipped the phone around, a still gridded with arrows, a rooftop, water tanks, a convenience-store sign half in frame “—they’re matching plumbing, Do-yun. There’s a girl walking Mapo right now holding her phone against buildings.”

“How long.”

Nobody answered, which answered it.

“Day,” Yejun said finally. “Two. The window doesn’t move and the window’s all they need.” He looked at me. “You don’t go back. A man walking in that door is the thing that turns a voice into a name. The two months are over as of this morning. You don’t get to feel it yet.”

That landed in a small dead pocket of quiet. I let it. Then the noise came back up, because it always does.

“Fine, so we firewall the flat from the inside,” Yejun said, moving again, brisk, the strategist with a fix. “The roommate. Jae-wu. We brief him, he keeps his head down, doesn’t open the door, doesn’t—”

“He’s read in already,” Sun said. “Has to be. You can’t hide a mug in a flat that size, never mind a comeback.”

“Right, so Jae-wu’s an asset, not a risk, we route the flat through Jae-wu—”

“Wait,” I said.

“—give me his number, I’ll brief him myself—”

“Wait.”

“What?”

“Jae-wu doesn’t know.”

Yejun blinked. “Jae-wu lives with you.”

“Yes.”

“For two months.”

“Yes.”

“And hasn’t worked out you’re—” he flung a hand at the phones, the chat, the entire country “—this.”

“No.”

“How,” Reo said, personally affronted.

“Because she doesn’t own a television.”

I didn’t even hear the pronoun leave. I heard the room hear it.

The phones kept buzzing. Nobody touched them.

“…She,” said Sun.

And I watched Reo go still by the window — not the dramatic still, the working still, the one he gets when a face in a crowd doesn’t match its own name. He wasn’t looking at me. He was looking at the middle distance, and his mouth opened a little, and I knew, with the specific horror of a man watching a wave he can’t outrun, exactly which two facts had just slid into the same slot in his head.

“Hold on,” Reo said. Quiet. “Your roommate. Yun Jae-wu.”

“…Reo—”

“My family’s books. The accountant. Jae.” He turned from the window. He’d gone a color. “Quiet. Reads everything. Halmeoni feeds her, won’t take no. Pen behind her ear, fights the espresso machine, doesn’t own a phone case let alone a TV. That Jae. Yun Jae-wu. She’s been doing my family’s books for eight months and rooming with you and I never—” His voice cracked clean in half and reassembled one notch lower. “They’re the same person. Doha’s secret roommate and the girl my grandmother loves are the same person.”

Nobody said anything.

That was the silence the whole morning had been building toward, and it was total. Even Hyun, off his call now, didn’t fill it. Four men who’d been yelling for ten minutes stood in a cheap room and understood, all at once, that the most-protected secret in the country had been sitting five feet from Reo’s grandmother for eight months, getting fed, balancing the till, while everyone — everyone — assumed somebody else had it covered.

“She does my family’s books,” Reo said again, like saying it twice would make it not true. “Hyung. My halmeoni has talked to me about her. To my face. ‘The accountant, so thin, so sad, Joon, you should bring her something.’ And the whole time she’s been living with — and nobody ever — because why would anyone—” He sat down hard on the arm of the couch. “Because we all thought your Jae-wu was a guy and my Jae was just my Jae and there was no reason on God’s earth to put them in the same sentence.”

“That’s the entire problem,” I said. “Nobody hid it. Everyone just assumed.”

“Including her?” Tae asked, gentle, the way you talk to someone near an edge.

“Including her. She thinks I’m—” I stopped.

“She thinks you’re what,” said Reo.

“…A spy.”

The sound Sun made was a kettle reaching the boil.

“She thinks you’re a spy,” Reo repeated, and then it tipped, the horror sliding into something that was almost a laugh because the alternative was unbearable, “she thinks you’re a spy, and you let her, for two months, you let a woman think you were clandestine intelligence rather than say the words ‘I sing in a boy band’—”

“It got complicated—”

“It’s not complicated, it’s four words—”

“Reo.” Tae again. He wasn’t looking at Reo. He was looking at me, and his voice came down soft and flat and landed like a hand on the shoulder. “Do-yun. You’ve lived with a woman who doesn’t know your name for two months. Why didn’t you tell her.”

And there it was. The thing under all the other things. The one I’d not-looked-at so hard the whole country found out before she did.

“I was going to,” I said.

Four faces.

“I was. Every morning. I’d think — today, over coffee, just say it. And then she’d look at me like I was a column that wouldn’t add and say something that took my legs out, and I’d lose the nerve, and tell myself tomorrow, and—” I scrubbed a hand down my face. “I’ve never had this. Any of it. I didn’t know how you tell someone ‘I’m not who you think and also my mother’s marrying me to a stranger on Saturday’ without — there was never a version where it came out right—”

“Oh my god,” Sun breathed, hands to his mouth, lit up with the awful joy of it. “He didn’t tell her because he likes her. He likes her so much he forgot to mention he’s a person of national interest—”

“That’s not—”

“He froze,” Reo said. No knife in it now. He’d been the one to crack it open and he was the one who put it back together quietest. “It’s not a strategy. He froze, the freeze ran two months, and now it’s run out of road. He got attached to a girl who doesn’t know his name, and the country found his voice before he found his nerve.” He looked at me, and for one beat dropped all the armor. “You absolute disaster.”

“Yeah,” I said. There was nothing else true to say.

Hyun put both phones face-down on the table — they kept buzzing through the wood — and sat in the one chair and pressed the heels of his hands into his eyes. “You tell her today,” he said. “Before the hunt finds the door and she hears it from a crowd outside her own building. Do-yun. Tell me you understand that telling her is now the safest thing left on this list.”

I understood it. That was the worst part. I’d run out of every reason but the real one, and the real one isn’t a reason — it’s that the second the true thing leaves my mouth, two months become a lie she’s been living inside, and I’ll have to watch it land on a face I’ve spent eight weeks learning to read, and there’s no version where she looks at me after the way she did before.

“Where is she,” I said. “I’ll go now.”

Reo had already gone back to his phone, because his family’s chat was the one place this nightmare and his real life overlapped, and I watched him read something and stop.

“Define now,” he said slowly. He looked up. “Halmeoni just posted that the accountant left in the middle of the books. Didn’t finish. White as a sheet.” He turned the phone face-down, careful, like it might go off. “Her words: looked like she’d seen a ghost.”
