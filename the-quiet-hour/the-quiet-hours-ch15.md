# Chapter Fifteen

### Rule Fifteen: A Useless Man Is a Safe Man

Director Oh called at nine in the morning, which is how I knew it was bad — Oh only uses his voice when a text won’t let him apply enough pressure.

“Hwangbo fell through, but I’ve got better.” The line was loud behind him, a depot somewhere, a forklift’s reverse-beep keeping time under his pitch. “Cold storage in Incheon. Three weeks, nights, pays like the warehouse and then some. You’re welcome. I had to call in a favour to put your name up after you left me hanging on the last one.” A pause shaped like a hook. “You don’t say no to me twice, Jae-wu. Girls who say no twice stop getting the calls.”

I was standing at the low table with the Maeil Galbi ledger open in front of me — I’d brought it home to finish the quarter, the pink penalty already killed, the columns finally telling the truth — and I looked at the work that was mine, in my own hand, daytime, with my name on the invoice and a small brass future at the end of it, and discovered the no was already in my mouth and weighed nothing at all.

“Then stop getting me the calls.” I said it without heat, which I could tell unsettled him more than heat would. “I’ve got steady work. I won’t be needing the gigs. Thank you for the ones that paid.”

A silence with a forklift in it. “Steady work.” Oh laughed, the laugh of a man who has only ever held people by their shortfall. “Doing what. You don’t have steady. You have me. You’ll be back by the end of the month, and the rate’ll be lower, because that’s what crawling back costs.”

“Maybe.” I was already pulling the ledger closer, the call ending in my mind a sentence before it ended on the phone. “But I’ll be doing the books for a restaurant in Jeongneung that pays me what the work is worth and feeds me besides, and if I’m wrong I’ll find another. I’m good with numbers, Director. I was always going to stop needing you. I just didn’t know it had a date on it until now.” I didn’t wait for the comeback. “Take care of yourself.”

I hung up. The forklift, Oh, the whole sodium-lit economy of men who priced me by my exhaustion — gone, with a tap of my thumb. And the flat was just the flat, and the ledger was just the truth, and my hands were not shaking the way they always shake after Oh. I sat with that a moment. Four years. The figure I keep trying to land on — the cost of the door that locks — and I’d just turned down three weeks of the money toward it without the math even arriving, because the math finally had a better answer than take what the predator offers. I didn’t count myself steady. I just noticed, distantly, that I was.

-----

“We need food in this flat that isn’t evidence of a crime,” I told him an hour later, because the alternative was sitting alone with how light I felt, and light is a thing I trust less than hunger. “You’re coming. You can carry.”

The market off the main road was loud with Saturday — the wet slap of fish going onto ice, a knife-sharpener’s whine, two ajummas arguing the price of garlic with the joy of people who’ve argued it for thirty years and will be disappointed if it ever resolves. I moved through it the way I always do, the small dense web of people who know my face turning toward me as I came: the vegetable man with the good box already half-lifted, the egg woman, the dried-goods stall. Doha — Doha, I still called him in my head, the lease-name, the only one I’ve got — kept his half-step behind my left shoulder, cap down, mask up, learning the choreography of being no one in a crowd.

Somewhere in the crush a stall keeper’s phone was going — one of those little screen-dramas they half-watch between customers, the sound turned up over the ice — and two women a basket over were passing the story back and forth with the relish people keep for a stranger’s bad luck. Some singer, off sick, weeks of it now, a comeback put back, the company saying rest and the women saying worse. Market filler. The kind of nothing that drifts through a Saturday like smoke off the grills. I’d have let it go straight past — my eyes live on the prices and my ears on the scales — except that at my shoulder Doha had gone still. Not his ordinary still. A held one: the breath stopped behind the mask, the cap pulled down a half-inch more than the cold asked for. So I filed it where I file all of him — next to the watch, the sink-dyed hair, the way he reads a window before he’ll stand near it. Hiding. More hiding. A man with something behind him that turns up on other people’s phones. It did not once cross my mind to set the sick stranger on that little screen beside the one breathing at my shoulder, because that is not a sentence a reasonable woman builds. I told him to keep up. He kept up.

And then, at the fish stall, he was — of all the unlikely things — useful.

It happened fast. The fishmonger, a barrel of a man named Pak who has overcharged me exactly twice and been caught both times, quoted me a number for the mackerel that was a thousand high, the Saturday number, the one he tries on everyone. I opened my mouth for the figure that ends it. And before I got there the tall quiet man at my shoulder said, mild, from behind the mask, “The stall on the corner had it fresher at eight hundred. We just came from there.” Which was a lie — we’d come from the egg woman — but it was delivered with such flat, bored, married certainty that Pak’s face did the thing a vendor’s face does when he’s been read, and he grumbled and dropped to eight-fifty before I’d said a word, and threw in the gutting for free to save his.

I stared at the mackerel. Then at him. “You don’t know that stall has it for eight hundred.”

“No idea.” He pocketed his hands. “But he didn’t, either.”

And something turned over in my chest that had nothing to do with mackerel.

Because here is the thing I’d been refusing to look at for three weeks. I had him filed as decoration. A soft rich man hiding from something, useless in the specific way of people who’ve never had to be useful — folds a kitchen wrong, prices nothing, has to be taught the bus. I catalogued his uselessness like a defence, and I knew exactly what I was defending, even if I wouldn’t say it: a man who can’t survive down here is a man who’ll leave, and a man who’ll leave can’t become a thing you need. Keep him useless and he stays safe. That’s the rule. I’ve run it on everyone.

And he just stood at a fish stall and saved me eight hundred won with nothing but nerve and a lie — the way you do when you’ve spent your whole life watching people work a room and have finally decided to spend it for someone instead of on them. He looked faintly amazed at himself. He looked, under the cap, like a man who’d just discovered he could carry his own weight, and was trying very hard not to let me see how much it meant that he could.

I let him carry the fish.

At the next stall I watched him do it again, worse this time, overplaying it, and the radish woman laughed in his face and gave the discount anyway because his trying was funnier than his haggling, and he took the win like it was a stage and the win was real and small and his. He’s never gotten to be useful, I thought, before I could stop the thought — and it came not as the soft thing I guard against but as the other thing, the worse thing, the one the guard doesn’t catch because it doesn’t look like wanting: recognition. I know that wound. I have worn the matching one from the opposite side — never spent on, only ever spent — and watching him learn he could be spent well, by his own choosing, on a Saturday, for a sack of vegetables, put a tenderness in me I shoved down hard, because tenderness is a debt and I do not do debt.

But it was too late; I’d seen it now and couldn’t unsee it. I put the mandarins in the bag he was holding and did not look at his hands, which were careful under the weight the way they’re always careful, as if the weight mattered, as if I mattered, and the number I was holding for the day’s spend went clean out of my head.

-----

Climbing the four flights with the bags splitting the load between us, I made the decision somewhere around the second landing, and it frightened me, and I made it anyway.

“You made charcoal,” I said. “Last week. Out of rice, which is the one thing that’s supposed to be impossible to ruin.”

“I followed the instructions.”

“The instructions are a trap. I told you.” I shifted the bag, didn’t look back at him, kept my eyes on the stairs so the words could come out level. “I’m going to teach you to make one thing. Properly. So that if you ever end up alone in a flat with a pot and no one to fix it, you don’t starve or burn the building down.” A breath. “It’s not a favour. I’m tired of smelling your mistakes through the wall.”

Behind me on the stairs he’d gone quiet — the particular quiet I’ve learned means he’s been handed something and is holding very still around it so it won’t break.

“Okay,” he said, careful. “Teach me.”

I unlocked the door, the brass bolt sliding back under my thumb, and let us both into the small cold flat that has, without my permission, somewhere in the last three weeks, stopped being only mine — and set the bags on the counter, and rolled my sleeves, and did not let myself name the warm dangerous thing rising in me: the thing that has a man’s borrowed name on it, and no number I can use to make it sit back down.
