# Chapter Seventeen

### Rule Seventeen: Never Reopen a Closed Account

Three days to the exam, and I had reached the part of studying that isn’t studying anymore.

I’d read the same standard four times and it had stopped going in. The book was open on the level table — his shim still holding, a month on — and the kettle had gone cold beside me twice, and the figures that used to come when I called them were coming slower, the way a tap runs slow before the building does work on the pipes. I knew the shape of it. I was three days out and full up; there’s a point past which more reading just sloshes over the side. The smart thing was to close the book and let it settle. I am not, it turns out, always smart about the one thing I’ve staked my whole life on.

My phone buzzed on the table. For one bad half-second the old wiring fired — Oh — before I remembered there was no Oh anymore. There was only the ghost of him: a text, the third since I’d cut the line, this one shorter than the last. Cold storage’s still open if you’ve come to your senses. Rate’s lower now. You know where to find me. I read it the way you read a final notice for a debt that isn’t yours. Then I deleted it, which is a thing I’ve learned to do with men who mistake my silence for a door left ajar, and I did not feel the old lurch of having burned a bridge I might need. I felt the flat stay warm. Director Oh was a closed account. I’d ruled the line under him myself, in my own hand, and the books balanced better without him in them.

I should have noticed that I was getting used to that — to closing things and the world not ending. I’d had a run of it. The warehouse, the agency, Oh. A clean column of no with my name at the bottom, and the rent covered anyway, by daytime work I was proud of, in a kitchen that fed me.

The smug doesn’t last. It never does, with me. The universe has an invoicing department and it had been letting an old one ride.

-----

Maeil Galbi at half past two is the dead hour — lunch cleared, dinner prep not started, the grill cold, Eomma at the register doing the thing she does now where she pretends she doesn’t need me to check her sums and then leaves them out where I’ll see them. Reo wasn’t in; it was one of his days at the studio, wherever the studio is, a part of his life he keeps in a separate drawer from this one. It was just the two of us and the smell of last night’s coals, and the ledger open between us, and the good quiet of work.

The bell over the door went.

“We’re not open till five,” Eomma called, not looking up, and then she did look up, and her whole register changed — straightened, warmed, the host coming online — because the man in the doorway was the kind of man rooms straighten for. I knew the exact wattage of that smile before I’d fully turned around, because I’d spent two years on the receiving end of it, back when I still believed a smile was a thing a person gave you instead of a thing they spent on you.

“Ji-hwan,” I said. Flat. A noun, not a greeting.

“There she is.” He came in like the place was glad to have him, which, for the first thirty seconds, it was. He had a paper cup of coffee he hadn’t bought here and a coat that cost more than it should and the easy, sorry-not-sorry warmth that used to work on me and worked, visibly, on Eomma, who had already half-decided he was charming before she’d clocked a thing about him. “I heard you’d landed somewhere good. Doing books for an actual business. Look at you.” He took in the kitchen, the apron Eomma keeps for me on a hook now, the ledger. “Suits you better than the coffee counter ever did.”

“How did you hear.”

“People talk. You know I keep an ear out.” He said it like a kindness. It is not a kindness; it is the thing it has always been, which is a man keeping tabs on a woman who closed the door on him and calling it nostalgia. “You look good, Jae. Different. Lighter.”

“I am lighter,” I said. “I set some weight down.”

He smiled wider, because he heard the edge and chose to take it as banter — that’s his whole gift, taking the thing you meant as a wall and treating it as a door. “Anything I’d know?”

“No.”

Here is the history, in the strokes I’ll give it and no more, because he doesn’t get more than that from me and neither does anyone. Two years. I ended it. I ended it because I caught him — caught him cleanly, the way I catch a column that’s been cooked, the evidence sitting right there once I knew to look — and because a man who lies that smoothly about the small things will lie that smoothly about all of them, and I had spent my whole life learning to read a false bottom in a number and I was not going to pretend I couldn’t read one in a person. He cried. He was good at that too. And he never once accepted the no, because in his accounting a no from me was a clerical error, a thing that would correct itself if he just waited by the door long enough, smiling.

It’s been a year. He’s still at the door. Smiling.

I felt the old thing move in me when he came in, and I want it on the record what the thing was, because it matters: it was not fear, and it was not the bad pull of an ex who still has a hook in you. It was dread. The specific dread of a person who has closed an account and watched the other party refuse to acknowledge the statement. He wasn’t a danger I could point to. He was a draft that finds the gap under a door you thought you’d sealed.

“Eomma,” I said, even and clear, “this is Ji-hwan. He was just leaving.”

“He hasn’t even sat—” Eomma started, host-warm, and then she looked at my face — she’s known me a month and she reads a room the way I read a ledger — and I watched her do the sum, watched the warmth go out of her like a burner clicked off, watched her arrive at the answer a half-second too late.

Because in the warm half-second before she did, she’d already said it.

“You’re as bad as her quiet one for turning up at odd hours,” Eomma said, fond, before the read landed — meaning the time Doha came, weeks ago, sat in the corner being no one while I fixed her books. “Doha. He at least eats when I feed him.”

The name went across the room and landed in Ji-hwan, and I watched it land, the way you watch a coin drop into a slot you didn’t mean to leave open. His face didn’t change much. His face is too good to change much. But something behind it filed Doha — a name, a fact, a there’s a man — and set it somewhere to be looked at later, and the smile stayed exactly where it was, and that was the worst of it, the staying.

“Doha,” he repeated, light, delighted, turning it over. “You’ve got a roommate.”

“I’ve got a lot of things now.” I closed the ledger. I stood. I did the thing I am, frankly, better at than anything else on this earth, the thing I learned behind a counter at twenty-one and have done to a hundred men since: I closed the door so smoothly he couldn’t find the handle to argue with. “It was good of you to come all this way on a rumour, Ji-hwan. You should get back before the traffic. Eomma’s right — we’re not open till five.”

“Same old Jae.” He said it admiringly, which is its own kind of refusing to leave. He set the coffee cup down on my clean counter, a small territorial nothing, a thing for me to clear after him. “I missed this. The wall. Nobody does the wall like you.” He went, unhurried, a man who wants you to know he’s choosing the door and not being shown it. At the threshold he turned, because of course he did. “Tell Doha I said hi.”

The bell went. He was gone. The dread stayed in the room like the smell of a coffee I didn’t pour.

Eomma came around the register and picked his cup up off my counter with two fingers, the way you lift something that’s been somewhere unclean, and dropped it in the bin.

“That one,” she said, “is a liar.”

“Yes.”

“You knew that already.” She looked at me, sharp and kind, the look she gives a receipt that’s trying to fool her. “You closed the books on him a long time ago.”

“A year ago.”

“Mm.” She went back to her register. “Some men can’t read a closed book. They just keep coming to the shop.” She didn’t look up. “Your quiet one. Doha. He read it the first day, didn’t he. Sat in my corner and asked for nothing and waited to be told if he was allowed.” It wasn’t a question. Eomma doesn’t ask the ones she already knows. “That’s the difference. You can’t teach it. A man either reads the room as a thing he’s a guest in or as a thing he’s owed.” She licked her thumb and turned a page of receipts. “Now check my column before you go. I did the gas man wrong again on purpose to see if you’d catch it.”

I caught it. I always catch it. And I did not let myself stand there in a galbi kitchen and notice, out loud or even very clearly to myself, that I had just heard the whole shape of what’s wrong with one man and what’s right with another laid out by a woman over a shoebox of receipts — that I’d spent two years giving a door to a man who treated my no as a clerical error, and three weeks failing to keep one closed against a man who treats it as the law. I am not built to hold those two facts at once in the daylight. I filed the second one where I file everything I’ll deal with on a day that keeps not arriving, and I checked her column, and I caught her error, and I went home.

-----

The guitar was going through the wall when I came up the four flights — low, a thumb keeping time on the bottom string, and under it the hum, half a tune, the one with no name that finds the old wordless place in me every time, the one I’ve stopped pretending I don’t lean toward. He’d had the dented thing a few days; I’d added it to the list of objects that turn up with him and don’t explain themselves, and then, somewhere this month, stopped keeping the list. He was in. The flat was warm. I stood on my own landing for a second and let the dread of the afternoon thin out against the plain fact of him being on the other side of the plaster, being quiet, asking for nothing, and I told myself the easing in my chest was just the warmth coming back into my hands after the cold stairs.

I did not knock. We don’t knock. But I stood a moment longer than I needed to.

I was three days out. I had a book to close and a head too full to read with and an exam that was the whole point of every cold shift I’d ever stood. I made tea I didn’t drink and sat at the level table and got, finally, the settling I’d needed — the figures coming back when I called them now, slower than they used to but coming, the way they do once you stop sloshing more in. For an hour the flat was just the flat and the work was just the work and Ji-hwan was a closed account I’d been foolish to think about twice.

Then, near eleven, the phone buzzed.

Not Oh. I knew the wrongness of it before I turned it over, the way you know a knock that isn’t the neighbour’s.

Ji-hwan: Good seeing you today. You really do look happy. Bit small, though — the place over the laundromat. You always did aim low. We should catch up properly. I’ll find the time.

I read it twice. I read what was in it, which was not the words. The place over the laundromat. He hadn’t been told that. I hadn’t told him that, and Eomma hadn’t, and there’s only one way a man knows the building you live over within a day of turning up smiling at your work, and it’s the way that doesn’t stop because you delete a text.

I deleted it.

I deleted it the way I delete all of them, clean, no reply, no acknowledgement, the silence that is supposed to be a wall and not a door. And I sat with the phone face-down and did the thing that has carried me down into sleep every hard night of my life. I counted. The days to the exam. The won in the envelope. The locks on this flat — the building door, the flat door, the brass bolt I fitted myself. Three between him and me. I counted myself steady.

It didn’t quite take. The numbers wouldn’t hold the shape I needed them to, and the locks, counted twice, stayed three, and the dread sat where it sat.

And then, late, through the wall, I heard a window go up on his side.

I felt it before I understood it — the small change in the cold, a thread of February coming under the bad plaster that hadn’t been there a second before, and I lay still and listened to a man open a window onto a freezing night for no reason I could account for. Then the guitar, the thumb on the low string finding its four. And then he sang — not the wall-hum, not the half-tune he leaks over the sink, but the whole of it, out loud, the words in this time, pitched not at me and not at himself but out, at the city, at the dark, a man singing the inside of his own chest through an open window to a city that hadn’t asked. Came in from the rain with my hands full of nothing. I didn’t know the words. I knew the tune the way you know a thing you’ve heard through a wall for a month without ever once being given it whole.

And given it whole, I finally heard what he was doing with it, and that was the part that undid me — not the sound, the making. He wasn’t pouring it out. He was building it, even now, even out loud: reaching a phrase and leaning on it and setting it down a hair to the left, nothing extra, nothing missing, every bar spent on purpose, the way I run a column until it squares and can’t rest until it does. Counting. Not in numbers — numbers are mine — but the same animal in a different coat. I have spent my whole life as the only person in any room who couldn’t let a wrong figure lie, and I’d long ago filed that under defect, a thing that made me hard to live near. And there it was through two sheets of bad plaster, in another language: he did it too. The man I’d decided was a problem and I keep the same books.

I’m not going to dress it up. It wasn’t his face — I trained myself off his face the first week, and anyway his face was the least true thing about him. It was that he had my exact wound and had gotten further with it than I’d ever dared, and listening to him work it pulled loose something I’d had bolted down so long I’d forgotten it could move. Not wanting him. I need to be precise about that, because the difference is the whole of how I got through what came after. Recognition. The specific, unwelcome relief of finding out, past midnight, on the wrong side of a wall, that the thing you were certain made you alone in the world is only a thing — a thing two people can be handed separately and carry in two different locked rooms — and that the other one holding it is a stranger you’d decided to be afraid of. I lay there and let it hurt. It was the closest I’d come in years to not being the only one. And I did not let myself wonder too hard who a man sings like that to, because the answer was a number I wasn’t ready to balance.

It ran up to a place near the end and stopped, one breath short, the way the hum always stops. The window went down. The flat went quiet.

I lay there a long time after, the dread thinned to nothing against it, and the counting that wouldn’t catch on locks or won or days caught, at last, on the memory of a stranger’s roommate singing his whole hidden heart at a city that would never know his face — and carried me under, three days short of the only door I’d ever tried to build for myself, and one day, though I didn’t know it yet, short of the morning the whole quiet thing came apart, and I learned that I had not been the only one in the city listening at an open window, and that the song with no name had a name, and a face, and that I was the last person alive to find out whose.
