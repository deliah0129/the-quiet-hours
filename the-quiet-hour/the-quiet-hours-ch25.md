Rule Twenty-Five: Never Bleed Where a Camera Can See

Jae


The street closed, and the first thing I did was the wrong thing, which was to look for the back way out instead of the front.

That's the audit talking. There's always a back way; there's a delivery door, a fire stair, a gap between buildings the bins go through. I'd lived on this street four years and I knew its exits the way I know a ledger's subtotals, and the cold useful part of me was already routing me toward the alley behind the fish stall before the warm stupid part had finished understanding that the alley was the problem. You don't run from a crowd. A crowd is a current. You turn side-on and you let it carry you and you get out at the bend. Running puts your back to it, and a back is a thing a current takes.

I didn't get the chance to be wrong for very long, because a hand closed on my arm.

I knew the hand before I knew the face. That's not romance; it's the opposite of romance. A grip like that teaches a body fast, the way a year on a register teaches it a float, and mine went still under it the way it had gone still under hands before, the old stillness, the trained kind, the kind a room watches and decides is consent. My body has a filing system older than I am for this exact thing, and it pulled the drawer open without asking me.

"Jae. Jae, it's me, don't fight it—" Ji-hwan, close, his breath sour with the particular sweat of a man who's about to be paid. "I've got a car. Let me get you out. I'm the only one here who's actually trying to help you, you understand that, right? Look at them. Look what they'll do. Come with me and this is over."

He had a car. I believed the car. I have never once doubted that the men who liquidate me also, sincerely, in their own accounting, believe they're rescuing me; that's the part the morning had already taught me and the part I keep relearning at a loss. The car was real. The car was just parked at the end of a sentence whose verb was sell.

"Let go."

"I'm helping you—"

"You're holding me where they can see you holding me." Cold, flat, the drawer all the way open now. "Let go, or the photo's you with your hand on me, and you can't sell that one. Nobody buys the guy in the frame."

It landed. I watched it land — the math reaching him a half-second late, the way it always reached him late, the boy who could never see the column until it had already totalled against him — and his hand loosened, and that was the moment the current chose for us both.

Someone behind me wanted the shot the hand was ruining. They pushed in. The girl with the bright obsessive face had a phone up and an elbow out and a body's worth of certainty behind her, and she came forward into the gap Ji-hwan's loosened grip had left, and I went back off a heel that had nowhere to go, to the top of the stone steps I'd carried groceries down ten thousand times, the public stairs that drop the street the last short way to the river, worn smooth in the middle by a century of feet and slick with the day's wet.

The stairs are the thing I'd have warned you about, if you'd asked me yesterday which part of my street could put you in a hospital. Not the people. Never the people. The drop they were all standing at the top of without once looking down.

I went down them. Not all of them. Enough. There's a sound your own head makes that you hear from the inside, a flat wooden knock with no echo, and it happened more than once on the way, shin and hip and shoulder and then the back of my skull finding the lip of a step, and then the stone was at eye level and very close and tilted wrong, and the cold of the wet was on my cheek, and a warmth I knew was the wrong temperature for rain was running into my eyebrow and pooling at the bridge of my nose. I have good eyes for upside down. I read the warmth before I felt it. Red on grey, spreading the way a figure spreads when the column tightens, finding the low places, the grout lines, the give in the world.

The phones did not come down. That's the part I'd put in the file if the file had room. A woman is at the bottom of a flight of steps bleeding and twenty arms stay up, because the arms aren't cruel, the arms are just doing the one thing the street trained them to do, which is to keep the lens on the figure that's worth something. I'd spent my whole life being the smudge in the corner that nobody bothered to keep in frame. I'd finally become worth keeping in frame, and the rate for it was my own blood on public stone with my street name underneath.

There's the rule. I'm giving it its own line because I earned it the hard way and a rule earned that way gets a line: never bleed where a camera can see. It is the cheapest thing in the world to take from a person, and they take it at a profit, and they do not need your permission, and there's no drawer you can close it in afterward, because it's already gone out over the wire in better resolution than your face ever managed.

Somewhere up and to the left, where the warm stupid part of me had spent the morning refusing to look, there was a hole shaped exactly like a person who wasn't in it.

I want to be accurate, because I'm the one who keeps mine. I did not, lying at the foot of those steps, want him. Wanting him was a door I'd handed back at a bathhouse desk at dawn. What I did was clock the absence the way you clock a missing entry — not with longing, with the specific irritation of a thing that should be on the sheet and isn't. The man behind my wall was the kind who'd have put himself between a crowd and a person without being asked; two months through a thin wall had taught me that much, and it was the one true thing the rubble had left standing. And here was the worst crowd of my life, and here was me on the ground in the middle of it, and the hole where that quiet steady body should have been was so clean and so total that the audit in me, even concussed, even bleeding, filed it the only way it knew how. He's not coming. Note it. Move on. Don't reopen it.

I didn't know yet that he couldn't. That the same machine that had paid full salaries to keep me unbriefed had also, that hour, built a wall around him too high to climb, and that his absence wasn't a choice but a cage. I'd learn it later, from someone else, the way I learn everything that might have changed how a number felt. At the time it was just a hole, and I was just a woman in it, and the street was just closing the rest of the way.

Then there were different hands. Gloved ones, brisk, a knee in a green trouser leg beside my head, a voice pitched to carry over a crowd without feeding it. "Don't move her neck. You, phone down — no, I don't care, down. Ma'am, can you hear me? Squeeze my fingers. Good. You're going to be fine, you've split your scalp, scalps bleed like a tap, it always looks worse than it is."

It always looks worse than it is. I filed that one to keep. It was, I'd find, the single most honest sentence anyone said to me for three days.

-----

They don't let you keep your own things at the front of a hospital. They take your name and your number and your blood and they give you a bracelet, plastic, a little locker key for a door that isn't yours, and I lay on the gurney holding my wrist up to read the band the way I'd held a stranger's locker key out to a bathhouse girl at dawn, the small administrative click of the world filing me where it files everyone, and I thought, with the flat clarity a knock to the head apparently sharpens rather than dulls: I have come full circle in nineteen hours, from giving back a borrowed door to being issued one. I own precisely nothing that locks. I rent the bed by the hour like everything else.

A nurse with a clipboard and the unbothered patience of someone four hours from the end of a long shift sat down beside me and started the patter that is the same in every admitting room in the country, the patter that runs whether you're conscious or grieving or bleeding into a paper sheet, because the forms don't care and somebody has to fill them.

"Stay with me, just paperwork. Any conditions we should know. Allergies. You're not pregnant. Any chance you're pregnant. No. Fine. Emergency contact?"

I looked at the ceiling tile, which had a brown bloom in the corner that I recognized, the exact shape of a slow leak somebody had put a bucket under and then forgotten, and I did not have an emergency contact, and the not-having sat in the oldest room in me without surprise, the way it always does, of course, who would I put, and I gave her the galbi house, the landline, because it was the one number in my phone attached to people who'd answer a stranger calling about me.

"And while we've got you in the system." She didn't look up; she'd said this nine hundred times. "Donor registry. You're not on it, I checked. Takes one box. You're already giving us the blood today for the panel, might as well do the paperwork once. No pressure, lots of people skip it."

"What does it do."

"If the worst happens to you, the best can happen to someone else. That's the brochure version." She tapped the clipboard. "Real version, it's a tick and a signature and you never think about it again. Goes in the central registry, sits there. You'd be amazed how many people mean to and never get round to it because they never end up on a gurney with a pen already in their hand."

A pen already in my hand. There's a sales technique in there I'd have admired on any other day. I had a pen in my hand because she'd put it there, the way the world puts things in my hand, and I was concussed and bleeding and the only thing my mind had room for was a hole up and to the left shaped like a man who wasn't coming, and I did the thing you do when a form is in front of you and your whole self is somewhere else: I let the form win. I ticked the box. I signed the registry the way I sign everything, small square figures, my name where a name goes, because if a name's going on a sheet I'd rather be the one who put it there.

I gave them my blood for the panel. A girl no older than I'd been at the register slid the needle in and filled a vial, dark and certain, capped it, labelled it, set it in a rack with a dozen others, and carried the rack away through a door that swung and settled, and I watched the one thing of mine that nobody could overlook get carried off to be read by a machine, and I thought, if I thought anything, that it was the most anonymous I'd ever be — a barcode in a rack, a vial among vials, finally and completely nobody.

I had that exactly backwards. But I didn't know it, and the not-knowing is the only mercy in the whole chapter, so I'll let me have it.

-----

The room they put me in had two beds and one of them was empty and within the hour the empty one had a foil packet of rice on it, gone hard, set down careful like an offering, and Halmeoni standing over it with her coat still on and her mouth a flat line that I'd learned, over a hundred ledgers, meant she was furious at the world on my behalf and had nowhere to put it but rice.

"You came back hungry," she said, "so I kept it. I said I'd keep it." Daring me to make it a thing.

"Halmeoni—"

"Eat or don't eat. It's there." She sat down hard in the visitor's chair like she'd fought someone for it, which, knowing the front desk of a hospital and knowing Halmeoni, she possibly had.

Reo came through the door sideways with a cap pulled low and the particular caution of a man who knows his own face is a liability in a corridor full of phones, and behind him, not even pretending at caution, openly weeping, holding a convenience-store bag like a casualty, was Sun.

"I told him not to come," Reo said.

"You can't tell me not to come, she's bleeding—"

"You're going to get photographed crying outside a hospital and then we'll have two problems."

"Then I'll be photographed crying, I don't care—" Sun got to the side of the bed and looked at the dressing on my head and the front of my coat, which a market had sold me cheap three winters ago and a flight of wet stone steps had now finished off for good, and his face came apart entirely. "Your coat," he said, devastated, like it was the coat that had been hurt, and somehow that was the thing, the coat, that put the first crack in the morning's plate, because of all the losses anyone had managed to name out loud all day the only one a person had wept over was the nine-thousand-won coat that was mine.

"It's an old coat," I said.

"It's your coat." He said it the way Reo had said don't on the phone, flat, like a fact, like a price, and sat down on the edge of the bed without asking, which nobody is allowed to do and which I let him do.

This is the part I can't audit cleanly, so I'll just set it on the counter and slide it across. I did not invite them. I have spent a careful life making sure no one ever has cause to come, building a self so squared-away and self-sufficient that the universe could go on assuming, reasonably, that I was already accounted for, that someone else had me, that no one need ever do the actual accounting. And the lesson of the last nineteen hours had been the cost of that, told to me in an alley by a man in an apron: we forgot you couldn't see the outside. They forgot because I built the wall so they could. I had made myself the one nobody had to tell, and then I'd been mortally surprised to discover I was the one nobody told.

And here they were anyway. Uninvited, against advice, at real risk to faces worth more than mine, with hard rice and a convenience-store bag and a boy crying over a ruined coat — here was a room that needed nothing from me, would take no face, would let me be furniture in the corner of it for as long as I wanted to be nothing, and which had, despite my entire life's work, come to find me on a gurney without being asked.

I didn't cry. I want that on the record, because the record's going to imply otherwise. I turned my face to the window and read the city's lights for a while, the way I'd read the warmth running into my eyebrow, from the outside, at a distance, the way I read everything. But I let them stay. That's the only entry I'll make. I let them stay, and I didn't reach for the armor, and the foil packet sat on the empty bed going harder, and nobody made me have a face about any of it.

The hole up and to the left stayed a hole. He didn't come, and I told myself again that he couldn't have crossed the room I'd put between us even if he'd tried, and I was right and I was also wrong, and I wouldn't learn which until later, and it wouldn't matter, because by then there'd be a much larger thing in the room and none of us would see it coming.

I lay back. The dressing pulled. Somewhere below me, in a part of the building I'd never see, in a rack on a cart behind a door that swung and settled, a vial of the one thing about me nobody had ever bothered to read went into a machine that read everything, and the machine did what machines do, which is match — quietly, instantly, without sentiment, a figure finding the figure it had been waiting twenty years to balance against — and a search that an old woman had kept running in the dark since before I had a name I kept woke up, four floors and a whole life away from the bed where I was busy feeling, for once and completely, like nobody at all.
