# Chapter Twenty-Two

### Rule Twenty-Two: I Had the Right Discrepancy and the Wrong Crime

I sat the exam at nine and was at the galbi house by two, because the alternative was going home, and home had started doing a thing to my chest that I did not have a column for.

Four years. Three hours. A room of strangers and the scratch of pens and a proctor whose shoes squeaked on every third pass, and at the end of it a door — the one I’d been building toward since I was twenty, the one that locks from the inside with me on the inside of it — finally, almost, mine. I knew I’d passed the way you know a column’s clean before you total it: by the absence of the small wrong feeling. I walked out into the cold holding the closest thing to a future I’d ever owned, and felt nothing I could spend, because I have never once managed to hold a good thing without first turning it over to check the underside for the bill.

So I went to work. Work I trust. Work doesn’t congratulate you and then ask for something back.

-----

Maeil Galbi at two in the afternoon is the best room in the city and nobody knows it but the people who can’t leave. The lunch rush had thinned to a single soju table up front — two men who’d ordered one bottle at noon and were going to die there — and the kitchen had dropped into its low hour, the hour for eating standing up and fighting quietly and resetting before dinner. The hood fan droned. The rice warmer breathed in the corner. And under it all the side fridge held its one flat sour note, the wrong one, the one I’d told them to call a man about a month ago and that they had, of course, not called a man about.

I had the ledger open on the steel counter and the borrowed pencil going and a plate of trim Eomma had put at my elbow without asking, because in this house not eating is a thing that gets noticed and corrected. She was across the kitchen arguing with a delivery slip. Min-ji was out front pretending the soju table couldn’t see her watching the clock. And the halmeoni was where she always is — parked in the warm seam beside the rice warmer, tailing a bowl of beansprouts one at a time with her thumbs, a small soft fixture I’d decided months ago had simply always been there and always would be, the way the stove is there. I was deep in the back column where the gas payments live, finally written down now, finally telling the truth, and I was, for the length of one afternoon, exactly where the math made sense.

That should have been the warning. The math always makes sense right up until you find the entry that doesn’t.

-----

The noise started at the soju table and worked backward into the kitchen the way noise does.

“—I’m telling you it’s him. Listen to it. That’s not nobody, that’s a trained—”

“It’s a kid in a window. Half the country’s a kid in a window.”

“Play it again.”

“I’ve played it nine times.”

“Play it the tenth, hyung, for me, for the nation—”

Min-ji drifted toward them with the bored gravity of a woman who has decided her shift is now about this. The delivery boy abandoned a tub of plates mid-stack to lean over a phone. Even Eomma put the slip down.

I kept my eyes on the gas column. I want that on the record — that I had a real and decent chance, for about ninety more seconds, of being a person this didn’t happen to. I do not own a television. I have spent four years treating the entire churning business of who is famous and why as weather happening to other people, a thing that did not bill me and so did not concern me. Whoever the kid in the window was, he was not in my ledger.

“What is it,” I said, not looking up, only because the not-knowing had started to cost more attention than the knowing would.

“A ghost,” Min-ji said happily.

“A what.”

“A ghost. Some boy sang out a window in the middle of the night and somebody filmed it and now it’s everywhere and nobody knows who he is. They can’t find him. The account that posted it deleted itself.” She said deleted itself the way you’d say vanished into mist. “Whole country’s playing detective. They’re matching rooftops. Water tanks.” She wiggled her phone. “It’s very romantic and very stupid.”

“It’s both,” agreed one of the soju men, with feeling.

“He sings to the dark and disappears,” Min-ji said. “What’s not romantic.”

“The part where eleven thousand people are reverse-engineering his plumbing,” I said, and turned a page, and the table laughed, and I thought that was going to be the whole of my involvement.

Then somebody turned the sound up.

-----

Here is the thing about a voice you have only ever heard through a wall.

You don’t know you’ve memorized it. You’re not trying to. It comes in at the edge of sleep, most nights, on the far side of nine inches of plaster — a hum that finds the note before the note’s there, the same three bars circling a fourth that never lands, a man building a thing the way I build a column, one careful figure at a time, refusing to total it until it’s true. I’d stopped hearing it the way you stop hearing the fridge. I’d have sworn I didn’t have it.

The phone on the soju table played four seconds of it and my pencil stopped on the page.

It was the rain song. The one through the wall. Came in from the rain with my hands full of nothing — I knew the line before the tinny little speaker finished it, knew the shape of the next one and the turn after that and the place near the end where it climbs and climbs and then just stops, a note short, the ending walked right up to and never taken, because I’d heard it stop in exactly that place a hundred nights running and had started, God help me, to wait for it. To lie in the dark and wait for a stranger’s song to not-finish.

I did not look up right away. Log that, too. For one whole breath I sat very still over a gas column and ran the only argument I had, which was: lots of people sing. It’s a sad pretty tune. Sad pretty tunes rhyme with each other. There is no entry here. Don’t make an entry here.

Then I looked up at the phone in the delivery boy’s hand, and the entry made itself.

A dark window, fourth floor, the city orange behind it. A figure backlit so you got no face, only the shape — a man and a cheap dented guitar, the kind you buy off a pawn rack for the price of a coat, the kind that turns up in a flat over a dead laundromat one week with no explanation and gets played low so the neighbor won’t — and the angle was wrong, the angle was from across the gap and up, the angle was a phone held in a dark window facing a dark window, and I knew that rooftop. I knew that water tank. I had hung wet socks under that water tank.

It was my building. It was the window across the airshaft from mine. It was the man on the other side of my wall, singing the inside of his own chest out into the cold for twelve million people, three nights ago, while I lay nine inches away and listened and thought: I should not be allowed to hear this, and stayed, and heard all of it.

“Told you it’s him,” said the soju man, somewhere very far away. “That’s Seungwoo. I’d swear my mother on it. That’s a Seungwoo high note or I’ve never heard one.”

“Seungwoo doesn’t do windows, he does stadiums—”

“Seungwoo’s been off sick for two months, genius, where do you think a sick man goes—”

-----

I have spent my whole life being good with numbers, and the thing nobody tells you about being good with numbers is that you cannot turn it off. You cannot un-see a sum once the figures are in front of you. The total arrives whether you want it or not, cold and complete, and it does not care that you’d built a life on the answer being something else.

Here were my figures.

A man with no past who climbed down into my flat instead of up, who fanned cash at a grocery like money was a foreign language he’d learned from a textbook, who didn’t know a bus, who flinched from windows, who paid two months up front in an envelope and gave a name — Doha — that I’d taken because beggars audit later. Home-dye gone in badly over my sink. A watch that cost more than my year and then was gone, sold or hidden, in three days. A way of going still and pleasant when a stranger looked at him too long, a learned, professional stillness, a booth-voice that closed questions. A man who hummed in his sleep like the humming was involuntary, like it was the only true thing left in him, and who wrote tunes on the backs of my discarded receipts and then balled them up, and who I had — this is the part I’d been keeping in a drawer I didn’t open — let stay. Whose nothings I’d flattened and kept in the back of my own ledger like they were worth accounting for, which is the one thing I do not do for things I cannot account for.

I had filed all of it under spy. For two months I had looked at a man hiding from his entire life in my spare room and called him a spy, in my head, with a straight face, as a way of not calling him the other thing, which was: someone. I had been so proud of the spy theory. I had clocked every discrepancy and I had been right about every single one of them, every flinch and fanned bill and sold watch, right that he was hiding, right that the books didn’t balance, right that there was a true number he wasn’t showing me.

I just had the wrong crime.

He wasn’t a spy. He was the most famous voice in the country, asleep behind my wall, and I was the last person in twelve million to find it out.

-----

The kitchen kept going. That was the obscene part. The fan droned, the fridge held its wrong note, the soju men argued lineage, and the floor of my whole life went out from under me in total silence and not one of them felt the draft.

Because here is what landed, after the math — and it didn’t land like heartbreak, I want to be precise, I would have known what to do with heartbreak. It landed like a discrepancy finally explained. The discrepancy was me.

Somebody had known. That’s the figure that wouldn’t go back in the box. A man this size does not hide in a flat for two months by himself; there are people, there is a whole apparatus, there are men paid full salaries to know exactly where he sleeps, and every single one of them had known there was a roommate, and every single one of them had decided the roommate was a thing that did not need to be told. Not lied to. Worse. Assumed past. The way you don’t brief the furniture. The way you don’t read in the wall. Somebody, somewhere, had looked at the situation and concluded that the person in closest proximity to the most-guarded secret in the country was the one person it was safest not to mention it to, and they’d been right, because I’d carried it for two months and never once been worth the sentence.

I have spent my entire life being the one they assumed someone else would handle. The one nobody claims because surely somebody already has. I knew that math in my bones before I ever met a man with a dented guitar. I just hadn’t expected to find it on the back of a viral video at two in the afternoon, in the one room in the city I’d let myself feel safe in.

He’d had eight weeks of mornings. Every one of them he could have said four words. Every one of them he’d looked at me across that thin wall and chosen the silence, and I’d thought the silence was ours — I’d thought we were two people good at leaving things unsaid, two people who moved gates instead of explaining them — and it turned out only one of us was keeping a secret and the other one was just being kept in the dark and calling it intimacy.

“Aigoo — Jae-ya.” The grandmother was up out of her corner and on her feet, which I had never once seen, and that — more than the phone, more than the song — was what told me my face had done something I couldn’t take back. She came around the counter and got a hand over the back of mine, warm and dry, and looked at me, and whatever she saw she didn’t ask about. She just decided, the way you decide about weather, that something had gone wrong with me, and that the answer was to keep me in the warm and put food in me until it passed.

“Sit. Sit down, child, you’ve gone the color of the rice.” A bowl came from somewhere. A cup of barley tea. Her hand stayed light on my shoulder, an anchor pretending to be a fuss, while her other hand went into her apron and came out with her phone and started, slow and stubborn, to dial. “Eat. You’ll eat, and you’ll feel better, and then you’ll tell me nothing, that’s all right, you’ll tell your halmeoni nothing —” the phone at her ear now, ringing somewhere I couldn’t hear. “Joon-ah. Pick up. Pick up for your grandmother.”

I sat as long as a person can sit inside a thing that’s still falling. The bowl cooled. The tea went untouched. Across the kitchen Eomma had stopped fighting the delivery slip, her pencil gone still on the page — and from Eomma a stilled pencil is a hand laid on your shoulder. The whole room had gone careful around me, the way a room goes careful around bad news it can’t yet see the shape of.

Then I stood up, because I could not be inside walls that knew me for one second longer, and the grandmother — who had been trying to keep me, and was old enough to know the exact moment that stopped being possible — didn’t reach for me again. She moved fast instead: tipped the bowl into a foil packet, pulled a cold barley juice from the case, and folded both into my hands, my fingers around them, the way you fold a child’s hand around a coin. “Take it. Take it, go on. Eat when you can.”

I took them. I don’t remember deciding to. I took the food and the juice the way you take anything held out when the floor’s gone — both hands, absent, no thank-you left anywhere in me to give her — and I turned for the door.

I did not run. That goes in last, for whoever’s keeping mine. I walked out of Maeil Galbi at a normal speed, past a wall of people who loved me and didn’t know they were watching a woman find out she’d been furniture, and I got the door open, and the bell did its small bright stupid sound, and the cold came in.

Behind me, the call connected. I heard it in the change in her voice — relief and fear arriving in the same breath: “Joon-ah. The accountant, our Jae — she’s left, she didn’t finish, white as a sheet, like she’d seen a ghost —”

And the door shut on the rest.

None of them knew the shape of it — not the grandmother with her foil packet, not the soju men, not the boy with the window still glowing in his fist. They knew the famous part. Not one of them knew the part that mattered, which was mine: that the ghost had a key to my flat and a name I’d given him and two months of my mornings, and I had let him. I had let him. I had unlocked the one door I keep locked and handed it to a stranger in a borrowed name and called it the best thing that had happened to me in years.

-----

The street outside was just a street. That was almost the worst of it. The fish stall, the knife-sharpener, the long ordinary slope down toward the river — none of it had heard, none of it had stopped. I stood on the cold pavement with my four-years-almost-a-future in one pocket and the entire bottom fallen out of the rest of it and I did the thing I do, the only thing, the thing that has never once failed me the way people fail me: I started to count.

I counted what I’d paid and what I was owed. I counted the months and the mornings and the rinsed rice and the kept napkins, every soft entry I’d let onto the books against my own rule, and I totaled them honestly the way I total everything, and the answer came up the same cold complete way answers always do, and the answer was that I had been seen by the entire country and overlooked by the one man standing close enough to do it, and those are not the same kind of seen, and I had let myself forget there was a difference.

A phone went by me in someone’s hand, his voice leaking out of it, the rain song climbing toward the place it stops.

Home was four flights and a thin wall and a window across an airshaft and a man who would, I had no doubt, be standing in it by now with four true words two months too late. Home was the one door I had let unlock. I had spent four years and three hours that morning earning a door that locks, and I could not, tonight, make myself walk back through the one I’d already failed to keep shut.

So I didn’t.

I turned away from the river and the laundromat and the whole small map I knew, and I walked. Not toward anything — just away, the juice sweating cold in one hand and the foil warm against my hip, the city handing me his voice out of every third phone I passed and taking it back. I walked until the shop signs changed and changed again into names I didn’t know, until my own feet were the only honest column I had left: one in front of the other, no total, no door at the end of it.

I ended up, somewhere near midnight, at a bathhouse off a road I couldn’t have named, because it was warm and it was open and no one in it knew my face. I paid out of the careful money I keep for nothing and never spend, and I locked my shoes in a locker that locked with a plastic band around my wrist — a lock that wasn’t mine, that I’d give back at the desk in the morning — and I lay down on a mat on a heated floor in a long quiet room full of strangers sleeping in rows, every one of us with a door that wasn’t ours.

I did not count myself to sleep. I just stopped. On the one night I had finally earned a door that locks, I had nowhere of my own to put it, and I lay among strangers in the borrowed warm and let the day be over, and did not let myself wonder, even once, whether he was still standing in the window, waiting for the wall to answer.
