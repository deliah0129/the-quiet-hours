Rule Nine: Carry Your Own Weight

Jae


The market was mine on a Thursday, and I had never once had to share it.

I did today. The soft stranger kept a pace behind my left shoulder, cap down, hands not knowing what to do with themselves, and I moved faster than I needed to out of pure spite, to see if he’d fall back. He didn’t. He stayed a half-step off me like a man who’d decided the half-step was a privilege, said nothing, and carried the bag I shoved at him at the second stall — because giving a man a job is cheaper than explaining why he can’t have one.

I owe everyone here, which is a different and better thing than knowing them. The vegetable man had the spring greens turned face-out before I’d stopped walking; I lifted a bundle, found the wilt under the good leaves, set it down, and he laughed and brought the better box up from under the counter without a word, because I’d caught him at it before and we keep the same accounts. I counted the change off his palm into mine and it came out right, the way market change always comes out right, the numbers landing clean and certain under my fingers the way nothing had landed clean for me in a week.

Behind me, the stranger watched me do it like a man at a museum.

“You’re staring,” I said, not turning.

“You know everyone.”

“I owe everyone.” Not a joke, though I let it wear one. “Different thing.” I found three mandarins that passed and dropped them into the bag he was holding, and his hand was careful under it, taking the weight like the weight mattered, and I did not look at his hand, because looking at his hands is on the long list of things I’ve stopped letting myself do.

-----

The phone went against my hip at the dried-goods stall, and I knew the buzz the way you know a particular knock at a particular door.

Director Oh. I stepped two paces off into the gap between awnings and took it, because not taking it costs more than taking it.

“Jae-wu-ssi.” Warm. He’s only ever warm when it’s going to cost me. “Good news. The Hwangbo people want you back — overnights, the warehouse inventory, two weeks solid. Better money.”

“The overnight warehouse is a ninety-minute bus each way and it pays the day rate for night hours.”

“It pays more than you’re making now, is what it pays.” A pause he was pleased with. “And about the catering Friday — the client trimmed the hours, so that envelope’s lighter than we said. These things happen. I’m doing you a favour even floating the warehouse; there’s a line of girls who’d take it.”

I said nothing. Silence is the one coin he can’t skim off the top, and I’ve learned to pay him in it. In the gap I ran the sum I didn’t want to run, fast and brutal: the exam eight days out; the warehouse fourteen nights of no studying and four hours of bus I can’t read on, because the bus makes me sick; the trimmed envelope rent I’d counted on, already spent in my head. Eight days. The licence on one side of the scale and two weeks of somebody else’s inventory on the other.

“I’ll let you think about it,” Oh said, which means he’d already pencilled me in. The line died.

When I turned back, the stranger was exactly where I’d left him, holding the bag, not pretending he hadn’t heard the half of it that carried. He didn’t ask. That’s the one thing I’ll give him — he never asks the question that would make me explain myself. He just shifted the bag to his other hand and said, “Where next,” like the call had been weather and the weather had passed, and something in my chest I don’t have a number for eased a half-degree it had no business easing.

Then, between one stall and the next, he stopped. Just stopped, the bag swinging, his head tipped half an inch like a man catching a sound under the market noise, and he set the bag down and wrote on the back of his own hand with a pencil stub he had somewhere — fast, four or five strokes, eyes gone somewhere I couldn’t follow — and picked the bag back up and walked on as if nothing had happened. I’d seen him do it twice now. I filed it with the napkins and the receipts and the binned scraps and the thing I will not look at directly, and said nothing, because asking would mean admitting I’d been counting his strangenesses like a woman keeping a tally she means to do something with.

“Fish,” I said, and went, because going was better than standing in the look he wasn’t giving me.

-----

It nearly came apart at the fish stalls, and I was the only one who saw it start.

A schoolgirl — sixteen, a phone already half up the way the young hold phones now, like a reflex they were born with — stopped mid-step and looked at Doha, and looked again, the second look gone sharp and certain at the edges. I’ve spent my whole life reading rooms for exactly that look: the recalculation, the moment a stranger stops being scenery and becomes a thing that wants something. I’ve worn the receiving end of it myself, more nights than I’ll count, a man’s eyes deciding I was available. I know the shape of prey being chosen from both sides.

And beside me the stranger went still — wrong-still, the stillness of a man who’s heard a sound in the dark, the easy half-step gone rigid, his free hand starting some small count against his thigh he didn’t seem to know he was doing.

I moved before I’d decided to.

“Doha.” Flat, bored, a woman addressing the man who carries her bags. I shoved the radish bunch against his chest so he had to grab it. “Hold the radishes. You’re in the man’s way.” And I stepped him sideways with one shoulder into the press of halmeonis at the next stall, into the steam and the haggling and the absolute ordinariness of it — a tall quiet man carrying a sharp-eyed girl’s vegetables on a wet morning, which is the least remarkable thing in six streets — and the schoolgirl’s certainty wobbled and folded, and the phone went back down, and the moment closed over like water.

It was done in three seconds. My heart was going too fast for three seconds’ work.

I covered for him. I kept my eyes on the mackerel and would not look at what I’d just done. I keep no one. I owe no one and I am owed by no one and that is the whole architecture of how I’ve stayed standing — and I had just put my own shoulder between a stranger and a stranger on pure animal instinct, the way you put a hand out for someone falling, and I did not have the first idea why, and the not-knowing was worse than the doing.

He didn’t thank me. He’s clever enough not to. But the look he gave the side of my face was one I could feel without turning, and I did not turn, because the eyes are the one thing I’ve stopped being able to count my way out of, and I had a fish to buy.

-----

At the last stall I stopped in front of the cabbages and did the math I’d rather he didn’t watch me do.

A hand on a good firm head — and a glance at the worn envelope, the trimmed envelope, the eight days, the warehouse waiting in it — and I set the good one back and took up the smaller, lighter, cheaper one, because that’s the shape of the week and I would sooner not eat than let it show on my face.

The good head was on the vendor’s scale before I’d registered him moving.

“Let me get this one,” he said. To me, plain, no fold of cash fanned at me this time — he learned that, somewhere, I don’t know where. “You carried the bags last week.”

There were no bags last week. There was no last week. We’d known each other a fortnight of bad plaster. It was a lie, and it was the right shape of lie — not charity, not a man paying for a thing he wants paid back, just two people keeping a level column between them, a turn taken — and I stood there and ran it for the trap the way I run everything, the angle, the price named later in the dark, the cost of saying yes.

There wasn’t one. I looked and looked and there was no price on it. Just a man who can’t make rice, buying a cabbage, getting the grammar of it almost right.

It undid something small and load-bearing in me, and I hated it.

“…Fine,” I said, like it had been pulled out of me with pliers. “Next one’s mine. And you carry that, too.”

“I’m carrying everything,” he said.

“You’re soft. It’s good for you.” And there it was, before I could stop it — not a smile, I don’t spend those — the place a smile would sit, a warmth at the eyes I’d deny under any oath he could name. I turned it off and turned away, but he saw it, and I knew he saw it, and the knowing followed me down the wet row like a hand not quite touching my back.

-----

I studied at the fold-down table with the cabbage — the good one, his — going quietly judgmental in the fridge, and the numbers would not come.

The exam was eight days out. I’ve worked this class of problem a thousand times; I can do them on a bus, in a queue, half-asleep on the safe side of my own bolt. I opened the book and set my pencil and the figures swam and refused to land — I carried a one wrong, which I do not do, have never done, told him I never do — and worked it again and got a different wrong answer, which is worse, because a person who’s consistently wrong has at least got a system. And that frightened me worse than Oh, worse than the warehouse, worse than a stranger with no surname and a watch worth my year.

The licence is the only road I’ve got. One sharp line, and then I made myself stop, because the rest of the thought had a man in it.

I got up. I did the dishes I didn’t have. I came back. I did the problem a third time at the kitchen counter standing up, because the chair had started to feel like a place where thinking goes to die, and it came out right, finally, the once — and I couldn’t tell if I’d learned it or simply outlasted it, and on an exam there is no standing up, no third try, no kettle to go and fill while the answer assembles itself behind your back.

I pressed the heels of my hands into my eyes. Eight days. Two weeks of warehouse nights would buy the rent and cost me the exam. Skipping the warehouse would keep the study days and lose the rent the trimmed envelope had already lost me. There was no version where every column totalled; I’d run it from every direction and it came up short the same amount each time, which was the one figure I’d got to land all day.

Through the wall, the soft, low, almost-nothing of him moving around his room. Careful, always careful, a man learning to live quiet in a house he’s afraid of frightening. I could have put my fist to the plaster and told him to stop being so considerate — it’s the considerateness that’s wrecking me; a loud man I could armour against —

The phone lit. Not Oh. A face I do set down and keep.

-----

“Tell me it’s not three in the morning there,” Han-byeol said, before I’d even got the phone to my ear, and the sound of her went through me warm and aching at once — the one person I ever picked on purpose, eight thousand kilometres into a life with a real visa and a real program and a real future stacked up in front of her like a thing you’re allowed to have.

“It’s afternoon. It’s three in the morning there.”

“It’s gloriously three in the morning here and I have a hydrology exam at eight and I am calling you instead, which tells you how that’s going.” A rustle, a yawn. “Talk to me about anything that isn’t groundwater. How’s the licence. How’s the — wait.” A pause, and I felt it coming the way I feel Oh’s calls coming. “You sound weird. You sound like you slept. Are you sick?”

“I’m not sick.”

“You sound rested, Jae, I’ve known you six years, you have never once sounded rested, it’s unsettling, what’s happening over there.” I could hear her sit up against her pillows. “How’s the mystery roommate. The one whose name I’m not allowed to know because you signed before you met him like an absolute lunatic.”

“He pays on time.” Which is true, and the whole of what I meant to say. “He’s quiet. He fixed the table.”

The silence on the line was the specific silence of a best friend adding two and two.

“He fixed the table,” she repeated, slow, delighted and alarmed in equal measure. “The wobbly one. The one I propped with a coaster for two years. He just — fixed it. Unasked.”

“It’s not a thing.”

“It is the single most romantic sentence you have ever said to me and you said it like a weather report.” She laughed, and then, gentler, the laugh going careful the way only she’s ever allowed to go careful with me: “Hey. You okay? Actually. Not the report version.”

And I stood in my cramped kitchen with the good cabbage in the fridge and eight days on the clock and a man’s quiet through the wall, and for one second I nearly said it — no, I’m not, the numbers stopped working and I don’t know how to make them start — and then I did the thing I always do, which is set it down somewhere no one can reach it.

“I’m fine,” I said. “Go fail your hydrology.”

“I’m going to pass my hydrology out of spite and you’re going to pass your licence out of spite and we’re going to be two terrifyingly competent women on two continents,” she said. “Eat something. Bye. Love you. Fix the table, my God.” And she was gone, and the flat was very quiet, and the ache she left was the particular ache of being thought of — somebody, somewhere, up at three in the morning, thinking to ask the actual question — which is a thing I’ve had so little of that I don’t have a callus for it, and it got all the way in before I could stop it.

-----

I went back to the book. The book did not cooperate.

I tried the trick that’s never once failed me. I counted. The won in the envelope, the days to the exam, the shifts left if I took the warehouse, the shifts left if I didn’t, the cabbage I shouldn’t have let him buy now sitting in the fridge like a debt I couldn’t find the size of. I counted, and the counting that has carried me down into sleep every night of my adult life simply did not catch, the numbers sliding off each other, and underneath them, where the count is supposed to go, there was only the wall, and the careful quiet behind it, and the memory of a shoulder I’d put between a stranger and a stranger that morning for no reason I could name.

I gave up at something past one. I lay down in my clothes. The exam was eight days out. The man was a column that would not total. There was a good cabbage in the fridge I didn’t pay for, and a warehouse waiting two weeks deep in the dark, and a friend on another continent who’d heard rested in my voice and got frightened of it the same way I’m frightened of it.

And then, low, through six feet of bad plaster — the hum. The one I’ve never heard and cannot stop almost remembering, the tune with no name on it that finds the old wordless place in me every single time, the place shaped like being very small and warm and held. It came through the wall the way warmth comes under a door, and my whole body turned toward it before I’d voted on it, and I let it. Just for tonight. Just this once. Because I was too tired to build the wall against a man who was only humming.

I did not count myself to sleep.

I’ve stopped being able to — and somewhere past the fear of that, in a place I’ll deny in the morning, I’ve stopped entirely minding.
