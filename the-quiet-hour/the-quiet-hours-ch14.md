# Chapter Fourteen

### Rule Fourteen: Silver Can Wait

The door between our rooms was still open when I woke, which was how I knew the night before had happened and wasn’t a thing I’d built out of wanting it. I’m an unreliable narrator about exactly one subject, and that subject is her.

I didn’t go through it. I lay on my side of the gap in the grey light and listened to the other side wake up — the brass bolt thrown back, the tap coughing rust and then running clean, the kettle starting its slow climb. And under all of it, a sound that was new that week and that I’d started setting my chest by: the dry, fast turn of pages, and her voice barely above nothing, running a list to herself the way other people pray. Allowance for doubtful debts. Accruals. The matching principle. She’d been at it past midnight every night and she was at it again before the kettle had even boiled, because the exam was five days out and she was going to walk into it on no sleep and a trimmed grocery envelope and pass it anyway, the way she did everything — by refusing the world the satisfaction of watching her drop.

Five days. I lay in the cold and did the sum I kept not wanting to do, because I’m a man who counts in beats and even I could do this one. Her exam — the thing that locks a door no one can open from outside — was five days out. The fourteenth — my own photographed step onto a stage I couldn’t step back off — was the same five days out. The two clocks had been walking toward each other since before I knew her name, and they were going to land on the same wall in the same week, and only one of them was hers.

Through the gap her chair scraped. The pages turned. The matching principle: recognise the cost in the same period as the thing it bought. I let the irony of that sit on me a while. Then I got up before I could knock on a door I’d been given no right to walk through, and went to be made into something saleable.

-----

The SEORÉ atelier runs at full tilt by ten, which is its own kind of weather: three machines going in the back, a steamer breathing in long hisses, a radio playing something old and low that nobody’s listening to and everybody would miss if it stopped. Bolts of cloth stand in the corner like a small grey forest. Two assistants move through it with their mouths full of pins and their hands full of someone else’s wedding. And at the centre of it, on the platform where I’ve stood since I was seventeen, I got turned slowly in front of three mirrors by a woman who has never once let me fidget.

“Stop holding your breath. I can’t fit a held breath.” Director Ahn talked around two pins, her reading glasses down her nose, her hands going up the line of the jacket without looking. She’s small and exact, she fitted me for my first stage and my father for his last suit, and she has never been impressed by me for a single second, which is the most restful thing about her. “There. Drop the shoulders. You’ve worn them up by your ears since you walked in.” A pin went home. “Whose funeral.”

“Mine, apparently. On the fourteenth.”

“Mm.” She didn’t laugh, but the corner of her mouth moved, and she took the chalk from behind her ear and marked the seam. “It’s a good suit. Midnight, not black — black photographs flat, your mother’s office was very clear about that. They want you reading new chapter, not reading of the will.” She stepped back, looked at the hang, stepped in again. “Turn. The light’s a liar on this side.”

I turned. In the back one of the assistants laughed at something the other said, low, a private thing about a hem, and the radio played, and the steamer breathed, and for a moment the room was just a room with work in it — the oldest, plainest comfort there is, people making a thing well with their hands and not needing me to be anyone for them. The camera in me that never quite shuts off settled on that small forest of cloth and stayed.

“Arms.” Ahn ran the tape from my wrist to my spine, read it, frowned. “You’ve lost width. You’re not eating.” It wasn’t a question and I didn’t insult her with an answer. She made a note in the book she’s kept on my body for nine years — the one that knows me better than my mother’s office does — and moved to the trouser break, and the fitting went on in the language we’ve always used, which is half measurements and half nothing said.

Then the door at the front opened, and one of the assistants straightened her spine before she’d even turned to see who it was, and the temperature of the room dropped three degrees.

It wasn’t my mother. It was worse, in a way, because it was my mother’s reach without my mother in it: a young man in a good coat with a tablet and an earpiece, one of the office’s smooth ones, the kind who exist to deliver the machine’s wishes in a voice trained to sound like a request.

“Director Ahn.” A small bow, exactly correct. “He looks wonderful.” He didn’t look at me, standing right there on the platform. He looked at the suit. “Two things from the office, for the fourteenth. The first—” a glance at the tablet “—styling will need him in by seven that morning. And the second.” The smallest pause, the kind rehearsed to seem unrehearsed. “The hair. It needs to be back to silver by the thirteenth at the latest. The colour grows a stage presence; the office has the reference images. Tonight would be better than tomorrow.”

The room didn’t stop, exactly. The machines kept going. But Ahn’s hands went still on the seam, and one of the assistants found something very absorbing to do with a bolt of grey, and the smooth young man stood there in his good coat, having said the thing he was sent to say.

Be silver by the fourteenth. The stone that’s been in my shoe since the start of all this — the one sentence under all the others. I’d been black-haired for weeks by then, the dye gone in badly over a sink that isn’t mine, in a flat that isn’t on any map, a small daily act of disappearing that was the only thing in my life I’d chosen without anyone’s permission. The office wanted it gone. Of course it did. The black was the part of me the machine couldn’t sell, which was exactly why it was the part I’d kept.

“He’s silver when I say the suit’s ready,” Ahn said, not turning around, the chalk going back behind her ear. “Which is not today, because the shoulders aren’t right, and I don’t send a man out of my house half-made for anyone’s office. Tell them the thirteenth. Tell them I said so.”

The young man’s smile didn’t move. “Of course, Director. The thirteenth.” Another correct bow, and he was gone, the door easing shut behind him, and the three degrees came back into the room slowly, like heat into a hand.

Ahn picked the tape back up. She didn’t look at my face, which was a kindness, because she fitted my father and she knows what the men in this family look like when they’ve just been reminded what they are. “Arms,” she said again, gently this time. I lifted them. I let her measure the width I’d lost, and said nothing, and neither did she, and the radio played us through it.

-----

Hyun was waiting with the car in the service lane, engine running against the cold, holding the particular stillness that means he’s been deciding whether to say a thing for the whole hour he’s been parked.

“Eat first.” He handed back a paper bag, warm at the bottom. “Then look. Not the other way round. I know you.”

I ate the gimbap, because refusing it would hurt a man who has only ever loved me by feeding me. Then I looked. His phone, turned to face me: a screenshot of an account with no name and a number for a handle, three posts deep, all of them circling the same drain without ever quite saying it. Some weddings are mergers and some mergers are funerals. Ask the bride who she’s really been crying over. And a photograph, half-blurred, of a café table I recognised, because I’d been sitting at it — the Sohwa, the private room, the back of a glossy expensive head that was unmistakably Seo Yuna’s, posted by someone who was not supposed to know that meeting had happened at all.

A man who takes it loudly, and in writing. Yuna’s voice, dry over a turning teacup, the one warning she’d bothered to give me. If you ever read something somewhere you weren’t expecting to.

“It’s small,” Hyun said, watching my face. “Three hundred followers. Nothing’s picked it up. The office monitoring flagged it an hour ago and they’re already drafting the takedown.” He pocketed the phone. “But somebody photographed a room that was sealed, Do-yun. Somebody inside the families is angry enough to start writing it down. That’s not a fan with a theory. That’s a person with a key.”

“I know who it is.” I didn’t say more than that. It wasn’t mine to spend. “She’s handling it.”

“She’d better handle it before the fourteenth.” Hyun pulled out of the lane, the wipers waking against a thin rain that had started without either of us noticing. “Because your mother’s office is very good at making one angry man disappear. And the way they do it is they tear up everything he might have touched, looking for the leak. Every room. Every contact. Every name near the centre of it.” His eyes stayed on the road, both hands at the cautious ten-and-two he only uses when he’s afraid. “Anything near the fourteenth that isn’t supposed to be there goes. You taught me that phrase. You didn’t make it up.”

The rain came down. The city slid by grey, and the song started up in me without permission, the count going into my folded hand against my thigh — four and four and four, the wet folded through it — and it ran, the way it only runs when I’m pointed at her, all the way to the edge it freezes at. And tonight it didn’t go past. Tonight it stopped dead at the seam, the way it always used to, because there was a man with a key starting to pull threads, and my mother’s office about to start pulling harder, and a flat in Mapo that is exactly the kind of thread they pull.

-----

I didn’t go straight in. I stood in the stairwell of the dead laundromat a while, in the cold, with my black hair and my half-made suit folded over my arm, and let myself be, for ten more minutes, a man who lives here.

When I came up, the flat was dark except for the lamp at her low table, and she was asleep over the open book the way she’d sworn she wouldn’t be — chin on her folded arms, the page she’d have to read again tomorrow gone soft under her cheek, the kettle long cold. Five days to her door that locks. Five days to mine that won’t.

I should have re-silvered the hair that night. The office said tonight. My mother’s reach said the thirteenth and means tonight; it always means sooner than it says. I went into the wet little bathroom and looked at the man in the spotted mirror — black hair, the colour gone in over this exact sink, the only thing I own. There’s no box on a shelf for what they want back. You don’t lift drugstore black to that pale ashy silver over your own basin; it’s a chair at the maison, a colourist’s whole afternoon, the strip that reeks like a swimming pool — and a paper trail with my name on every line of it. Ahn’s people had the slot held for the morning of, sitting in my phone like a verdict, and all it would take to set it running was the one word I always send.

I didn’t send it.

Five more days, I told myself, and I meant it as a stay of execution and knew it for what it actually was, which is the most dangerous kind of wanting — the kind that starts keeping things.

I took the folded stack out of the jacket before I turned off the light, because I do this some nights, the way other men count money or pray. I unfolded three years of her across the closed lid of the tank and looked at it. Napkins gone soft at the creases. Receipts with the ink run to ghosts. A convenience-store slip, a market slip, the back of my mother’s letterhead, the corner of a call sheet, two bars in ballpoint that had transferred faintly to the inside of my own wrist over a week of being carried. Laid edge to edge they almost made a thing — I could see the shape of it by then, the verses, the turn, the way the chorus opens its hand — almost a whole song, except for the place it always stops. The bridge running up to a note and then nothing. White space. The one bar I have never once been able to write, because it ends on a note I can’t reach on my own. Three years of scraps and the ending wasn’t on any of them. The ending was asleep in the next room over an accounting textbook, five feet away, breathing.

I folded it all back together and put it away. Turned off the light. In the other room she breathed, slow, on the same side of the wall as me, five days from a door that locks, and I stood in the dark of a flat that isn’t on any map and let the song stay frozen at its seam — because finishing it and silvering the hair are the same surrender, and I was not, that night, going to make it.

I left the door between our rooms open again. It’s the only thing I can give her that costs the machine nothing — and everything, if anyone ever counts it.
