Rule Forty-Four: The Sick Singer Buys His Own Vegetables

I told Hyun I was sleeping.

Then I pulled the cap down and the mask up and walked out into a Saturday that had no idea I existed. The market was already roaring — fish slapping onto ice, a knife-sharpener whining somewhere, two ajummas waging a thirty-year war over the price of garlic I hoped would never end. Nobody turned. Not one head. No manager three steps back, no schedule, no detail treating the scallion stall like a hostage negotiation. Just me and a crowd and not one person in it looking for me.

I could have stayed there till the fish ran out.

She’d been at the table all morning with her books and the bad pen, doing the thing where the numbers stop being kind to her. So: dinner. I’d handle dinner. I’d stood a half-step behind her left shoulder a dozen times and watched her work this exact crowd like a debt collector with a heart of gold. How hard could it be.

“Eight hundred,” I told Pak — fishmonger, built like a refrigerator that has opinions — with what I believed was bored, married authority.

Pak did not look up. “Where’s your wife.”

“She sent me.”

“She did not send you. She’d never let you out alone.” He wrapped a fish I hadn’t agreed to buy. “A thousand. And the corner-stall line is dead before you reach for it — you used it last week and the whole lane heard. Pak, he says, the corner stall has it cheaper. Son. I am the corner stall.”

So much for married authority. I shut up. Looked at him properly this time — the good fish kept at the back, the slow morning, the ice already sweating.

“That’s a beautiful fish,” I said, and meant it. “It’s also your fourth-best, and we both know it, because you keep the good ones at the back where the regulars can watch you save them. Eight hundred for one off the back, and I’ll tell every soul on this lane that Pak guts a fish like a surgeon.”

Pak looked at me a long, flat second. Then he barked a laugh that turned three heads, reached behind him, and put the good fish in my hand. “Eight hundred,” he said. “And you tell them faster than a surgeon.” He gutted it in front of me to prove it — quick, clean, openly showing off — and I told the next two stalls exactly that, and a man at the dried-goods table pressed a free handful of anchovies on me for the publicity.

At the radishes, the woman’s phone was going — the news in its soft, sad, important voice — and a woman buying scallions passed the story over the bins the way you hand someone something warm: that singer, still off, weeks now, the company saying rest, the comeback pushed back again, poor thing, you wonder what they did to him.

I turned and became extremely, scientifically interested in a daikon.

Three feet away, two women held a candlelight vigil for my health. I stood over the produce with eight hundred won of victory in my pocket and a fish that respected me, and I bit down on the inside of the mask so I wouldn’t laugh.

“The firm ones,” the radish woman said, watching me dither. “For soup, the firm ones.” She bagged them and gave me a price under the sign I hadn’t reached for. She’d watched the whole Pak business from two stalls down. “You did all right over there. He doesn’t put the good fish out for just anyone.” She pushed an extra radish into the bag, the way you do for someone you’ve decided to keep. “Tell your wife the boy can be let out after all.”

I carried it home, fish in one hand, a stranger’s anchovies in the other, and somewhere around the second corner I caught myself humming — four counts of something, over and over, nobody’s but mine. No manager. No schedule. No face.

I texted Hyun from the stairs. Still sleeping.

Then I went up to feed her.
