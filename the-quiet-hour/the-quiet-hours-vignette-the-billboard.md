Rule Fifty-Two: Do Not Let Her Look Up

She made me come to the stationery shop because the exam wanted one specific index card and the world contained, in her estimation, exactly one acceptable version of it.

“You don’t need me for this,” I’d said.

“I need someone to hold the basket.”

That was the whole negotiation. That was the contract. I’d have carried that basket into the sea.

Cap down, mask up, the box-black doing its tired job — patchy at the part, going grey at the crown where I can’t reach with a drugstore bottle and a bathroom mirror. She walked me down the main road reciting card weights like I’d be tested on them at the till, and I was happy, which on a public street is the same as careless. I’d stopped watching the corners.

So I didn’t see it until we were nearly under it.

The bus shelter on the corner. The long lit panel down its flank. And me — the other me, the silver one, the jaw they sharpen past anything God shipped, looking off down the street at nothing with the face that’s supposed to make you want a watch you can’t afford. SEUNGWOO, in letters taller than she is.

There’s no training for the morning you walk your roommate straight into your own face. I had four steps and no plan, so I did the dumbest thing on the menu: I got bigger. Stepped wide. Put my shoulders in her eyeline. Became, with no warning, a man profoundly interested in the far wall of the shelter, herding her toward the curb by sheer mass while my mouth ran without a driver — the weather, the cards, the price of cards, the effect of weather on the price of cards —

She stopped.

She looked at me the way she looks at a receipt that won’t total. “What are you doing.”

“Walking.”

“You’re walking like a door.”

“I’m a big fan of the curb.”

“You’re a big fan of standing between me and a bus shelter.”

And then she looked past me — because the one guaranteed way to make Jae-wu look at a thing is to put yourself in front of it — up and over my shoulder, straight at him. At me. Silver and enormous and four sizes too handsome, selling time.

I watched her eyes go up to my face and come back with nothing.

A flick. A verdict. The small flat thing her mouth does at advertising in general. “Imagine paying a man that much to stand still,” she said, and looked back down at her list, and that was the whole event. A stranger on a poster. Some silver nobody being paid to hold a pose.

The dye held. The cap held. The mask held, and not one of them was the thing that saved me. She’d never once gone hunting in my face for somebody else, because I’d given her no reason to think there was a somebody else parked in here to find — and that, it turns out, is a better disguise than the hair. You can strip and re-tone hair in an afternoon. You can’t buy what she was doing, which was simply taking me at the size I’d handed her.

The shelter was right there. The name was right there, taller than both of us. Four words would have done it. The sentence stood up in my mouth, fully built, ready —

“They overcharge for cardstock here,” I said.

“It’s the only good cardstock.”

“It’s identical cardstock.”

“It isn’t, and you can’t tell, which is precisely why I needed someone to hold the basket and not contribute theories.”

We left the corner. I didn’t look back at myself.

She glanced at me twice on the way to the shop, filing something — subject walks like a door near bus shelters, probably, going into whatever ledger she keeps on me — and I let her file it, because a man who walks funny is a far smaller problem than a man with a name on a wall, and I’ll take being the smaller problem for as long as she lets me hold it.

She bought the index cards. I held the basket. Behind us the silver one kept looking down the street at nothing, selling a thing no one on that corner would buy, and down on the pavement I matched her step and carried the bag and said nothing true at all, and she let me walk her home like I was no one she’d ever have to worry about.

The sentence was still in my mouth when she got the door open. I put it where I put all of them, and I went and made the tea.
