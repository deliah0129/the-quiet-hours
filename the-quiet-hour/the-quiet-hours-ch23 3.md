# Chapter Twenty-Three

### Rule Twenty-Three: Leverage Is Just a Debt You Haven't Called In Yet

The band told me not to go back to the flat, and they were right, and I went back to the flat.

Yejun said it cleanest, in the practice room, in the voice he uses for things that are true whether or not you like them: a man walking through that door is the thing that turns a voice into a name. The window was already matched. There were already people on my street holding their phones against buildings like dowsing rods. Going home was the single most expensive thing I could do, in a day that had already cost more than I'd ever held.

So obviously I went home. I have spent fifteen years doing the expensive thing exactly once — never — and I picked the worst possible afternoon to start, and I'd do it again, because the equation had stopped having anything to do with cost the moment Reo turned his phone face-down, careful as a man setting down something live, and told me his grandmother had posted that the accountant left in the middle of the books. White as a sheet. Looked like she'd seen a ghost.

I knew which ghost. I'd been the ghost for two months. The difference was that this morning the ghost had a voice now, and a window, and four hundred thousand reposts, and she'd been sitting in a warm kitchen she trusted doing the books for a family that loved her when my voice came out of someone's phone across the room and rearranged her entire understanding of the man behind her wall. She hadn't learned it from me, gently, over coffee, in the right order, like a person. She'd learned it the way you learn the floor is gone — all at once, with your weight already on it.

I went home to be there when she got back. That was the whole plan. Stupid and total. Be in the window so the first thing she saw was my face and not a stranger's, take the four words I'd been choking on for two months and finally, finally spend them.

She didn't come back.

I want to be accurate about the flat, because I've performed grief professionally and I know the difference between the version you do for a camera and the real one, and the real one is mostly just inventory. Her shoes were gone from the mat — the one pair, the sensible ones, the ones she stood in to fight the espresso machine. Her coat was gone off the hook. The room behind the wall was the kind of quiet that isn't waiting for anything, the quiet of a column already totaled and closed. I knocked. One knuckle, low, the habit, the best moment of my engineered life. I waited the way I've waited sixty mornings.

Nothing came back, because there was no one there to not-answer, and that turns out to be a different silence entirely. I stood at my own window over the airshaft, in the dark, looking at the dark square of hers, and I understood that somewhere out in a city of ten million she was awake and moving and deliberately, with that terrible competence of hers, putting distance between herself and this exact window, and that the worst thing I could do — the thing every instinct I owned was screaming at me to do — was chase her, because a man like me chasing a woman through Seoul is not a love story, it's a location pin for everyone with a long lens. I had finally, on the only night it mattered, run out of moves that didn't cost her something.

The fear I'd had since the night I first let myself want the two months — the what-if-she's-just-gone fear, the one I'd kept in a drawer because naming it felt like inviting it — it wasn't a fear anymore. Fears are about the future. This was just the weather now. She was gone, and I was standing in the place she'd left, holding four words and a window full of nothing.

Then my phone lit, because the machine does not observe grief, the machine considers grief a scheduling conflict, and it was the label, and it was Noh himself, and I answered it standing in the dark, which I think is the only reason I won.

—

"There he is." Noh has a voice like a good loan — warm right up until the terms. "The most-watched man in the country. Do-yun-ah. How are you holding up, you must be reeling, it's a lot, a lot of love coming at you all at once."

"What do you want, Director."

"Want. Such a word." I could hear him settling in, the creak of a chair built for settling in. "I want what I always want, which is for you to be taken care of. And I have been up all night being creative on your behalf, because the situation — and I say this with affection — the situation is a gift somebody dropped on the floor, and I would hate to see it swept up. You understand the engagement is dead, yes? Tonight's announcement. A man this raw, this wanted, this single in the public heart — you cannot sell that boy a merger. The numbers won't have it. So I've taken the liberty. I've already made some calls."

"You called my mother's people. To kill her merger. For you."

"I prefer to think of it as offering a better deal to a motivated seller." A smile in it. "Five years. We finish the song — your song, beautiful, I cried, genuinely — we build the whole comeback around the broken boy who found his voice, sad solo era, sold-out tour, the country eats it with a spoon. You stay single, you stay ours, you stay the most valuable unfinished thing in Korea, and in exchange you never have to marry a stranger you don't love. I'm rescuing you, Do-yun-ah. I'm the only one in this whole circus who's actually on your side."

And here's the thing. Eight weeks ago — a year ago, a week ago — I'd have heard rescuing you and felt the relief, because anyone offering to decide my life for me in a kinder direction has always felt like mercy when the alternative is the same decision made crueler. I'd have said yes. The product says yes. Yes is the only word a thing for sale is allowed.

But I'd spent two months living next to a woman who would have heard better deal to a motivated seller and known, instantly, cold, the way she knows a cooked book, that the seller was me, and the deal was a cage with nicer bars, and the man offering it had run the same sum on me that everyone runs — what is he worth turned into a product versus left as a person — and arrived where they all arrive.

"No," I said.

A pause. Noh does not get told no; it's not in his weather. "I don't think you heard the—"

"I heard all of it. You want to own the ending of the song. That's the whole call, the rest is upholstery. And here's the part you did your sums without: there is no ending. It stops on a cliff. It's stopped on that cliff for three years, and the entire country is currently in love with a thing that doesn't finish, and the only person alive who can finish it is standing in the dark in a pair of socks telling you no. You can't build a comeback on a song you can't end, Director. You can't sell the country a spoon and no soup. You need me to write the last line, and I'm not writing it for you, and I'm not writing it for my mother, and I'm certainly not writing it in five years of being your sad broken boy on a schedule."

"Do-yun." The warmth gone now; this was the real voice, the one under the loan. "You are speaking to me like a man who thinks he has a hand to play."

"I'm speaking to you like a man who finally noticed he's holding the only card on the table." I was, I realized, smiling — not the trained one, a real one, ugly and surprised, the smile of a horse discovering the gate was never locked. "A clip is weather, Director. You taught me that, sort of, somebody did this morning. Loud, then gone. What turns weather into a career is who owns the sky — the broadcast, the shows, the screens in the taxis. You don't own the sky. You rent it, same as me, from the three companies that do. So you can blacklist me, sure, and I'll be a tragedy for a week, and then I'll be a trivia question, and you'll have spent your one viral miracle on a song with no ending and a boy who walked. Or. You let tonight's announcement wobble. You stop calling my mother's people. You stop deciding which version of me prints money this quarter. And maybe — maybe — when this is over, when I've gotten my own life back in my own two hands, I finish the song, and when I do, I remember who let me breathe and who tried to put me on a five-year drip."

Silence on the line. The good kind, for once. The kind that means a player is recalculating.

"You've changed," Noh said at last, and it wasn't a compliment, it was a man revising a spreadsheet.

"Yeah," I said. "I got a roommate."

I hung up first. I have never in my life hung up first. It felt like the top of a roller coaster, that lurch where the thing you were dreading reveals itself as the thing you were built for, and I stood in the dark flat with my heart going and thought: that's it. That's the move. That's the door out of the machine, I can see the shape of it now, I just have to live long enough to walk through it and bring her with me—

— and then I remembered she was gone, and the roller coaster put me down hard on the part of the track where none of it mattered, because I'd just played the best hand of my life in an empty room, for an audience of one woman who would never know I'd done it, and who, even if she knew, would be right not to care.

—

Hyun came at two in the morning with a bag of something from a convenience store and the face of a man who had been holding a building up with his back.

I made him sit. I made him eat the thing he'd brought for me — held it out, didn't ask, just put it in his hands, the way Halmeoni does, the way Jae does, I've apparently been raised by three people now and only met one of them on purpose. He looked at the food like it was an accusation. Then he ate it, fast, badly, the way you eat when you've forgotten you have a body, and somewhere in the middle of it the building came off his back for a second and his eyes went bright and he had to look at the wall.

"I've got it handled," he said, with his mouth full, lying.

"You don't have it handled. Nobody has it handled. That's allowed."

"Your mother's calling me forty times a day. The merger's bleeding — not dead, she's still got the body propped up, she'll prop it for weeks out of pure will. The label's gone quiet, which from Noh is more frightening than loud. There's a girl from your fiancée's circle whose boyfriend keeps threatening to blow something up in the press, I don't even have the bandwidth to find out what, and Yuna—" he stopped. Something crossed his face when he said her name and he shoved it back down with another bite. "Yuna's having a worse night than anyone and nobody's allowed to say so because officially she's the happy bride of a happy announcement. The whole thing's a house of cards in a wind tunnel and I'm running around the bottom with my arms out and Do-yun, I'm thirty-eight, I never debuted, I have done nothing with my one life except hold up yours, and tonight I cannot tell if I'm a brother or a sandbag."

"Brother," I said. "You've always been the brother. The sandbag is a thing the brother does. Eat the rest of that."

He ate the rest of that. We sat in my dark famous flat next to the wall with no one behind it, two grown men and a plastic bag, and for a minute the machine could go to hell.

His phone buzzed. He looked at it and went carefully blank, which is the Hyun equivalent of a flare going up.

"Yuna," he said. "Four a.m. She does this. Can't sleep before an announcement, texts me logistics she could send anyone." He didn't put the phone down. He read it twice. Whatever it said, it wasn't logistics; logistics doesn't make a man's thumb hover like that. "She says — she says congratulations, we're both officially the loneliest engaged people in Asia, and do I think it's funny or sad that the two of us texting at four in the morning is the realest relationship either of us is going to be allowed to have." He huffed something that wanted to be a laugh and came out a different shape. He typed nothing. He put the phone face-down. "It's funny," he told the table, in the voice of a man for whom it was the other one.

I didn't say anything, because some gates you leave where the other person set them. I'd learned that from a wall. But I filed it — the brother and the bride, both shackled to the same announcement, both leaking something true at four in the morning to the one person who'd understand, and neither of them allowed to call it what it was. The machine doesn't just sell the people in the photo. It sells everyone holding the photo up.

—

Hyun left near five to go hold up the next part of the building. I should have slept. Instead I did the math one more time, the way she does it, cold and complete and arriving whether you want it or not, and the math came out simple and unsurvivable: I had finally become a man worth coming home to — fought the fight, locked the leverage, found the door out — and I had no way on earth to tell the one person it was all for, because somewhere between the empty mat and the dark window I'd understood the last and stupidest thing.

I didn't have her number.

Two months. Two months wall-to-wall, sixty mornings of one knuckle on the plaster, and I had never once needed to text her, because she was right there, four inches of cheap drywall away, and a knock had always been enough. I'd built an entire secret relationship on the one channel I could never use again. The wall was the only line I'd ever had to her, and she wasn't behind it, and I stood there with a phone that could reach four hundred thousand strangers and not the one woman in the world, holding the words I'd been choking on since the first morning she handed me back a mug and didn't say thank you because she didn't think she was owed one.

Not I'm an idol. Not I'm getting married. Those were facts, and you can survive being lied to about facts; she'd told me so herself, once, about a man named Ji-hwan, you close the book and delete the number. The true thing underneath was the one I'd never have to apologize for getting wrong, because it was the only honest line I'd had the whole time and the one thing I never said.

I should have told you.

Reo had spent the whole morning swearing it was four words. It's four words, Do-yun, it's not complicated — four words and you're free. He'd meant the facts: the band, the name, the thing the country already knew. And they were all wrong, because I finally had the real sentence in my mouth and I counted it the way I count everything since I met her, helpless, like a cold I caught off her — and it came out five. Five words, not four. The hard one always runs one longer than the version that lets you off. I count in beats, not words; I never get the words right. She's the one who counts. She'd have looked up from her ledger and said that's five before I finished saying it, and been right, the way she's right about every number in any room she's ever stood in. That was the whole sum of two months, in the end — the one person who could have caught my arithmetic was the one I'd been doing the arithmetic about, and I waited until she was gone to total it. Five words. I'd had them, near enough, for two months. I had them now, finally, fully formed, ready, and a window full of nothing to say them to, and no number to send them to, and a city between us that knew my voice and not one true thing about either of us.

I turned off the lights so the window would stop being a stage. I sat down on the floor with my back to her wall, the way I'd sat the night I sang the quiet part out loud, and I did not sing. There was nothing left of the song that wasn't already public, and the only line that mattered was one I still couldn't write, because the end of it had walked out in sensible shoes and taken the meaning with her.

Somewhere across the city, I'd find out much later, she was lying on a heated floor among strangers, giving back a lock that was never hers, not letting herself wonder if I was still in the window.

I was. For what it's worth. I was in the window the whole night.

It wasn't worth anything. That was the lesson of the fourteenth. You can hold all the leverage in the country and not have the one thing — a number, a door, the words said in time — that would let you spend it on the only account that ever mattered.
