Rule Ten: Keep It Off Your Face in Your Mother’s Weather

Do-yun (“Doha”)


There was rice on the shared shelf when I woke, in the cheap white pot, still faintly warm.

I stood in the cold kitchen in yesterday’s shirt and looked at it for longer than a pot of rice can possibly deserve. She’d made it before her eyes shut — I’d heard the cooker play its small finished tune through the wall somewhere past one, while she thought I was asleep — and she’d left it where I’d find it, on the one shelf we share, and she didn’t knock, and she didn’t say, and there was no note, because a note would make it a thing said and she does not say things. Just rice, made right, first knuckle, left for a man who can’t make his own.

She fed me without being in the room to be thanked for it. I’ve been fed my whole life — nutritionists, managers with macros on a clipboard, a mother who counted my meals like everything else she owned. No one ever just put food where I’d find it and went to sleep so I wouldn’t have to perform the gratitude. I got a bowl down, filled it, ate it standing at the counter the way she eats, and it was the best thing I’d tasted in a year, and the rice had nothing to do with it.

The phone, off in my pocket since the rain two nights before, had to come back on eventually. I gave myself the whole bowl first.

When I thumbed it alive it shuddered for a full ten seconds, two days of the world arriving at once. I let the official ones stack unread and went straight to the only thread that isn’t an instruction — the group, talking to itself in the dark the way it does, four men dropping flares for whoever surfaces next.

Reo: the tubs in the left fridge are LABELLED and DATED. do not touch the unlabelled ones. those are an experiment.
Sun: reo is doing science to the kimchi again
Yejun: It’s not science. It’s neglect with a hypothesis.
Reo: yejun that is the meanest thing anyone has ever said and it’s going in the song
Reo: real talk though, eomma got another tax letter. the pink kind. she keeps telling me it’s handled. it is not handled. i sent her money for “ingredients” again. she knows it’s not for ingredients. we both pretend.
Sun: reo.
Reo: it’s fine. i’m fine. the restaurant is a hole i throw money into and call a childhood.
Tae: hyung, if you’re reading this: water. then text one of us a single letter so we know your thumbs work.

I looked at it a while, the warmth of it and the ache of it the same temperature, and typed one letter — k — into the box, and watched three dots come up under it instantly from Sun, who was supposed to be asleep, and felt, for the length of one breath, held by four people across a city who’d asked me for exactly nothing today but proof that my thumbs work.

I read Reo’s lines again after the others had scrolled past them. The pink kind. It is not handled. I filed it somewhere that wasn’t quite conscious yet, the way a melody files itself before you know you’ll use it. Then the official flood remembered me, and the one at the bottom had teeth.

-----

It was from Hyun, and it wasn’t a summons to a fitting. Call me before you do anything today. It’s not the suit this time.

He picked up on the first ring, which told me everything before a word.

“They’ve moved the meet up,” he said. No good morning; there’s no morning good enough to cushion it. “You and the Seo girl. Today. A private room at the Sohwa, three o’clock, both mothers in the building but not in the room — they want you two photographed looking acquainted on the fourteenth, and acquainted takes rehearsal, apparently.” A breath he didn’t bother hiding. “It’s a week out, Do-yun. Seven days. I can’t move this one an inch; I tried at six this morning and your mother’s office had already lapped me.”

“I’ll be there.”

“You’ll be silver there, is the note that came down. Wear what Ahn sent.” A pause, and the manager went under and the brother came up. “It’s an hour. You sit, you’re polite, you let them get their picture of two reasonable young people, and you come home. That’s all today is. Don’t make it more than it is in your head on the way over — you do that, I can hear you doing it now.”

“I’m not doing anything in my head.”

“You’re doing it in your head,” he said, almost gently, and hung up.

-----

The Sohwa keeps a floor for people who pay not to be seen on it.

The private room was all warm wood and a wall of cold grey afternoon over the river, a table laid for two with a service nobody would eat, and a woman already in it when I came in, standing at the window with her back to the door — which is its own small statement, I thought, a person who’d rather watch the river than watch the door open on her future.

She turned. Seo Yuna was exactly as composed as the photographs my mother’s office had sent: glossy, expensive, a face arranged for rooms like this one. The only thing the photographs missed was the look in it, which was not nerves and was not hope. It was the flat, level boredom of someone serving a sentence and refusing to pretend it’s a holiday.

“You’re the idol,” she said. Not unkind. Just establishing the file.

“I’m told I’m the heir, this week.”

“Mm. They do keep changing what we are.” She sat, and waved me to the other chair the way you’d wave at a colleague, no ceremony in it. “Sit. They’ve got someone outside who’ll come in at half past to make sure we’re getting along, so we should at least be sitting down when she does.”

I sat. A girl came and poured two teas neither of us had asked for and left. Out on the river a barge went by, slow, indifferent to both our families.

“So,” Yuna said, turning her cup without drinking it. “Here’s mine, and then you can decide how much of yours to tell me, and we’ll get through the hour.” Briskly, a woman reading terms. “My family doesn’t need your family’s money. We have our own; that’s the point of the merger — two of a thing make a wall nobody climbs. I gain nothing from this but a surname I don’t want and a wedding I’ll have to smile through. I’m doing it because my father asked me to in the specific voice he uses twice a decade, and because saying no to that voice costs more than I have.” She finally looked at me directly. “I assume yours is some version of the same. You don’t strike me as a man marrying for the joy of it.”

“No,” I said.

“No,” she agreed, satisfied, a thing settling into its slot. “Good. Then we understand each other, and we can stop performing for each other at least, since we’ll be performing for everyone else for the rest of our natural lives.” She picked up the tea now and drank it. “I find it’s easier if we’re honest in the rooms where no one’s watching. We’re going to be in a great many rooms together. I’d rather not spend all of them acting.”

It was the most restful thing anyone outside a thin-walled flat had said to me in months — honest in the rooms where no one’s watching — and the thing in my chest that braces for every meeting came down a notch, because she wasn’t a rival, and she wasn’t a prize, and she wasn’t, blessedly, a woman who wanted anything from me. Just another person the machine had picked up and set down where it needed her.

“All right,” I said. “Honest. What do you actually want.”

Something flickered across the composure — there and gone, the first real thing in her face. Her thumb went to her phone, screen-down on the wood beside her cup, and stopped just short of it, the way a person reaches for a thing they’ve trained themselves not to reach for in company.

“Not this,” she said. “I want the version of my life where my father asks me something else.” She turned the cup. “There’s someone. Obviously there’s someone; there’s always someone, that’s why these things have to be arranged, because left alone people go and want the wrong person entirely.” A short, dry breath that wasn’t quite a laugh. “He doesn’t take it well. The arrangement. He takes it — loudly, and in writing, and at hours when a sensible man would be asleep. So if you ever read something somewhere you weren’t expecting to read it, that’ll be him, and I’ll handle it, and I’d take it as a kindness if you didn’t mention it to anyone whose job is making problems disappear.”

A man who takes it loudly and in writing. I filed it where I file the things that can go wrong — the same drawer that holds my mother’s voice over the scar in the fitting room — because a jealous man with a grievance and a phone is exactly the kind of small loose thing that brings a whole carefully-built wall down on everyone standing near it. On her. On the families. On a flat in Mapo that isn’t on anyone’s map.

“I won’t mention it,” I said. “And — for what it’s worth. I’m sorry about the voice your father uses twice a decade.”

She looked at me then, properly, reassessing, the boredom lifting off her face for the length of the look. “Huh,” she said. “You meant that.” She set the cup down. “You’re not what the file said, either.”

She studied me a moment longer, and I watched her decide something, the way she’d decide whether a dish at a table was worth the calories.

“You’ve got one too,” she said. Not a question. “Someone. You’ve had your eyes on the door since you sat down, like there’s somewhere you’d rather the next hour put you, and you keep checking a phone that hasn’t lit once.” She tipped her head, almost kind. “I’m not going to ask. I’m the last person on earth with standing to ask. I’m only telling you I can see it from here, across a table, in an hour I didn’t want — so you should think hard about who else can see it, and whether any of them love you less than I’m about to.”

It landed cold and clean as river air. Because she was right, and because she was the gentle version of being read — Seo Yuna had no reason to do anything with it but pour herself another tea — and the next person to clock the same thing off my face would be a woman who reads me like cloth for a living, who has already promised, with her thumb on a scar, to make the problem disappear before she asks my opinion of it.

“There’s nothing to see,” I said, level, the booth-voice, the one that’s closed a thousand questions.

“Mm.” She let it stand, the way Tae lets fine stand, the way my mother never will. “Then there’s nothing to see.” A quarter-turn of the cup. “But if there ever is — keep it off your face in your mother’s weather. I’ve watched her work a room. She finds the loose thread and she pulls it, and she’s smiling the whole time, and she thinks she’s doing you a favour.” A small, dry lift at the corner of her mouth. “Takes one to know one. My father’s the same animal in a better suit.”

It was, I thought, the single most useful thing anyone had said to me about my own life in a year, and it came from the woman the families had picked to be my wife, in the hour designed to make us strangers who photograph well.

-----

The handler came in at half past, brightly, to find two young people sitting at a laid table talking quietly over cooling tea — precisely the photograph everyone upstairs wanted — and she was so pleased by it she forgot to actually listen to what we were saying, which by then was Yuna, deadpan:

“Did the card come with a lanyard.”

“It did.”

“Of course it did.”

— and the two of us very nearly, not quite but nearly, laughing. Two prisoners comparing the same cell.

It was the easiest hour I’d spent in a room with my clothes on in a year, and the ease of it was its own kind of grief, because I understood, walking out past the mother who is and the mother who will be — the two of them murmuring in the corridor with the warm efficiency of people dividing up a country — that I could do this. That this was survivable. That I could marry this dry, trapped, decent woman and be politely unhappy in a series of beautiful rooms for the rest of my life and no one would ever once be hurt by it except the two people inside the marriage, and the machine does not count those.

I could do it. That’s the trap with the real teeth. Not that it’s unbearable. That it isn’t.

My mother caught my eye across the corridor — a single look, reading me head to foot the way she reads cloth, checking the hang of me, snagging a half-second on the black hair that should be silver — found me passable, and turned back to the other mother. That look is the whole of what she’ll ask of me for the rest of my life: be silver, be saleable, be where I put you, and want nothing I didn’t choose for you. I gave her the blank face she trained, and walked to the lift, and my fingers were going against my thigh before the doors closed, four and four and four, and I didn’t make them stop.

-----

I got out where Hyun’s note said to get out, two blocks short, and walked the rest in the failing light, hood up, the city not looking at me.

The flat was dark when I climbed the four flights — no lift, my calves know the count now without me — and on the other side of the wall there was no sound, which meant she’d gone to the night shift, the warehouse or the diner or whatever the call from the man at the market was steering her toward. The flat without her in it is just a cold flat. I’ve learned the difference. I learned it the night I turned my phone off and walked home in the rain terrified she’d be out, and heard her kettle through the wall and nearly went down on the stairs from the relief.

I put the kettle on — hers; I’ll buy my own, I keep saying — and stood in the kitchen where she’d fed me that morning, and let the day sit on me.

I could marry Seo Yuna. I kept turning it over and it kept coming up the same: survivable. A dry alliance, two honest prisoners, a wall nobody climbs. My mother’s whole life made safe. The merger that is her citizenship, stamped. And not one single thing in it that does what a pot of rice left on a shelf by a sleeping woman did to me at six o’clock that morning.

I made myself stop there, because the next thought had my mother’s voice in it — the day anything comes near the fourteenth, it goes, the way the rest went — and I know exactly what my world does to a woman who gets too close to me.

I’ve watched it done. A problem turned into a person, a person turned into a payment, a name scrubbed off a list and folded out of a night so smooth she thanks you on the way to the door. I never even got that girl’s face. And the thought of that machine turning, just once, just slightly, toward the woman four flights up who feeds me rice and goes to sleep so I won’t have to perform the thanks — it put me on the edge of something I had no business standing near, in a cold kitchen, over someone else’s kettle.

The kettle climbed toward its boil. The song came up under my ribs without permission — the rain-bars, and after them the two that only ever come when I’m pointed at her — and that night, with her side of the wall silent and the bowl she’d fed me from drying in the rack, it ran further than it had run in three years, all the way up to the edge of an ending I could finally almost hear.

And stopped. A step short. At the exact place she is, and I am not allowed to go.

I turned the kettle off before it screamed. I got the bars down first, though — the reflex runs ahead of the dread, always — the back of an envelope from the official stack, the one with my mother’s office as the return address, turned over and filled with a quick crooked stave and the run of notes that ran further that night than they have in three years. And a line came with them, and my hand wrote it before my judgment could stop it, because it was the truest thing the merger had taught me that day: every song I made, they owned it before it was dry. I stared at it on the back of my mother’s letterhead, the irony of the paper, then folded it notes-in and slid it into the pocket with the rest, the words hidden on the inside of the fold. The bars I kept. The bitterness I kept too, this once, because it isn’t about her — it’s the one true thing I’m allowed to write down plainly, since no one who finds it could use it to hurt anyone but me.

I stood in the dark with the unfinished song and the survivable marriage and seven days, and for the first time I let myself hold the question I’d been refusing to pick up since the fitting room — the heavy one, the sharp one, the one my mother warned me not to lift only to prove I could.

Is there a version where I don’t hand them the yes.

I didn’t have an answer. I’ve never once in my life had to find one. But I held the question, in two cupped hands, the way I held the silence the night the phone went off — like the only thing I own that’s mine — and through the wall, far off, a key turned in the building’s front door four floors down, and a tired tread started up the stairs, and the flat began, by degrees, to stop being cold.

I set a second cup out on the shelf beside hers before I could talk myself out of it, and went to my room, and left her the quiet to come home to.
