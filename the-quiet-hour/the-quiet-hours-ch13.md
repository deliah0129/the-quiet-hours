# Chapter Thirteen

### Rule Thirteen: Always Correct the Record

The bus back from Jeongneung was half empty and smelled of wet wool and someone’s convenience-store fish cake, and I sat with the pink notice copied into my own notebook in my own hand and could not stop reading the column I’d squared.

It came off. Four hundred thousand won, gone, because the assessment ran against gross instead of net and nobody in that kitchen had ever once been the person who knew that. I knew it. I said it out loud in a room, and a woman I’d never met put down the angry pink paper and listened — argued every receipt after, yes, fought me line by line, but listened, and then told her son to pay me properly and feed me until I die. Nobody has asked me my number in four years of being hired by the night. The captain at the banquet hall read the tip-out wrong over my head and I signed for it because signing was all I had standing to do. That afternoon a stranger handed me a shoebox, and my standing came with it.

That’s the thing the licence buys. One line, and I made myself stop there, because the rest of it had a man in it — the one who found the door and stood in the corner of it and didn’t once try to be thanked.

The bus swayed. I held my notebook and the warmth of having been useful to someone out loud, and rode it the whole way home like something I might be made to give back.

-----

The radiator went at nine, and it went the way I always knew it would.

It’s banged in that hall every cold night since before Han-byeol left — the knock I stopped hearing years ago, the way you stop hearing your own clock — and that night the knock climbed to a hiss, the hiss to a thin shriek, and a seam let go and rust-brown water sheeted out across the boards between our two doors. I was off the couch with a towel before I’d fully decided to be, and his door opened the same second, and he came out with that ridiculous multi-tool, the one that shimmed my table, like a man who thinks a multi-tool fixes radiators.

“Shutoff’s under it.” I was already down on my knees in the spreading wet, fingers finding the iron behind the pipe by feel — I’ve done this before, the year the boiler went, Han-byeol holding a pot. The valve wouldn’t give. Twenty years of nobody turning it, and the cold water was coming through the knees of my trousers, and the only warmth in the flat was running out across the floor and down toward the bath. “Landlord won’t come till Monday. He never does. We do it or we mop till spring.”

“Move your hands.”

“It’s my radiator.”

“It’s everyone’s radiator and it’s winning. Move.”

I didn’t move so much as make room, because two hands weren’t turning it and four might, and he came down into the cold water beside me and put his hands over the valve and mine stayed half on it, and for a moment we were both gripping the same seized iron — his hands over the backs of mine, mine gone stiff with cold, the rust-and-water smell coming up off the boards between us. We leaned into it together. It ground, gave a quarter-turn, came free — and the shriek dropped to a spit, the spit to a drip, the drip to nothing.

The quiet after was enormous, and I was the one who didn’t come out of it.

Nose to nose on the floor of a flooded hall, my hand still pinned warm under his on the dead valve, both of us soaked through — and my body did the thing it had been threatening to do since the doorway three weeks before. The breath gone shallow, the pulse up where I could feel it in my own throat, the want arriving low and total and entirely uninvited. My eyes went to his mouth. Once. I didn’t decide it; it just happened, the way the numbers used to just happen, automatic, before he broke them. I would log it, for the record, as the body being an animal at the end of a cold night and nothing past it — except that wasn’t the part that undid me.

The part that undid me was that he didn’t move.

His hands stayed exactly where they were, over mine, not closing the inch — and I understood, in the half-second I let myself look at it, that he was waiting. That the inch was mine to close or not. That he had handed me the whole of it and would sit in the cold water all night before he took a thing I didn’t give. Nobody has ever once left the choice with me. The world takes; that’s its whole nature; I built a bolt against it. And here was a man holding still in eight inches of rust water, making himself into a thing I could walk away from — and that, not his mouth, not the warm of him, was what put my pulse through the roof, because I had no column for it and nowhere to file it and no wall built against being handed my own choice.

I pulled back. An inch. Enough.

And the spell let us both down onto the cold wet boards and the standing water we still had to bail, and a laugh came out of me I hadn’t signed off on — helpless, whole, the first one in longer than I can name — at the sheer idiot size of the disaster, the two of us wrecked and dripping over a dead radiator at nine o’clock at night.

“We need a heater.” I was wringing my sleeve, still laughing, my breath fogging faint in the cooling air. “And tape, before it drips all weekend. There’s a place by the station, open till twelve.” I was up, hunting dry socks, because moving was better than sitting in what just didn’t happen. “I’m not freezing all night to make a point.”

-----

The hardware store at half eleven was half its lights, switched off for closing, the aisles gone the flat blue of a shop that wants to go home. I was pricing the floor-model heater against the boxed one — nine thousand in it, and nine thousand is a day — when I clocked the girl by the rice cookers go still, head tilting, phone coming up halfway, the exact angle I’d seen twice now and learned to hate.

I didn’t think. I’d covered for him on instinct once already, at the fish stall, and been frightened by how easily my body did it; that night my body did it again before the fear could catch up.

I stepped into his side, hooked my arm through his, turned us both to the shelf with my back square to the girl, and heard myself say it loud and bored, in a voice that belonged to some other woman entirely: “Babe. I’m not paying full price when the floor model’s nine thousand cheaper and does the exact same nothing. We’re not made of money. The radiator already ate the week.”

It was the most I’d ever said to him in one breath and not one word of it was true and I have never in my life been so fluent. I felt him catch it — the half-second where he could have ruined it and didn’t — and then he was in it with me, it’s got half the wattage, and I was walking us down the aisle on his arm like a wife who’s had this fight a hundred times, the most invisible thing two people can be. The girl’s certainty went soft. The phone dropped. The danger wandered off toward the rice cookers.

I didn’t let go of his arm for two more aisles. I told myself it was to sell it. His sleeve was warm the whole length of my forearm and I was aware of every inch of that, and I filed it, hard, somewhere I wouldn’t look.

The man at the register was seventy if he was a day, glasses on a cord, a trot song playing low under the counter. He rang the heater, the tape, a roll of grey shop towels, and took us in — soaked to the knee, my hair drying wrong, the wrung-out look of people who’ve been bailing out a room — and his whole face went soft in a way that put a stone in my chest before he’d said a word.

“First place together?” He knocked two thousand off the heater without being asked. “It’s always the radiator. Or the boiler. Always something.” He bagged the tape. “My wife and I, first winter — the pipe under the sink let go at two in the morning. I held the bucket, she held the wrench. Forty years next spring.” He slid the bag across. “Keep the one who’ll hold the wrench at midnight. The pretty ones are everywhere. The wrench ones, you marry.”

“We’re not—” I started, and the rest of it didn’t come.

It should have come. It always comes — we’re not together, it’s not like that, you’ve got the wrong idea — the correction is right there, the one I’ve made to a hundred people about a hundred things. I am the woman who corrects the record. I will sit in a banquet hall and square a tip-out in my head that I have no standing to say out loud rather than let a wrong number stand inside my own skull. And I stood at that counter with a stranger’s kindness and a heater I could almost afford and the warmth of his arm still on mine, and I let the unfinished sentence lie there between the three of us — left the record wrong, on purpose, for the first time I can remember — because finishing it cost more, that night, than letting an old man be right about the wrong thing.

“Thank you,” Do-yun said, and took the bag, and the trot song played us out into the cold.

-----

We mopped till midnight, badly, together — I wrung the towels grey-brown into the bucket, he hauled it and dumped it down the bath drain and came back for the next, the new heater set in the mouth of the hall ticking out its small orange warmth. The flat smelled of rust and wet board and, under all of it, the charcoal that had come home in our clothes. Neither of us said much. There wasn’t much that needed it.

When the boards were only damp we gave up and sat against opposite walls of the hall, knees up, too wrung out to make it the last few steps to our own rooms, the heater breathing its one warm note between us.

“Thursday,” I said, to the heater, not to him. “Her filing’s clean by Thursday. Four hundred off.” I turned my notebook over in my hands. “Thank you. For the door.” I meant it to come out level, a receipt closed. It didn’t quite. “Just the door.”

“You did the rest.”

I didn’t answer that. I was done arguing with him about who did what; arguing takes a kind of awake I’d run all the way out of. The heater ticked. There was a scrap of paper on the wet floor by his foot — fallen out of a pocket in the bailing, one of his, the five lines and the marks gone soft at the edge from the flood. I picked it up before I’d decided to, dried it against my dry sleeve, and folded it into my notebook with the others, and he watched me do it and said nothing, and I didn’t explain, and the not-explaining sat between us warmer than the heater.

Through the wall — except there was no wall that night, we were on the same side of it — I could hear him breathing, and somewhere under that, low, the hum that has no name and finds the old place in me every time, and I let it, because I hadn’t the strength left to build against a man who was only humming.

Sleep took me sitting up, chin on my knees, before I’d decided to allow it.

I surfaced once, for a second, near the bottom — to the weight of something warm settling over my shoulders, a blanket I hadn’t asked for and was too far under to refuse, laid there by hands that didn’t linger and didn’t wait to be thanked. Don’t, said the old reflex, faint, the one that knows what it costs to be given to. But I was already going back down, and the warmth was already mine, and for once — just once, just that night — I let the gift stay where it had been put.
