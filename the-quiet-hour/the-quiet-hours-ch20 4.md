# Chapter Twenty

### Rule Twenty: Never Sing the Quiet Part Out Loud

I woke up famous in a way I had spent two very deliberate months not being, and the first thing I did, like an idiot, was knock on the wall.

Habit. Sixty-odd mornings of it. One knuckle, low on the plaster, then wait — and most mornings nothing came back, because she was a night creature and dead to the world at seven and considered dawn a rumour. But once in a while she'd rap back, one flat knock, which translated roughly to I am counting something, go be a person elsewhere, and that knock was, no exaggeration, the best-engineered moment in my entire engineered life. So I knocked. Before I'd looked at the phone walking itself across the floor on vibrate like a thing trying to crawl out of the room. Knock. Nothing. Of course nothing — it was the fourteenth, and on the fourteenth she had a door of her own to go and earn, and she'd have left before light, and I knew that, and I knocked anyway, because the body learns things the brain is too proud to say out loud.

Then I picked up the phone, and the phone was on fire.

Two hundred and some missed calls is a number you don't read so much as feel, the way you feel a lift drop. My first thought — and I want this on the record as evidence of exactly how stupid hope makes a person — was that something nice had happened. A second thought, faster and more honest, said no, idiot, somebody's dead or you're cancelled, those are the only two things that ring two hundred times before breakfast. I thumbed it open. The band chat had achieved a state I can only describe as fully molten, a vertical blur of caps and links, and at the top, pinned, from Yejun, who pins nothing, who once watched me break a hand and didn't pin anything: WHO FILMED THIS.

And then a link. And then, because the universe enjoys a slow reveal, my own thumb, betraying me, tapping it.

You know that sound. By now everyone does. But I heard it first the way you hear your own voice on a recording for the first time as a child and recoil — wrong, too close, not mine — except it was mine, unmistakably, a guitar I'd restrung myself and a low string I keep the time on and a voice doing the thing it only does when it thinks no one is paying. Came in from the rain with my hands full of nothing. A backlit shape in a window. Mine. My window. The little square of dark across the airshaft where I'd sat three nights ago with the sash up, singing the true one to a city I was certain wasn't listening, because she was awake and it cost me nothing to be heard by a girl who didn't know the difference between my voice and the radiator.

Someone across the gap had been listening. Someone across the gap had filmed it.

A hundred and fifty thousand of them, the counter said, and climbing while I watched, like a tide coming in over your feet before you've decided to move.

Here is the thing about being a product. I have been a product for fifteen years; I know the spec sheet better than I know my own blood type. A product has releases. A product chooses the window — pun fully intended, I'm aware, I make this joke later to a man who does not laugh — chooses the window, the lighting, the caption, the second the thing goes out. That's the whole job. That's the deal you sign. You hand them your face and they decide when the world gets to look at it, and in exchange you never, ever have to find out what it feels like to be looked at without permission.

And I'd gone and opened an actual window.

The song wasn't a release. The song wasn't a product. The song was the one thing in the entire inventory of me that had never been for sale, the thing I'd been not-finishing for three years precisely because finishing it would mean it was done and done meant gone, and now it was out, raw, unmixed, with the meaning showing — and the worst part, the part that landed last and lowest, was that it was still broken. They'd taken the unfinished one. The whole country was now passing around the version that stops. And the light had a — and then nothing. I'd let the most private cliff in my life go over the edge in front of everyone and it didn't even have the dignity of an ending.

I'd sung the quiet part out loud. Turns out there's a rule about that.

The phone rang in my hand mid-thought — not the chat, an actual call, the contact that comes up as nothing because I'd long ago deleted the word for it — and I answered it the way you answer the thing you can't not answer.

"Tell me you were home alone." My mother does not say hello. My mother regards hello as a tariff. "Tell me the room behind you was empty."

"Good morning to you too."

"Do-yun." The voice that has arranged my entire life into a series of yeses, level as a spirit bubble. "There is a window in that footage. Windows have neighbours. Neighbours have addresses. We announce tonight, and I will not have the announcement share a news cycle with a question I cannot answer. Was the room behind you empty."

And this is the part I think about. Because the true answer was no — the room behind me had a wall in it, and behind the wall a woman who keeps her careful money in a shoebox and my discarded scraps in the back of her ledger and has never once asked me for a single thing, including my name — and what came out of my mouth was the trained thing, the fifteen-year reflex, smooth as a swallow.

"Empty," I said. "I live alone."

She hung up. She always hangs up the second she has what she came for; it's almost restful, being used by someone that efficient. And I stood in my socks in the grey light holding a lie I'd told to protect a woman who would, if she heard it, file it under exactly the kind of thing she already suspected me of, and I understood — slow, cold, complete, the way she'd understand it — that I had just done to Jae the precise thing the whole machine does to everyone. I'd looked at her and decided, on her behalf, what she got to know. For her own good. The road to that woman's particular hell is paved entirely with people deciding things for her own good.

I was still standing there indicting myself when Hyun came through my door without knocking, which Hyun never does, and that was how I knew it was worse than the phone.

He looked like a man who'd been awake since a yesterday that had also not gone well. He took the phone out of my hand, turned the counter face-down on the table like switching off a smoke alarm, and said, "Have you eaten."

"Have I — Hyun. There are a hundred and fifty thousand—"

"Two hundred now. Have you eaten."

"No. Have you?"

He didn't answer that, which is its own answer, which is the answer he's been giving for two months, and I felt the thing I always feel watching Hyun run himself down to the rim to keep my life from spilling — the want to put my body between him and the next thing, and the total uselessness of a product trying to protect its handler. "You eat first," I said. "Whatever this is, you eat first, I'm not—"

"The label called me at five." Flat. He sat on the arm of the one chair, the way Reo sits when a thing is too big to take standing. "Noh, personally, which Noh does not do, Noh has people to call people. He has thoughts about the clip. He used the word opportunity four times and the word liability twice and I could hear him doing sums the whole way through, and Do-yun—" he scrubbed his face "—your mother called him at four. An hour before me. They are both already inside this, pulling opposite directions, and you are the rope."

"What does Noh want."

"What Noh always wants. He wants the version of you that sells. And right now the version that sells just changed shape under him overnight, and a viral solo with a missing ending is the single most valuable unfinished thing in the country, and he would like to own the ending. Lock you down, milk the moment, no merger, no marriage, a sad boy with a guitar prints money — that's his pitch. Your mother wants the opposite. Engagement tonight, on schedule, the clip buried under a happy announcement, you retired into a merger by summer. One of them wants you single and bleeding on a stage forever. The other wants you safely married and off the board. They have never agreed on anything in their lives and they are about to have a very loud argument with you standing in the middle of it."

I should have felt cornered. For fifteen years that sentence — two powers want opposite things and you're the rope — has meant exactly one thing, which is that I lose, the only question is the exchange rate. And I stood there waiting for the familiar smallness to arrive, the fold, the okay, the yes I always give because giving it is the only move a product has.

It didn't arrive.

Instead, something I'd never once felt in fifteen years of being moved around a board: I noticed I was holding a piece.

"Hyun," I said slowly, testing it like a stair in the dark. "They both need the song."

He went still.

"Noh needs it to be the next thing. My mother needs it to be nothing. Neither of those works without the guy who wrote it, because it's not finished, and the only person on earth who can finish it is—" me. The word stuck, because finishing it meant her, and I wasn't ready to say the shape of that out loud in a room with the lights on. "It's mine," I said instead. "The thing they're both fighting over is mine. I've spent my whole life being the asset. For about a day, until somebody takes it back, I'm the one holding the asset."

"Do-yun." Careful. The careful he uses near edges. "That is leverage. I need you to understand that what you just described is leverage, and that you have never in your life been allowed to have any, and that the very next thing that's going to happen is two extremely good players are going to try very hard to make sure you don't notice you're holding it."

"Then don't let them buy it back cheap."

He looked at me for a long second, and something moved in his exhausted face that I'd put — if I had to file it, and I do, I file everything, it's the family disease — under pride, badly hidden. "Okay," he said. "Okay. But not from here, and not in your socks, and not before you eat, because a man negotiating on no food makes mistakes, and we cannot afford a mistake, because here is the part neither of them said." He stood. "A clip is just weather. Loud, then gone. What turns weather into a career — into a man's whole life — is the broadcast. The shows. The distribution. Who decides the country sees it on Saturday and again on Sunday and on every screen in every taxi. There are maybe three companies in this country that own that sky, that decide whether a storm gets a forecast or just rains itself out, and Daon owns most of it, and Daon is exactly the kind of door neither you nor I have ever had a key to. The clip is yours for a day. The sky belongs to people we will never meet. Remember that before you start feeling powerful."

I'd remember it. I'd remember it later, in fact, with a specificity that would take my legs out — that on the worst morning of my life Hyun stood in my socked-feet flat and named, like a thing safely out of reach, the one door that would turn out to open from the inside the whole time, standing five feet away behind a wall, doing someone's books. But that's getting ahead. That morning Daon was just the sky, and I nodded, and meant it.

And then, because the body keeps its own ledger, I looked at the wall.

"She's at her exam," I said.

Hyun followed my eyes and understood, the way Hyun understands everything a half-second before I've finished being stupid about it. "The roommate."

"It's the fourteenth. She's been working toward this morning for four years. She's in a room right now with a pencil, not knowing the building she lives in is about to have a street team on it by lunch, because a window in a video is going to get matched to a roof, and the roof is this roof, and when the crowd finds the door she's going to come home from the most important morning of her life to a mob outside her own laundromat, and she's going to find out what I am from a stranger with a phone, and she still thinks—" I stopped. I could not, in front of Hyun, in the lit room, say she thinks I'm a spy and I let her, because the let her was the whole crime and saying it would make it real.

"What does she think," Hyun said.

"That I'm nobody. That I'm a quiet weird man who can't make rice." My voice did something I didn't authorize. "That was the best thing that's happened to me in fifteen years, Hyun. Being nobody to her. And it's got maybe four hours left and I'm going to spend them in a car going to fight Noh instead of telling her the four words before the country does."

"What four words."

I knew them. They'd been sitting fully formed behind my teeth for two months, simple as a sum, and I hadn't once managed to spend them. They weren't I'm an idol. They weren't I'm getting married. Those were facts, and facts I could've filed her into at any point. The four words underneath all of them, the ones that were actually true and actually hard, were a different four entirely, and I wasn't ready to hand them to Hyun, so I shook my head.

"Tell her tonight," he said, gently, gathering my jacket off the chair, my shoes, herding me toward the door the way you herd someone who's stopped being able to herd himself. "After. You can't tell her now and then leave her in it alone for twelve hours; that's the worst of both. Fight the fight, lock the leverage so the crowd outside her door is the smallest crowd it can be, and then go home and say the four words to her face like a person, not text them to her like a coward at a stoplight. Can you do that. Do-yun. Can you do the hard one in the right order."

"Yeah," I said.

I could not, as it turned out, do that. Not because I wasn't willing. Because by the time I'd done the fight, the door I meant to say it to face-to-face had already been answered by the whole country, and by the time I got home there was no face on the other side of the wall to say it to at all.

But I didn't know that yet. That morning I knew only that it was the fourteenth, that three powers wanted three different versions of me and for once I had a card, and that somewhere across the city a woman with a pencil was being the same person who'd found four hundred thousand won in a shoebox a stranger handed her, going to remind a room.

I put my shoes on. I let Hyun take me out the door.

I didn't knock on the wall on the way past. I want that on the record too. It was the one morning I should have, and I'd run out of the nerve to hear it not answer.
