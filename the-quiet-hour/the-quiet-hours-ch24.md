Rule Twenty-Four: Last to Know, First to Leave

Jae


I gave the lock back at dawn.

That’s the part that stayed with me, out of a night I’d have happily handed back whole. A girl at the bathhouse desk, half asleep, holding out her palm, and me peeling the plastic band off my wrist and setting the little locker key in her hand, and the small administrative click of a thing that was never mine going back to the people it belonged to. I’d paid to borrow a door for one night out of the careful money I keep for nothing. Now the night was over and the door was theirs again and I was back out on the step in yesterday’s clothes with a foil packet of cold rice going hard against my hip, the owner of precisely nothing that locked.

Four years I’d spent earning a door of my own. I’d sat the exam for it — a morning test, at the end of a week I’d barely slept and a life I’d barely slept — and come out the other side knowing I’d passed the way you know a column’s right before you’ve run the check: privately, completely, with no paper yet to prove it. The paper takes a few days. So I had no licence in my hand, only the felt fact of one, sitting where good news goes to wait for permission to be felt, and I could not, that morning, find the permission. You cannot move into a licence. You cannot sleep in a column that finally balances. I’d done the one thing that was supposed to make me safe and discovered, in under a day — before the post could even make it official — that I’d built it on a floor that had been somebody else’s the whole time.

I did not go home. I want to be accurate about why, because it wasn’t bravery and it wasn’t even pride. It was arithmetic. Home was four flights and a thin wall and, on the far side of that wall, a man I’d have to have a face about. And I did not have a face yet. I had not finished doing the sum, and you don’t stand up from the table until the sum is done.

-----

Here is the sum, for the record, since I’m the one who keeps mine.

A lie I could have filed. People lie; I’ve audited liars my whole life, I know the shape of a cooked book and I know what to do when I find one — you close it, you don’t reopen it, you delete the number and the man who wrote it. Ji-hwan was a lie, and I’d closed Ji-hwan in an afternoon. If the man behind my wall had simply lied to me — given me a false name and a false story and let me believe them — I’d have been furious and I’d have been fine. Fury I can spend. Fury has somewhere to go.

This wasn’t that. This was worse, and it took me until a heated floor full of sleeping strangers to find the word for it, and the word was overlooked.

He hadn’t lied to me. He’d been kept from me — by the apparatus, the people, the men paid full salaries to know where he sleeps — and every one of them had looked at the situation and decided I was the safe place to not mention it. Not because I was trustworthy. Because I was furniture. You don’t brief the chair. You don’t read the wall in. The whole machinery of the most-guarded life in the country had run its numbers on me and come back with the same total I’d been getting my entire life: she’s the one nobody has to tell. Somebody else will handle her, and if nobody does, it won’t matter, because she was never really in the room.

I’d carried his secret for two months and never once been worth the sentence it would have cost to give it to me.

So no. I wasn’t going to go home and have a face about it. I was going to do what I have always done, which is the only thing that has never once done it back to me: I was going to pull the armor up, plate by plate, and become a woman this had happened to rather than a woman it was still happening to. There’s a difference. The difference is a door. I’d wanted one my whole life and I finally understood I’d been building the wrong kind — a door that locks the world out is no use at all if you’ve already handed someone the key and called it the best thing that ever happened to you.

I would not be making that error twice. I closed the book on the flat the way I close any book that’s been lying to me. Quietly. Completely. With me on the outside of it for once, which I told myself was the same as being safe.

It is not the same as being safe. But it’s quieter, and that morning I’d have traded almost anything for quiet.

-----

The quiet lasted until a little after eleven, which is roughly how long it takes a photograph to find its price.

I was in a franchise coffee place I’d never been in and would never be in again, the kind with the laminated menu and the seat bolted to the floor, nursing the cheapest thing on it to rent the chair, when the two students at the next table tilted a phone between them and made the noise. You know the noise by now. I’d heard the whole country make it at a soju table the day before. “No. No way. That’s — wait, zoom in. That’s the same window.”

I kept my eyes on my cup. I had got very good, in twenty-four hours, at keeping my eyes on my cup.

“He’s got a girl,” one of them breathed, in the tone people save for other people’s ruin, and something in me went very still, the held kind, the kind I’d felt come off a man in a market once and filed under hiding. “Look. Going into the building. They got the building. They got the street.”

I looked. Of course I looked. I am constitutionally incapable of leaving a number on the table once it’s in front of me, and there it was, on a stranger’s cracked screen, upside down to me and perfectly legible anyway because I have spent my life reading things upside down across counters: my street. My laundromat, dead-windowed, with its faded OWNER — CALL FOR ANYTHING still taped in the door. And in the foreground, caught mid-step with a grocery bag and her hair wet from a shift, a woman whose face was blurred to a smear by the bad long lens but whose coat I knew, because I’d bought it for nine thousand won at a market three winters ago and mended the cuff myself.

The blur was me. The most famous unsolved window in the country had a street now, and an address, and a woman going into it with the groceries, and the woman was me, rendered down to a smudge and a coat, which is about the resolution at which the world had always been prepared to see me anyway.

I did the thing I do. I read it for the false bottom. And the false bottom was right there, because I knew exactly two things no caption did. I knew that lens hadn’t found that street by matching rooftops and water tanks like eleven thousand romantics playing detective. And I knew exactly one person on this earth who had my address, who I hadn’t given it to, who’d turned up at the galbi house a week ago wearing a coat that cost more than it should and a debt I could smell on him like smoke.

-----

The text came an hour later, because of course it did. He’d never once accepted that a closed door was closed.

“Jae. We should talk before this gets away from you. I can help. I know how these things work and you don’t. Call me.”

I read it standing on a corner I didn’t know, and I did the math on it the way I do all my math, cold and complete and arriving whether I want it or not, and the math was so sad it nearly cut through the armor I’d spent all morning bolting on.

Because here’s what I clocked, and I clocked it the way I clock a column that’s been cooked — not the lie itself but the pressure behind the lie, the shape of the hand that moved the number. Ji-hwan didn’t sell me because he hated me. Hate would have been almost a comfort; hate, at least, is a kind of attention. He sold me because somewhere a man he owes had stopped being polite, and somewhere in the same week the broke ex-boyfriend of nobody became the broke ex-boyfriend of the secret woman in the most valuable photograph in the country, and a man drowning in a number will sell whatever the tide floats up. I’d been the thing the tide floated up. I can help meant there’s more where that came from and I want to be the one paid to find it. The talk he wanted was a negotiation, and the asset on the table was me.

He’d looked at me — at the address, the coat, everything he’d ever known about exactly how thin I live — and he’d seen a line item. A thing worth more sold than kept. I’d like to say it surprised me. The truer and stupider thing is that it landed in the oldest, most furnished room in me, the one that’s never needed redecorating, the one that’s known since before I had a name I kept: of course. Of course that’s what I’m worth to the people who get close. Of course the move is to liquidate me. Two men had now done their sums on me inside a week — one by saying nothing, one by selling everything — and both had arrived at the same total, which was that there was more value in what I could be turned into than in anything I actually was.

I didn’t answer him. I’m not built to answer a man like that; answering is the door reopening, and I was finished reopening doors. I blocked the number — a small clean satisfying nothing, the lock that was mine — and I turned my phone face down on the cold metal of a bus shelter bench and sat in the noise of a city that had, overnight, learned the street I lived on.

-----

I would have stayed out there all day building higher walls if my phone hadn’t lit, face-down, and buzzed itself in a half-circle across the bench, and I’d turned it over out of pure animal reflex and seen it wasn’t the blocked number. It was the galbi house. It was, specifically, Reo, who I had a number for because I do everyone’s books and people who owe you their tax penalty give you their number.

I shouldn’t have answered. I answered.

“Don’t hang up.” No hello. The kitchen behind him, the hood fan, a clatter, his mother’s voice somewhere shouting something about a delivery. “I know you can hear me deciding whether to hang up. Don’t.”

“I’m hanging up.”

“Jae.” And then nothing, for a second, from a man who is never nothing for a second, Reo who fills every silence he’s ever been handed — and the nothing told me he’d seen the photo too. “We didn’t know.”

“I know you didn’t know.”

“No, listen to me, because I can hear you already filing us, I can hear it.” The fan cut out behind him; he’d stepped into the alley, the way he keeps the parts of his life in separate rooms. “We genuinely thought you knew. All of us. You live with the man, Jae. You live with him. You do his — you did the books for the family of the guy he —” he caught himself on something, a wall I didn’t have the map for yet, and went around it. “Nobody hid it from you. That’s the part I need you to have. There was no meeting. Nobody sat in a room and decided not to tell you. It’s worse than that and I know it’s worse than that. We all just looked at the two of you and thought — obviously she knows. She’s the one who’d know. You were so far inside it we forgot you couldn’t see the outside.”

And there it was, handed to me by a man in an apron in an alley: the exact shape of the wound, said out loud by someone who loved me and had helped inflict it without a drop of malice. We forgot you couldn’t see the outside. Not a conspiracy. Not even carelessness, quite. Just the thing that has followed me my whole life like a smell I can’t wash out — the universal, load-bearing, entirely reasonable assumption that surely, surely, someone else has got me, that I am so obviously already accounted for that no one ever has to do the accounting.

“Sun’s been crying since six,” Reo said, into my silence, “if it helps.”

It did, fractionally, help, which I resented. “Why is Sun crying.”

“Because Sun cries. He sent the group thirty messages and they’re all just your name and broken hearts. He wants to come find you and feed you. We talked him out of it. You’re welcome.” A breath. The fond, blunt thing, the thing about Reo that gets under the armor because he says the soft part flat, like a fact, like a price. “Halmeoni hasn’t put her phone down. She keeps the foil packet thing by the till in case you come back hungry. We didn’t know, and we’re not going to pretend it makes it hurt less, but the kitchen’s yours whenever, and nobody in it is going to make you have a face about it. You can come be furniture in the corner. We’re good at letting people be nothing here. You taught us that. Sort of. You and—”

He stopped. The name he didn’t say sat in the gap, the way names do.

“Don’t,” I said.

“Wasn’t going to.” He didn’t. Some gates you leave where the other person set them; he’d learned that from me, I think, or maybe I’d learned it from this kitchen, I’ve stopped being able to tell which of us taught which. “Come eat. That’s the whole offer. No face required.”

I almost did. I want that on the record too — that the one door in the city that opened for me that day was a galbi house with a wrong-noted fridge, and the people inside it held it open and asked nothing, and for about four seconds I let myself want to walk through it. Found family. The warm room that needs nothing from you. The thing I’d spent my whole careful life proving I didn’t require and had, apparently, been quietly storing in the back of my own ledger anyway, like a soft entry I’d let on against my own rule.

“I have to get my things,” I heard myself say instead. Which was true, and was also the armor finding the one errand that would let it keep moving. “My books. My — the licence paperwork’s at the flat. It comes in the post and I have to be there for it. It’s the only thing I—” The sentence had a true bottom and I didn’t go down to it. “It’s mine. It’s the one thing that’s actually mine and it’s sitting in a flat with my street on the internet.”

A pause from the alley. When Reo spoke again the fond had gone careful. “Jae. The street’s on the internet. Which means the street’s got people on it by now. You know that, right? Don’t go alone. Let me—”

“I’ll be fine.”

“That’s not — that’s not even an answer, that’s a thing people say instead of—”

I hung up. The lock that was mine.

-----

I should have listened to him. I’ll give the record that much in advance, since the record’s going to need it.

I took the long way back as the light went, down through the streets that turn into names I know, toward the river and the dead laundromat and the four flights and the door I’d already failed to keep shut once. I told myself I’d go up the back, get the box of my life — the textbooks, the careful money, the envelope the licence would come in — and be gone before anyone with a long lens finished their dinner. I told myself it was a clean errand. One in, one out, no total, no face.

I came around the last corner and the street that had always been just a street — the fish stall, the knife-sharpener, the long ordinary slope down to the water — was not just a street anymore.

There were people. Not a mob, not yet; that’s never how it starts. It starts as a loose scatter of phones and a parked van and three or four of them leaning on the laundromat’s dead glass like they had a right to the doorway, and a girl at the front of it with a face I half-knew from somewhere bright and obsessive, already lifting her phone the second a woman in a nine-thousand-won coat came round the corner with her hands jammed in her pockets and her exam-passing future in one of them.

The girl’s face did the thing the schoolgirl’s had done in the market, months ago, when I’d shouldered a stranger out of frame to keep him whole. The certainty arriving. The phone coming up.

Except this time there was no stall to shoulder him into, no halmeonis to fold the moment shut, no shoulder to put between anybody and anything. This time the certainty was about me, and the street was already turning, the loose scatter tightening the way a column tightens into a total, every phone finding the same figure at the same time, and the figure was me, and there was nowhere left to be furniture.

“It’s her,” somebody said. “It’s her.”

And the street closed.
