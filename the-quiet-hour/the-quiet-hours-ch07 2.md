Rule Seven: Water to the First Knuckle

Jae


Seven in the morning, and the stairwell smelled of scorched rice.

It got worse the higher I climbed — four flights, no lift, past the bulb that’s been dying since the year I moved in, past the door where the couple were already at it — until there was no mistaking it at my own landing: rice taken well past brown, into black, into the particular reek of a pot a person had walked away from. My door was off the latch. Mine is never off the latch.

The kitchen told the whole story before he did. A pot in the sink, the bottom gone to tar, filling slowly with grey water. On the counter a rice cooker — new, cheap, the box flattened against the wall with the delivery label still on it, a price sticker I read without deciding to: nine thousand nine hundred won, the very bottom of the range. A week ago there had been a watch on this table worth more than the room it sat in. The watch had been gone three days.

His shelf — the one he promised, the one he labelled — held four white dishes of the kind sold two to a sleeve for pocket change, stickers still on them. No knife block. No decent pan. A whole kitchen bought in one frightened sweep by a man who has plainly never once in his life had to buy a kitchen.

And among it, by the cheap cooker, two more of the scratched scraps — a convenience-store receipt and a torn corner of something, both ruled with the five lines and the marks, both carrying the disconnected words I’d stopped pretending were code. Warm. Count. House. I had a small stack of them now, kept for no reason I’d say out loud, pressed flat in the back of my ledger between the bus fares and the rent.

A spy does not write the same three soft words forty ways and then throw them in the bin. I didn’t yet know what kind of man does. I only knew the theory I’d filed him under was coming apart in my hands, and that whatever was under it was going to be worse — because worse, for me, has always meant harder to hold a wall against.

Men climb. I’ve watched it since I was old enough to watch anything: everyone reaching for the next rung, the better label, the bigger number. I had never once, in my life, seen a man climb down on purpose. People don’t erase what they have unless they’re hiding what they were.

“Don’t.” His voice came from the bathroom doorway, wrecked with no sleep. “Whatever face you’re about to make. Don’t make it.”

I hadn’t turned around. “I’m not making a face.”

“You’re making it at the pot. I can hear it.” He came in — grey T-shirt soft from washing, nothing printed on it, bare feet, the black hair sleep-flattened on one side and the grey surfacing at the crown where his hand never reaches. He looked at the ruined pot the way a man looks at a bill he can’t pay. “It lied to me.”

“The pot lied to you.”

“The bag.” Total seriousness. “The rice bag. One part rice, one and a half parts water. I measured it. I measured it exactly.”

“In what.”

“…A mug.”

“Whose mug.”

A silence. “Yours.”

I closed my eyes. “And then.”

“And then I put it on the stove, because I didn’t trust the machine, and I did something else for four minutes, and it became charcoal.” His hand went flat on the counter and his fingers started up against the laminate — tap, tap-tap, tap, a small even rhythm, like a man keeping time with something only he can hear. “I followed the instructions and the instructions were a trap.”

“What were you doing for the four minutes.”

“Learning how to do it properly. On my phone. There are nine hundred videos. Every one starts with a woman saying it’s so easy and ends with rice that looks nothing like mine.” The fingers went still. “I asked Hyun. Hyun is thirty-eight years old and he said — I’m quoting — you put it in the thing and you press the thing.” He gestured at the cooker, betrayed by the entire world. “I pressed the thing.”

“That’s not the thing you pressed. That’s a pot.”

“I’m aware of that now.”

“Did you rinse it.”

The fingers stopped again. “…Rinse it.”

“The rice. Before. Until the water runs clear.”

He looked at me the way you’d look at someone describing a step in a magic trick. “There’s a rinsing.”

Twenty-six years old, hands that have never wrung out a cloth, standing in my kitchen genuinely astonished that rice gets washed. “Where,” I said, “did you grow up.”

The shutter came down so smoothly I’d have missed it if I weren’t already watching for it. “Around.”

“Around.”

“It’s fine. I’ll order another bag. I’ll have it tomorrow.”

“You’ll order another bag of rice.” I pointed, slowly, at the window, at the awning two storeys down and forty steps left. “There is a shop. On the corner. It sells rice. I walk past it twice a day.”

“I prefer delivery.”

“At nine thousand won a cooker you can’t work, you prefer delivery.” I let it sit, the way you let a column sit when the total’s wrong and you want to see who flinches. He didn’t flinch. He’s good at that. He’s good at all of it, which was the trouble — nobody is that smooth about rice. “Who brings it, then. The sandwiches in your fridge. The drinks. Somebody’s carrying it up four flights, and it isn’t you, because you don’t go out.”

“Hyun.”

“Who’s Hyun.”

“A friend. He helps me out.”

“A grown man who can’t make rice has a friend who does his shopping and won’t let him walk to the corner.” I filed it on the long list — the one that gets longer every day I live three feet of bad plaster from him, and shorter not at all.

-----

I should have gone to bed. Eleven-to-seven is a long night and my body was past the part where standing comes free; the exam was nine days out and the sleep between here and there was the only thing standing between me and another year of the same. I knew the math of it cold.

Instead I was at the sink, running water into the murdered pot, because a ruined pot is a problem with a solution, and almost nothing else that week had been.

“Give me the new bag.”

“You don’t have to—”

“Give me the bag, Doha, or you’ll starve in a building that stops delivering at nine.”

He gave me the bag.

What followed was a lesson in a kitchen built for one and a half people at the outside. I tipped rice into the cooker’s bowl by eye — this much, two days — and his shoulder was at my shoulder because there was nowhere else in the room for a shoulder to be. I ran the tap in, swirled it with my fingers, tilted the white cloud of it off into the sink. Again. The water went from milk to haze. “You’re not getting it clean. You’re getting the dust off so it doesn’t turn to glue. Again.”

“How many times.”

“Until it runs clear.”

“That’s not a number.” He sounded almost injured. “Everything you do is a number. The till, the rent, the bus. Suddenly it’s until it runs clear.”

“Three times, near enough, this rice. Different rice, different number. You learn the rice.” I said it because he was plainly going to stand there all morning otherwise.

“You learn the rice.” He said it as though I’d told him the rice had feelings.

“Everything has a number if you stand still long enough to find it. Rice. Rent. People.” I tipped the last of the cloudy water off. “Rice is the easy one. Here — you do this part.”

I stepped half a pace back; there was nowhere to step. He put his hands into the bowl like a man defusing something, swirled it once, carefully, as if speed might offend it.

“It’s water and rice. It won’t break.”

“You don’t know that. Nothing in my life has ever not broken when I touched it.” He said it lightly — too lightly, the shutter doing its quiet work — and tipped the water off, and got most of it into the sink and a fair amount of it down his own front, and I watched all that easy grace of his fail completely at a bowl of wet rice and had to bite the inside of my cheek to keep my face flat.

“Level it.” I flattened the rice under my own palm to show him, then took his hand — his actual hand, there was no graceful way past it — and stood his finger upright on the wet bed of it. His hand ran warm. The scar across his knuckles was a pale ridge under my thumb, healed wrong, a small white country. “Water to the first knuckle. Not the mug. Not the line on the bowl. Your knuckle, every time, and it comes out right every time.”

He had gone very still. “A knuckle isn’t a standard unit.”

“It’s your knuckle and your rice. It’s the most standard unit there is.”

Neither of us took a hand back. He could have; he didn’t, quite; neither did I, quite, and the not-quite was louder in the small room than anything either of us had said. Two people crowded at a sink with their fingers in the same half-inch of cold water, the tap still running, the kitchen very small, and he smelled of sleep and cedar and that green thing and the plain warm fact of a body that had run too hot through a sleepless night, the same as mine had through a working one.

My pulse did a thing it had no business doing over rice. I’d like that noted as the body being an animal — I’ve read the chapter, I know how the wiring goes — and not as anything I signed off on. The number I’d been holding, two days, two bowls, went straight out of my head, and I let the water run a moment longer than the rice needed before I reached past him and shut it off.

I set the lid down. Found the button, because he plainly never would, and pressed it, and the cheap cooker breathed on with a small electronic sigh and a little four-note tune, and he watched it light up like it had done something holy. I had to look at the floor so I wouldn’t smile, because a man astonished by a nine-thousand-won rice cooker is not a thing I can afford to find charming at seven in the morning on no sleep.

-----

I opened his fridge while I was up, because someone had to, and it was worse than the cooker. A row of identical triangle sandwiches, all one flavour. A wall of the blue sports drink. Instant noodles two shelves deep. Nothing that had ever been alive.

“You’re going to get scurvy.”

“I’m efficient.”

“You’re going to get scurvy, Doha, this is a fridge from a hostage situation.” I know this fridge. I’ve owned this fridge, in worse months.

“It’s only for two months,” he said, as if that accounted for the sandwiches.

“You can eat vegetables for two months. People do it for whole lives. There’s a market two streets over, past the laundromat — vegetables that aren’t a crime, half what the corner charges and a quarter of whatever you’re paying to have garbage carried up here. I go Thursdays. Give me a list. I’ll bring it up with mine.”

He went still in a particular way — and then his hand dropped to his back pocket, to a wallet, and he was counting out notes before I’d finished talking, a fan of them, far too many, the gesture automatic as breathing: a man who has never once been offered a thing that didn’t come with a price he was expected to cover twice. “That’s — yes. Thank you. Here, for the groceries and for the—”

“Put it away.”

“It’s only—”

“Put it away.” Sharper than the rice. Sharper than I meant, because the sight of money fanned at me across a counter does a thing to me that cannot be done to me at seven in the morning. I have had money pushed at me over a counter before. By men who wanted me quiet, or gone, or turned back into a stranger. Once by a whole room of it that decided I was a thing that could be settled and made never to have happened.

I do not get settled.

“I offered to buy you a cabbage. You don’t pay a person for that. You pay it back with a cabbage of your own, or you say thank you and you remember it. That’s how it runs, down here.”

The notes went back into the pocket, slowly. Something worked behind his face — not embarrassment, closer to a man holding an instrument he’s trusted his whole life up to the light and finding the needle’s been off the entire time. “…Thank you,” he said. Only that. Nothing folded inside it. He said it carefully, like a word in a language he’d just learned plays by different rules than the one he grew up speaking. “I’ll make you a list.”

“Good.”

“A short one.”

“Make it however long it is. I’m going regardless.”

-----

The cooker counted down in its own private language. He leaned back against the counter to wait — nowhere to go in a flat that size — and somewhere in the waiting he started to hum. Low. Not for me. The way a person hums with idle hands.

I didn’t know the song. I was certain I’d never heard it. And it reached anyway into some place in me with no edges and no date on it and turned a key there — something from before I had words for anything, warm, close, held, gone the instant I reached toward it. The hair stood up along my arms and I could not have told you why, and not knowing why is not a feeling I keep in the house.

“What is that.”

He stopped. “What’s what.”

“That. You were — humming.”

“Was I.” He looked honestly caught, a different caught than the rice. “Sorry. I do it without noticing. Is it bothering you.”

“No.” That was the trouble; it wasn’t. “It’s familiar.”

Something crossed his face and was gone before I could price it. “Probably the radio,” he said, easy again, the shutter sliding back over. “It’s an old thing. It’s nothing.”

The night chose that moment to land on me all at once — the standing, the eleven hours, the whole long graveyard of it — and the kitchen tilted a half-degree and righted itself and I had a hand on the counter without deciding to put it there.

He saw it. Of course he did; he watches me the way I watch a till. “When did you last sleep.”

“I sleep days. I work nights.”

“That’s not what I asked.” He was already moving — filled the kettle, set it on the ring, took down the chipped mug and then caught himself, set it back, reached instead for one of his own cheap white ones. Even now, even half-dead on his feet, deciding not to use my things without asking. “Sit. The rice has fifteen minutes. You can be horizontal in fifteen minutes. First you’re going to put something warm in you, sitting down.”

“I came in here to fix your pot.”

“You fixed my pot. Now you’re going to let somebody hand you a hot drink and not turn it into a transaction.” My own words, handed back, dry. He set it in front of the chair — barley tea, the cheap bag, because that’s what there was — and then he did the thing that undid me, which was that he didn’t hover for the thank-you. He turned back to the counter and gave me the one mercy nobody ever thinks to give me: he stopped watching while I failed to know what to do with being given to.

I sat. I wrapped both hands around a mug I didn’t fill, in a kitchen I didn’t cook in, and the warmth went up into my palms, and I could not for the life of me find the right size for the thing I was meant to say. Thank you is too small and anything larger costs more than I keep on hand at seven in the morning. So I said nothing, and drank it, and he let me say nothing, which was somehow the most that has been handed to me in longer than I’ll count.

He didn’t sit. He stayed at the counter, leaving me the room, and after a while he said it lightly, to the window, not to me. “Nine days to what.”

I didn’t remember telling him nine days. I must have, somewhere in the rice. “An exam.”

“For.”

“A licence.” When he only waited, I gave him one more coin, because the tea had made me stupid. “Accounting. After it I can sit behind a desk, in a room with a door that locks, and nobody gets to tell me to move along.”

“That’s the whole of it. A door that locks.”

“That’s the whole of it.” I heard how small it sounded out loud and didn’t take it back. “Some people want yachts. I want a door.”

He was quiet a moment. “That’s the least greedy thing I’ve ever heard a person ask for,” he said, “and it sounds like the hardest.” And he didn’t push it — didn’t dress it up, didn’t tell me I deserved more — which was the only answer I could not have sat through.

“What about you,” I said, before I’d cleared it with myself. “What do you want. Since we’re trading.”

The pause ran a fraction too long. “A quiet two months,” he said. “Somewhere nobody’s keeping score.”

“That’s not a want. That’s a hide.”

“…Maybe down here they’re the same thing.”

I let that one go past me, because I of all people am in no position to argue it.

The guard loosened a notch — the traitor — and I heard myself, to his back: “You can’t cook. You can’t shop. You won’t go out, and when you do it’s a cap pulled down and a mask, at night, like you’re ducking somebody. You bought a rice cooker rather than walk forty steps. A week ago you wore a watch worth more than I earn in a year and now it’s gone, and you’re buying plates by the two-pack, and you dyed your own hair black in my bathroom in the dark.” A sip. “None of it’s a person. It’s a costume somebody’s still sewing, and badly.”

His shoulders moved, not quite a laugh. “Is that a question.”

“It’s a column. The total won’t come out.”

“Maybe you carried a one wrong somewhere.”

“I don’t carry ones wrong.” It was out before I could stop it, and it’s true — it’s the truest thing about me — and he turned at it and looked at me, and there it was, the thing his face does once in a while: the grey of his eyes going level and quiet and all the way open, no shutter on it at all, for half a second.

The look went into me like a key turning in a lock I never knew was set, and my chest forgot its count entirely. The body I could explain away; bodies are animals, I’ve read the chapter. It was the eyes I couldn’t get a number around — and a thing I can’t get a number around is the only kind of thing that has ever frightened me.

“No,” he said, quiet. “I don’t think you do.” A breath, and then, lighter: “Maybe I’m not a column. Maybe I’m just a man who’s bad at this and worse at saying why.” Which was somehow both completely true and not an answer to a single thing I’d asked.

And he didn’t explain the watch, or the rice, or the song, or any of the rest of it. He held the look one moment longer than was safe for either of us, then turned back to the cooker and left me in possession of my tea and all my questions both.

-----

The cooker played its small finished tune. The rice, this time, was rice.

I got up before I could be in the room for the end of whatever that had been. At the door of my room I stopped, because it had to be said and I hadn’t the strength left to dress it up. “First knuckle. Every time. You won’t burn it again.”

“First knuckle,” he repeated, like a man learning a prayer he intends to keep. “Thank you, Jae-wu.”

The given name, soft, the way he always lays it down — not Ms. Yun, never that, straight past the surname to the bare name itself, as if it were a thing he’d been keeping in a pocket a while. It did to the back of my neck what it did the first night, and I still hadn’t worked out what the thing was, only that it wasn’t unwelcome, which is worse.

I bolted the door — cheap brass, the one improvement I ever made to a place that was never mine. On the far side of it the cooker was quiet and a man who can’t make rice ate the rice I made him, humming, low, the song I’ve never heard and couldn’t stop almost remembering.

I lay down with the grey light coming up at the window and the smell of rice that didn’t burn this time threading under the door. The exam was nine days out. The man was a column that wouldn’t total. The market was Thursday.

I did not count myself to sleep.

I was gone before I could notice I hadn’t.
