# Chapter Eighteen

### Rule Eighteen: A Countdown Is Just a Clock That Hates You

Three days to the fourteenth, and the machine had stopped pretending to ask.

It used to ask. There was a whole performance of asking, back when I was younger and they still needed me to feel like a person who said yes — a call framed as a question, a schedule framed as an invitation, my mother’s office wording an order so it landed like a favour. The closer the date came, the less anyone bothered. The car was just outside now. The fitting was just confirmed. The seating was just finalised, the statement just drafted, the silver just booked for the morning of, and every just was a door already shut that I was being walked through for the look of the thing.

I gave the yes. I always give the yes. I gave it to my mother’s assistant over the phone at eight — yes, I’ll be silver; yes, I’ll be there at seven; yes, the midnight suit — gave it the way you hand over a coat at a door, automatic, a thing you’ll get back at the end of the night except you never do. I signed the merger paperwork Hyun slid in front of me in the back of the car, three places, my own name in my own hand making real a thing I’d had no hand in, and the pen worked fine, and my fingers were going four-and-four against my thigh the whole time, and nobody counts that.

Here’s the new thing, the dangerous thing, the thing three years of yes hadn’t prepared me for. There was now, in my life, a single object that made me want to say no.

Not a grand no. Not a stage-no, a press-no, a no with a lawyer behind it. A small flat no, the size of a box room over a dead laundromat, the size of a woman who fixes a wobble in a table without being asked and then won’t be thanked for it. Every yes I signed that week, some new muscle in me set its feet and pulled the other way, and the pulling had her shape, and I had no idea what to do with a muscle I’d never been allowed to grow.

The song knew before I did. It always does. It sat where it’s sat for three years — nearly the whole of it finished, clean, the verses and the turn and the chorus opening its hand — and then the outro, the last stretch home, just gone. White space where the ending lives. I’ve written endings for other people’s songs in an afternoon. My own won’t come, because the end of this one isn’t a note. It’s a person, asleep three days’ walk and four flights from here, and I cannot write her down without writing her in, and writing her in is the one yes the machine would tear up everything to stop.

So the song stayed where it stays, a step short of its own end. And the countdown ran. And a countdown, it turns out, is just a clock that hates you — same hands, same numbers, except every tick is a thing being taken instead of a thing arriving.

-----

Hyun picked me up himself, because he still won’t let anyone else know the road that ends at my door, and he looked like a man who’d been awake since a yesterday that hadn’t properly ended.

“You eat?” I asked him.

He glanced over, thrown, because that’s my line turned around — it’s his line, it’s been his line for fifteen years, the only way he knows how to say I’m frightened for you is to ask whether there’s rice in you. Hearing it come back at him visibly cost him something.

“I’m fine,” he said, to the windscreen.

“That’s not what I asked. You’ve got the thing your face does. The merger thing.” I’d watched that face manage my entire life; I can read its weather better than my own. “You’re running my hideout, my schedule, the takedown on whatever that account’s posting now, and a wedding. That’s four jobs and you’re one tired man.”

“It’s three jobs. The wedding runs itself.” He signaled, changed lanes, the cautious ten-and-two. “Mostly the bride’s side. Her people, her dress, her — complications.” A pause he tried to make sound like traffic. “She’s got a situation. The Seo girl. A boyfriend who isn’t taking the announcement like a gentleman. He’s the one writing things down. We think.” Another pause. “I’ve been on the phone to her about it more than I’ve slept.”

“To Yuna.”

“To Miss Seo,” he corrected, too fast, and I let it lie there, because too fast is the most honest thing Hyun had said all morning and we both heard it.

I looked out the window and didn’t push. But I filed it — the way she files things, I’ve started filing things — because I’ve spent my whole life watching this man pour himself out for other people’s weddings, other people’s schedules, other people’s lives, the never-debuted one, the one the company used up behind the scenes and never once put on a stage, never once picked, and the idea that some piece of him had gone and gotten attached to the woman he was professionally obligated to hand to me on the fourteenth was the kind of disaster that, three years ago, I’d have managed for him without blinking.

I’m not three years ago anymore. I know now exactly what it is to love someone the machine has assigned to someone else. I wasn’t going to manage it. I was going to watch, and ache for him, and say nothing, which is the only kindness I had on hand.

“Eat something today,” I said. “That’s not a manager talking.”

“I know what it is.” His jaw did a thing. “Eat something yourself. You’ve lost a hole in your belt and Ahn told your mother’s office and your mother’s office told me.”

“It’s handled.”

“You sound like the bride,” he said, and almost smiled, and didn’t, and drove.

-----

The label wanted me at noon, which is how I ended up in a glass room twelve floors up, caught in the exact center of the first real crack.

Noh runs the label the way weather runs a coast — you don’t argue with him, you build for him. He had the numbers up on the screen and the comeback in his mouth and the particular warmth of a man about to take something while smiling. “Spring’s soft,” he said. “We slipped it once. We slip it again, we look indecisive, and indecisive is death. So.” A click. “We bring it forward. Studio next week. Your voice is rested, the doctor cleared you—”

“The doctor cleared me for rest,” I said.

“—and a comeback the same season as a society wedding is a gift, Do-yun. The story writes itself. The heir settles down, the idol comes back, the brand goes vertical.” He spread his hands. “Everybody eats.”

And there it was, in the room with me: the two machines that own me, wanting opposite things out loud for the first time. The label wants me working — on stage, on the chart, on the campaign, a product that performs. My mother wants me finished — silvered, married, retired into the merger, a man who stops being a singer and becomes a holding company with a face. Noh wants the comeback. My mother wants the comeback cancelled, because a husband of a merged dynasty does not sweat under stage lights for screaming teenagers; it’s beneath the wall she’s building. They’ve been able to want both as long as I just kept saying yes to whoever was in the room. Three days from the fourteenth, they couldn’t both have me anymore.

I sat in the crossfire and felt, under the dread, a thing I had no business feeling, which was the cold clean click of an idea.

They both want the song. Noh wants a comeback he can’t have without new material. My mother wants a clean retirement she can’t sell if the label drags me back to the stage. And the one piece of new material in existence — the only thing either machine actually needs from me that isn’t just my obedient body in a suit — was nearly whole and living in my own chest where neither of them could reach it, with its ending missing, withheld, mine.

I didn’t say it. You don’t show a card the second you see it’s a card. But I felt the weight of it settle into my hand for the first time — that the thing I’d been failing to finish for three years might be the only leverage I’d ever held over the people who’ve held all of mine — and I filed it next to the muscle that wants to say no, and I told Noh I’d think about the studio, and watched him hear think about it as yes, the way they all do.

-----

The merger logistics meeting was the last thing on the day’s sheet, and it was where I saw it.

A conference room, the families’ lawyers, a planner with a binder thick as a hymnal, and Yuna across the table in armour that costs more than my flat’s whole building — glossy, perfect, bored to the bone. She caught my eye when I came in and did the small dry thing with her mouth that means welcome to the cell, and I sat, and the binder opened, and the machine began deciding where we’d stand and when we’d smile and how long we’d be permitted to touch for the photographers.

Hyun ran it. Of course Hyun ran it. He stood at the head of the table with his tablet and his exhausted competence and walked everyone through the choreography of a wedding that gives the woman across from me to a man she’s met twice, and I watched him do the most ordinary professional thing — slide a water glass two inches toward Yuna without breaking his sentence, because she’d reached for one that wasn’t there — and I watched her notice.

That was all it was. A glass moved. A man too tired and too decent to do otherwise, attending to the person nearest him because attending is the only language he has left. And Yuna looked at the glass, and looked at him, and something went across her armoured face that I had not once seen the office’s photographs catch — not the boredom, not the gallows humour, but the look of a person being noticed by someone who isn’t being paid to want anything from her, which I happen to be an expert in, because it’s the exact look I must wear around a certain accountant, the one I keep trying to keep off my face in my mother’s weather.

Hyun didn’t see it. He was three sentences down the binder. He never sees it; that’s the tragedy of him, the man who attends to everyone and has never once been the person someone moved a glass for. And Yuna picked up the water and drank it and put the boredom back on like a coat, and I was the only one in the room who’d watched two exhausted, never-chosen people see each other across a binder full of someone else’s choreography.

“Your friend with the pen,” I said to her, low, while the lawyers argued a timeline. “The loud one. Hyun says he’s not settling down about it.”

“Min-gyu.” She turned her cup a quarter-turn, a gesture I was starting to recognise as hers the way I recognise mine. “He’s decided the wedding’s a thing that can be stopped if he just makes enough noise. He’s wrong, but he’s loud, and loud finds keyholes.” A dry breath. “He photographed a room he shouldn’t have known about. He’ll do worse before the fourteenth, because men like Min-gyu mistake I won’t let you go for love, when it’s just a hand that won’t open.” She looked at me. “Keep your own house quiet, idol. Whatever’s in it. He’s the kind who pulls threads, and the machine that comes to cut him will pull every thread near us looking for the loose one.”

Every thread near us. A flat in Mapo. A bolt on a bedroom door. A woman three days from the only thing she’s ever wanted, who has no idea she’s a thread at all.

“I’ll keep it quiet,” I said.

“Mm.” She let it stand, the way she does, the way Tae does, the way the one woman who matters does. “Some weddings are mergers,” she said, almost to herself, the planner’s binder turning under her hand, “and some mergers are funerals. He’s not wrong about that part. He’s just wrong about whose.”

-----

Hyun drove me home in the dark, two blocks short the way we do, and didn’t say much, and I didn’t make him.

I climbed the four flights with three days left and the whole machine tightening behind me like a fist, and on the other side of the wall there was the small live silence of a person awake — the dry fast turn of pages, the kettle’s climb, a woman cramming a lifetime of figures into the last hours before the door she’s built her whole life toward.

She wasn’t turning pages anymore. She’d gone quiet in the particular way she goes quiet over the books when a column finally comes out clean — and then, low, not meant for anyone, she ran a figure to herself and turned a page in the back of the ledger, and I knew which page. I’ve seen the back of that ledger. Pressed flat in it, smoothed at the creases, are the napkins I throw away — the bars I scrawl and ball up and leave on the table, that should go in the bin and instead go, somehow, into the back of an accountant’s book between the receipts, kept, by a woman who keeps nothing she can’t account for and has never once told me she does it.

There’s no dignified word for what that did to me. I’m going to use the undignified one. A grown man, a professional charmer, a face on the side of buildings — undone into an actual blush by a woman ten feet away keeping a ledger she’s got me in. My collar went tight. My face went hot in a cold flat. I needed the night on it.

So I opened the window.

Not to be quiet. Let me kill that idea before it starts, because it would be a lie and the wrong kind: I did not open the window to shock myself silent or to keep from being heard. I opened it because I was too warm from the inside and the only cold I could reach was out there. The night came in off the rooftops, February and indifferent, and I stood in it and barely felt it, because the heat I was trying to lose wasn’t coming off the radiator.

And I didn’t care who heard. That’s the part I want down plainly. She’s never asleep at this hour — years of shifts that let out at four in the morning have wrecked her into a night creature, and near-midnight is just the broad middle of her day; I knew she was awake the way I always know, by the live quality of the quiet. I knew, and I didn’t lower my voice. I have never once in my life wanted quiet. Quiet is the thing they put me in. Let her hear. Let the whole cold indifferent city hear, all twelve million of them asleep on it.

I picked up the guitar — the dented one off the pawn rack down the road, bought cash that week for the price of a coat, the only thing in the flat that’s mine and not the machine’s — and I started to play. Just to hold time, at first; my thumb on the low string, marking the four, the count that lives in me where other people keep a pulse. And then the rest came up under it the way it always comes up when she’s near, and for once I didn’t fight where it was going.

Here’s what nobody understands about a man who sings all day. The sound isn’t the secret. I’m wallpaper — I hum over sinks, I harmonise with refrigerators in my sleep, Sun has it on video; the people around me tuned me out years ago, the way you tune out a fridge. Being heard costs me nothing. What costs is this: there’s one song I write and then strip of its words and bury at the bottom of a coat, because it’s the only true thing I make and the truth has her fingerprints all over it. I keep the music and I kill the meaning. Every time. For three years.

Tonight I didn’t kill it. I let the words stay in. Out loud, on the open string, into the dark — came in from the rain with my hands full of nothing — the whole aching unhidden length of it, meaning and all, sung straight out a fourth-floor window to a city that didn’t ask and a woman who did nothing but keep my garbage in a sacred book and never mention it. I sang her the thing I’ve never let a soul hear with its heart showing, and the maddening part, the part that put the crack in my voice on the last chorus, was that the one person it was for was ten feet away through a paper wall, wide awake, and I wasn’t allowed to go in and tell her she’s it. So I told the city instead. The city can hold a secret. The city doesn’t have her phone number either.

It ran all the way up to the place it always dies — and the light had a — and stopped, the way it stops, one note short, the ending still hers and not mine to take even now. My hands gave out before the song did anyway; they do, when it’s real, when you’ve stopped performing and the strings stop being kind and your fingers stop being yours. I set the guitar down. I shut the window.

On the other side of the wall: the careful quiet of someone on the far side of a thing they weren’t supposed to hear. Not asleep. Still. Listening to a stranger’s roommate sing the inside of his own chest at the cold, and not knowing — neither of us knowing — what any of it was about to cost.

I lay down in the dark having let the truest thing out, openly, where it could be taken, and didn’t take it back, and didn’t make myself fix it. For the first time the books came out wrong in the machine’s favour and right in mine, and I let them. I didn’t know yet that the night had a witness. I didn’t know that across the cold gap a phone had been up in a dark window the whole time, that the indifferent city had, for once, been paying attention. I went to sleep three days out, lighter than I’d been in years, the most reckless thing I have ever done already done and out of my hands — believing, the way she was believing four feet away, that the worst thing coming was only the fourteenth.
