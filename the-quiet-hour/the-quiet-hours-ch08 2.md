Rule Eight: Next One’s Yours

Do-yun (“Doha”)


The phone had been dead in my hand all night, and the room had been the better for it.

I lay on the mattress on the floor of the bare room with the dead radiator and watched the window go from black to the colour of dishwater, and for the length of that greying I was no one’s. No car was coming. No desk was waiting on a yes. The flat held last night’s cold the way it always does by morning — she works the dark and the ondol stays off and the place keeps the chill like a held breath — and through the wall, the bad plaster she’d priced me through that first night, there was nothing yet but the small machinery of a person asleep. A turn. A sigh. The radiator over there knocking once and going quiet.

Six feet. Maybe five. I’d measured it a hundred times. The number never gets bigger.

I turned the phone on because a man can’t stay dead to the world forever, and because the not-knowing has its own teeth — and the screen came up white and then the machine found me again all at once, the way water finds a hole in a boat.

It came in a stack I could feel in my teeth. Noh’s office, twice. The Cartier desk. The schedule, the car I never got into, a confirm-receipt that went unconfirmed and bred three more. My mother’s assistant, a seating chart, the word fourteenth sitting in the preview like a stone in a shoe. And under all of it, last and first and the only one that landed anywhere soft: Hyun. Timestamps walking down the night like footprints. You good. Car’s been an hour. Doha. Pick up. Then, near three in the morning, just — Have you eaten.

I sat with my back against the cold wall and the dead weight of fifteen years in my palm and did the triage I’ve always done, because the alternative is the edges going soft, and I would not do that here, not in the one room that has never asked me for anything. To Noh: resting the voice, status Friday, as agreed. A lie with a true face on it. To the Cartier desk: confirm. To the schedule: nothing; let them read the silence as sleep. To my mother’s office I didn’t reply at all, which is its own kind of reply and the only one I had the stomach for before food.

To Hyun I wrote: I’m fine. Phone died. Ate. Two true things and one clean lie, and I looked at the lie a moment before I sent it, because of the three people in this city who have ever once wanted me fed for the sake of my being fed, Hyun is two of them, and the woman behind the wall is the third and doesn’t know it.

The reply came back before the screen dimmed. Liar. There’s tubs in the dorm fridge with your name. Reo labelled them. He’s furious you’re not here to be furious at.

Then the group thread woke up, because Hyun had told them I was alive, and for a minute the machine stopped being the machine and became the five of us, which is a different thing entirely.

I sat in the cold with my thumb over the keys and the thing in my chest the lenses have never once caught loosened by a notch. Fifteen years. I didn’t write back the truth — that I was in a fourth-floor walk-up with a dead radiator and a stranger’s kettle for company, and that I was, for reasons I would not examine before breakfast, happier than the comeback has ever once made me. I wrote: eating the frog. tell Reo his tubs are safe from me, I respect the labelling system. And Sun sent back a wall of hearts, and Yejun sent a single full stop that means we were scared in the private grammar of people who’ve been hungry in the same room, and Tae sent nothing, which means he understood the part that wasn’t typed.

Be silver by the fourteenth. The stone in the shoe, still there. I put the phone face-down on the floor and let it be the thing it can’t be again for at least another hour.

-----

On the other side of the wall, her morning came to me by ear.

The bolt went back, the brass one, the cheap one, the only lock in the flat that answers to a single person. Water in the kitchen, the tap coughing rust before it ran clean. The kettle’s slow climb. A chair. The fridge opening on its complaining hinge and shutting on near-nothing — I’ve heard that fridge enough nights now to know the difference between the sound of a full one and the sound of hers, and hers is the sound of a woman counting what she can and can’t spend. Then the dry rattle of canvas bags shaken out, and I was up off the mattress before I’d decided to be, because some part of me knew that sound and went toward it: someone getting ready for market.

I stood in my doorway in yesterday’s shirt and watched her move through the grey front room, and the camera in me that never quite shuts off settled on her and stayed.

She was dressed for work that isn’t the kind I know — a coat gone soft at the cuffs, a list in her fist she didn’t look at because it was already in her head, the bags over one shoulder. She moved through the cramped room without touching anything, the way a person moves through a space they’ve measured to the centimetre, and she was so completely the owner of her own morning that I felt, standing there, like weather that had blown in through a window she forgot to shut.

“Market,” she said. Not to me exactly. To the room, the way she announces the few things she announces, so the fact exists and no one has to discuss it.

Ask her. The want came up in me fast and ridiculous and enormous, the want of a man who has been driven to a hundred countries and never once chosen the errand. “Can I come.”

It was not smooth. None of it is ever smooth with her; the smooth is for cameras and she gives me nothing to be smooth at. She stopped with one bag half on her shoulder and looked at me with the flat green-going-gold assessment she’d turn on a column that wouldn’t total, and for a second I was sure the answer was the small no I’d earned by being a thing that turns up in her flat and eats her rice and won’t explain the watch.

“It’s loud,” she said. “And cold. And I don’t wait for people.”

Which is not no.

“I’m not slow,” I said.

“You’re soft.” She said it like a line item, no cruelty in it, pulling the second bag up. “City-soft. You’ll want to leave after one row.” Already at the genkan, already stepping down into her shoes, toes squaring to the baseboard out of a habit so deep it isn’t a thought. “Cap,” she said, not turning around. “It’s bright.”

And that was the yes — sideways, unfinished, handed over with her back to me so neither of us had to hold it. I have had contracts signed with more ceremony and meant less.

-----

The market is six streets of awning and steam, and it nearly undid me on the first breath.

It’s the sensory opposite of everywhere I’m normally kept. No one had cleared it, dressed it, lit it, walked it ahead of me to make it safe. It was fish on ice and the orange roar of a heat lamp and a man shouting the price of mandarins like a verdict, old women three deep at a stall where something was frying, wet concrete underfoot, the awnings dripping the last of the night’s rain in cold strings down the back of my collar. I kept the cap low and my chin down and my shoulders in, the geometry of a man trying to be a coat-rack, and the panic I’d carried over here on a dead phone two nights before tried, for one bad second, to come up again — too many faces, no exit logged, no one between me and the crowd.

Then she took the first stall and I forgot to be afraid, because I was too busy watching her be good at this.

She’s known there. That’s the thing that went into me sideways and lodged. The vegetable man saw her coming and had the spring greens turned face-out before she’d stopped walking; she lifted a bundle, turned it to the light the way Ahn turns a shirt, found the wilt I couldn’t see, set it down without a word, and the man laughed and brought the better box up from under the counter because she’d caught him at it before and they both knew the choreography. She didn’t haggle the way I’d imagined haggling, loud and bruising. She did it in numbers, low and final, a figure stated once like a door closing, and the man countered and she let a silence sit that I would pay good money to learn, and the price came down to hers. Money changed hands — her own won, counted out of a worn envelope, every note accounted for before it left her fingers — the greens went into the bag, and she was already three steps to the next stall, and the whole of it had taken ninety seconds, and she had a place there. A standing. A small dense web of people who know her face and her eye and the exact weight of her word.

She has somewhere she belongs. I followed her down the row carrying the bag she’d wordlessly handed me, and the thought sat in me strange and aching, because I’ve been wanted by entire stadiums and never once belonged to a single street.

“You’re staring,” she said, not looking back, picking through a crate of mandarins.

“You know everyone.”

“I owe everyone.” Dry, but not quite a joke; the truth wearing a joke’s coat, the way her truths travel. “Different thing.” She found three mandarins that passed whatever test mandarins must pass and added them to my bag. Then, lower, the closest she’d come to telling me anything: “You go to the same six stalls for three years, they stop treating you like a mark. It’s not friendship. It’s just — they’ve decided you’re real.” A shrug, small. “Took a while.”

They’ve decided you’re real. I filed it next to the other things of hers I keep and don’t show her I’m keeping.

-----

It nearly came apart at the fish stalls.

A girl — sixteen, maybe, in a school coat, phone already half up out of pure teenage reflex — clocked me. I felt it before I saw it, the particular heat of a look that has stopped being a glance, the recalculation behind it. No. Not here. Not with her three feet away and her name not even something I’m allowed to know yet. The dark closed a half-inch at the edges. My free hand found the rhythm without me, four-and-four against my thigh, the count Ahn won’t take from me, the only handrail I travel with.

And the woman beside me — who doesn’t know what I am, who priced me at a hot can and a bad home-dye and a watch I can’t be bothered to wear — read the freeze in me without reading the reason, and did the most ordinary thing in the world.

“Doha.” The plain name, flat and bored, the name that is none of my names, and she shoved a second bag against my chest so I had to take it. “Hold the radishes. You’re in the man’s way.” And she stepped me sideways with one unbothered shoulder into the press of halmeonis at the next stall, into the steam and the noise and the absolute armour of the mundane — a tall quiet man carrying an old woman’s worth of vegetables for a sharp-eyed girl in a worn coat — and the schoolgirl’s certainty wavered, because that is not what the most-looked-at face in the country does on a wet Thursday, and the phone went back down, and the moment closed over like water, and I was no one again. Saved, the way I’m only ever saved lately, by being allowed to look like a person.

She didn’t ask what that was. I watched her file it — one more entry in the long ledger she’s keeping on me, the watch and the rice and now the freeze at the fish stall — and she said nothing, and the not-asking was the kindest thing anyone had done for me in a year.

My pulse came down. The count in my fingers slowed, four and four and then nothing, still, the way it goes still only ever in one woman’s weather. And underneath it, arriving the way it only arrives when I’m pointed at her — two bars of the rain-song I’ve been failing to finish for three years, slotting into the noise of the market like they were always the missing end of it.

-----

At the last stall she stopped in front of the cabbages, and I watched her run a sum she didn’t want me to see.

It was quick and quiet and brutal: a hand on a good head of cabbage, a glance at the envelope, the head set back down, a smaller one chosen. The exam nine days out, the shifts what they are, the won counted — she was putting back the cabbage she wanted for the cabbage she could carry, and she did it without a flicker, because it’s simply the shape of her days and she would die before she let it look like anything.

I had the better head in my hand and on the vendor’s scale before the want had finished forming, and I caught myself — I caught myself, which two months ago I would not have — and didn’t reach for the fold of cash that fixes everything, because I’d learned in her kitchen what that costs, learned it with the word thank you like a foreign coin in my mouth. So instead I said it plainly, the way she taught me without ever meaning to teach me anything:

“Let me get this one. You carried the bags last week.” It was a lie — there were no bags last week, there was no last week, we’d known each other a handful of days — but it was the right shape of lie, the shape that isn’t charity, the shape of two people keeping a column even between them. Not a transaction. A turn. “Next one’s yours.”

She looked at the cabbage. She looked at me. I watched her run it for the trap, the angle, the thing I wanted back, the way she runs everything, because the world has only ever handed her things in the dark and then named the price after. And she found — I watched her find it — nothing. No price. Just a man who can’t make rice buying a cabbage and getting the grammar almost right.

“…Fine,” she said, like it had been wrung out of her. “Next one’s mine. And you carry that, too.”

“I’m carrying everything,” I said.

“You’re soft,” she said. “It’s good for you.” And there it was, for half a second, before she caught it and put it away — not a smile, she doesn’t spend those, but the place a smile would go, a warmth at the eyes she’d deny under oath. It was worth more than the wall of hearts and the labelled tubs and every stadium that ever screamed a name that wasn’t even mine. I’d done nothing to earn it but get the cabbage right.

-----

We walked back the long way without deciding to, the bags cutting red lines into my fingers, the rain holding off, the market falling away behind us into its noise.

She didn’t talk and I didn’t make her, and the quiet between us was the good kind, the kind that has weight and doesn’t ask to be filled — the quiet I’ve spent fifteen years being too valuable to be allowed. A bus sighed past. A shutter went up somewhere. The song kept arriving, two bars and then two more, the most it has given me in a year, and I understood with a clarity that frightened me that it’s hers, that it has always been hers, that it stops the moment she does, and I have no way to tell her and no right to and would not dare.

In my pocket the phone was on again, and the fourteenth was in it, and the silver, and the seating chart, and the car that would come and the yes I’d hand them, because my yes is the only word that ends the call. I had perhaps eleven days of being a man who carries cabbages up four flights for a girl who decided the vendors were real before she decided I was. I knew the number. I’ve always been good with the clock that counts down to the end of a thing.

Eleven days. Careful. I was not careful. I haven’t been careful since a hot can crossed a counter in the rain three years ago and a stranger waved my coins away and wanted nothing, and some animal part of me has been walking toward her ever since with no way to call ahead.

At the foot of the stairs she took her own bags off my arm, evening the load without comment, because she will not be carried for, either. Four flights, no lift. She went up ahead of me, sure-footed, counting the steps she’s stopped needing to count, and I followed her into the cold flat that is the only warm thing I own — and through the thin wall, later, when she thought I was asleep, I heard her put the good cabbage away, and hum. Just once. Half a bar of nothing, a tune she doesn’t know she knows.

It’s my song. The end of it. The part I’ve been missing for three years, coming back to me through five feet of bad plaster in the voice of a woman who has no idea she’s the one carrying it.

I got it down before it went — a market receipt from my pocket, flattened on the floorboards by the wall, the pencil moving in the dark by feel: the half-bar she hummed, and under it the line that comes with it, the truest one yet and the most dangerous. A man, where the rest of them saw a face. I looked at the words a moment in the grey light. Then I tore the receipt in two, the way I’ve taught myself to — the bar of music folded into the inside pocket with the others, kept; the strip with the words torn small and pushed down to the bottom of my own coat where even I won’t easily find it again, because the notes are a musician’s mess and the words are a confession, and only one of those can be left where the world might look.

I keep the music. I always keep the music.

I lay in the dark and didn’t write the rest. Some things you hold in two cupped hands and don’t dare close your fingers on.

Careful, I told myself, one more time, knowing I wouldn’t be.

The radiator knocked twice and went still. The market’s tomorrow was already somebody’s problem. The fourteenth was eleven days out.

I slept better than the comeback has ever let me, and in the morning there was rice, made right, first knuckle, waiting on the one shelf we share.
