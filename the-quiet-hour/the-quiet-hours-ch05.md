# Chapter Five

### Rule Five: Don’t Accept Anything You Can’t Account For

I woke to water behind the wall, and a voice under the water.

The shower, running in the next room. And under it a man singing — low, half to himself, not meant to carry, not meant for anyone. A good voice. Too good for a box-room sublet, which I’d like entered as a discrepancy and not a compliment. It went down into the last of my sleep and found some old place in me with no name on it and no date — a place shaped like being very small, and warm, and held — and I came up to catch it and it was already gone. The water cut off. The wall went back to being a wall.

I lay there for a second, annoyed at a wall.

Then the coffee found me.

Good coffee, the expensive kind, coming up through the flat from my own kitchen, made by a pair of hands that were not mine. And for one unguarded moment it was the kindest thing I’d woken to in longer than I can tell you — somebody up before me, somebody who’d thought to put the pot on. The year Han-byeol and I split the rent I never once woke to that. Han-byeol’s mornings were a slammed door and a sprinted bus and nothing meant for anyone.

Then I remembered, and the warmth closed over like water over a dropped coin.

There was a man in my flat. I’d put him there myself, my own thumb on a lease.

I’d been up most of the night listening to him not be a problem, which is a more tiring activity than it sounds. Eleven-to-seven is the shift my body runs on, and there was no shift anymore to spend it on — only the safe side of my own bolt and a stranger being painstakingly quiet in the dark. The flat told on him the whole night: a floorboard found and avoided, a cupboard eased shut instead of clicked, a tap turned down to a thread so the pipes wouldn’t sing. So I did the thing I do with anything that won’t sit still. I added him up.

Here is the file as it stood at two in the morning.

A man who dyes his own hair black in a stranger’s bathroom and rinses the grey down the drain. A man who takes a room sight unseen and pays two months up front before the tenant can find the nerve to ask his name. A watch left out on a table worth more than the table, the chairs, and most of what I’d cook on the stove between now and the exam. I ran the totals twice. They did not reconcile, and a thing that does not reconcile is not, to me, a mystery. It’s a liability with the paperwork missing.

By two I’d filed him, with the flat certainty of the very tired, as a spy. Witness protection at the kindest. And when I went looking for the fear that’s supposed to come with finding a spy behind your bathroom door, it came up smaller than the curiosity — which I could not afford, and could not put down. The bolt held. The rent had cleared. And a man who hushes his own feet so he won’t wake you is not, whatever else he is, building toward anything.

The scraps fit the spy theory too neatly to leave alone. There were more of them by morning — I’d found two, one half under the couch and one smoothed out of the bathroom bin, because I read everything, it’s a condition. The same five ruled lines, the marks strung along them, and a scatter of words that didn’t connect to each other. Rain. Still. Key. Code, I’d decided at half one. The private cipher of a man with something to hide — because the alternative, that a grown man covers napkins in some shorthand only he can read and then throws them in the bin, made no sense I could file.

For the record, I was wrong about what they were, and I didn’t know it, and I kept the second one anyway. Flattened inside my ledger, where I keep everything I can’t yet make balance.

What actually frightened me came after. I’d opened my book — the licence is the only road I’ve got, and the exam is the gate on the road — and I worked the problem I was behind on, and I got it wrong. Worked it again: wrong, differently. A third time. A fourth. The figures swam and would not land.

I count myself to sleep. I keep my whole life balanced to the coin. The numbers had never once, in my life, refused me. They refused me at half past two with a man asleep six feet away, and I shut the book and did not let myself look straight at why, because some accounts you don’t open at night.

My stomach ended the inquiry — a noise long enough to carry to the next building, the body hauling me back to the only ledger that’s ever loved me. A review session at nine I could not miss, the last one before the exam the whole thing turns on. The clock had been counting down without me, the way it does. So I got up, gathered what I needed — the towel gone thin in the middle, yesterday’s clothes off the chair, the cheap shampoo — and opened my door into the rest of my own flat like a woman entering a room she’s not sure she still lives in.

-----

The bathroom was still breathing his shower. Door ajar, the small room thick with steam, the mirror blind with it, the warm wet smell of him filling the space the way it had filled the night — cedar and something green I didn’t have a name for and resented having noticed. Ours now. Shared. The water barely cooled, the pipe still ticking down from the heat of it.

Then he was in the kitchen, and the bathroom stopped mattering.

He was dressed. Soft dark clothes a man might’ve pulled off the floor, except nothing on him had ever come off a floor in its life. The trousers were worn at the knee on purpose. The shirt sat the way cloth only sits when it’s been cut to one body and told to forget there are others. The shoes were soft pale leather with a name on them I half-knew and couldn’t summon, off a rack I will never in this life stand at — my own clothes come off a thrift rail, sold by the kilo. The distance between those two facts is the whole of what wouldn’t add up about him.

And the heat went up my neck before I’d finished not adding it, which I’d like to lodge a formal complaint about — my own body, voting without me, on the wrong side of a man I’d filed as a security risk not nine hours earlier.

He looked up.

And he smiled. Not the easy one — a real one, surprised out of him, the kind a person can’t manufacture and can’t take back, that went all the way up and lit the grey of his eyes.

And the machine in my head — the one that counts and sorts and keeps me three steps ahead of every room I’ve ever walked into — quit. Stopped dead, mid-sum.

I want to be precise about what that was, because it is not a swoon and I will not have it filed as one. It was a malfunction. The most dangerous man in any room is the one who can reach in and switch off your alarm without laying a finger on it, and I had just watched mine go dark over a cup of coffee. I added it to the column. In red.

“Oh—” He lifted the mug, caught out. My mug. The chipped one. “I owe you for these. I used your cups. I’ll pick up my own today, separate shelf, I swear.” The coffee steamed in the cracked white mug, and the smell of it was, frankly, indecent. “There’s plenty made. Help yourself, if you want it. No pressure either way.”

He said it easy. At home being looked at, needing nothing back for it, settled in his own skin the way I have never once been settled in mine. I stood in my doorway with a thin towel bundled to my chest and the word would not come. Every time he got near, the machine just stopped, and my body took the floor and started holding opinions I had never given it leave to hold.

“…Thank you,” I managed.

It came out the wrong size. Too small. The way a coin sounds when you’d meant to set down a note.

I crossed to the pot, because crossing to the pot is a task, and tasks are safe, and I set my bundle on the table to free a hand —

and the table didn’t move.

It has rocked under me every morning for a year. The back leg’s a half-inch short — a wobble I’ve killed a hundred times with a folded receipt that never holds past a week. I pressed my palm flat to it the way you press a tooth that’s stopped aching, half expecting the ache back. Solid. Somebody had shimmed the short leg in the night. Folded card, cut clean, wedged tight, level.

“Leg was short,” he said, not making anything of it, not looking up from the window. “Couldn’t sleep. Hope that’s not against a rule.”

Here is the trouble with that.

It wasn’t a man taking. It was a man giving — small, unasked, free — mending a thing I’d never once complained about, because he’d heard it wobble in the dark and decided it was his to fix. Nobody fixes what you don’t ask them to. In my experience people find the wobble and they lean on it; that’s what the wobble is for. I stood at a level table with coffee I didn’t make going warm in a hand I didn’t trust, and I could not find a thank-you that wouldn’t cost me something I wasn’t sure I had in the account. And a why — why would you do that, what do you want for it — would’ve handed him the one fact I guard hardest, which is that no one ever has.

“It’s not against a rule,” I said.

Smaller still.

He heard the size of it. And he decided, kindly, not to make me pay for him hearing it. He nodded, turned back to the window, and left me the room — which was somehow worse than if he’d pushed, because I’d have known how to win that.

-----

His phone went at half past. Not a ring — a flat institutional buzz, three times, a sound that does not expect to be ignored. Over the rim of the chipped mug I watched the change go through him in real time: the warmth folding down, the shoulders squaring, a man putting on something that wasn’t clothes.

“I have to go in.” The word did too much lifting for its size. “Work.”

“You said short stays suit you.”

“They do.” He didn’t smile. “This is the part that doesn’t.”

A cap, pulled low. A black mask of the kind half the city wears, which on him flatly refused to be ordinary. At the door he checked the hall through the gap before he opened it — a half-second sweep, left then right, the habit of a man taught to look before he steps into a corridor. And down on the street a car was already waiting. Too clean for this block. Idling. A driver in it who wasn’t killing his own time on a phone the way a man waiting for nobody important would be.

Spy, said the tired part of me, only half joking now. The column was longer this morning than it had been at two.

At the door he stopped, half turned. “Coffee’s still warm,” he said, “if you want the rest. Separate shelf.” The easy thing surfaced one last time — only for me, just for a second — before the cap and the mask took it under. And then he was gone, careful down the stairs, and the building closed over the sound of him the way water closes over anything you drop in it.

-----

The flat was mine again, and it was not the same flat.

It smelled of cedar and that green thing and good coffee. The table was level. There was a mug warm in my two hands — mine, the chipped one — that I had done absolutely nothing to earn. Down on the street the too-clean car pulled out with him folded into the back of it, and the quiet he left behind was not the quiet I’d built in the two weeks since Han-byeol’s room went dark. That quiet had been empty, and safe, and mine. This one had a shape standing in the middle of it.

I had eight minutes to make a session I could not miss, and I’d spent three of them at a level table I could not stop touching, the way you keep checking a thing to see if it’s still true.

Under all of it the voice from behind the wall hadn’t quite stopped. I’d placed the eyes the second I saw them last night — a wet night three years gone, a free can of coffee, a man going under at my counter; the universe has a long memory and, apparently, an invoicing department. The eyes I had. It was the voice I couldn’t file. It had reached somewhere the eyes never went, somewhere older than the coffee, older than anything I could account for — and a thing I can’t account for is a thing I can’t close, and I do not leave accounts open. I keep no faces. I trained that out of myself a long time ago and it’s a good system and it has never once failed me.

His wouldn’t file.

The numbers, at least, came back. Eight minutes, then seven. I worked the morning’s problem one more time on my way out the door and it came out right — the only thing all morning that did — and I told myself that meant the machine was fixed, and went to catch my bus, and did not look up at the window once, which I counted as a victory and have since reclassified.
